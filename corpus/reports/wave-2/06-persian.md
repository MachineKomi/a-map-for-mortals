# A Map for Mortals — Research Report: Zoroastrianism, Persian Wisdom Poetry, and Pre-Islamic Arabian Hikma

## TL;DR
- **The single most important finding:** The Persian–Iranian corpus does not converge on one answer to the deepest fork — **fate vs. will** — but *stages the contradiction internally*: the Zoroastrian Gathas make free personal choice (asha vs. druj, "man by man for himself," Yasna 30.2) the explicit foundation of ethics and reject predestination, while Ferdowsi's Shahnameh, FitzGerald's Khayyam, and Zuhayr's Muʿallaqa voice tragic fatalism. The book should preserve this as a live fork, not resolve it.
- **The Zoroastrian "truth (asha) above all / the Lie (druj) as the great enemy" is a genuine, ancient, independent convergence candidate** with this project's own "truth above all" rule — flag it, but keep it tagged normative/metaphysical (a theological claim), never empirical.
- **The Khayyam material is an attribution minefield:** every famous English line is Edward FitzGerald's Victorian recomposition ("very un-literal," his word: "transmogrification"), not Khayyam's; the underlying quatrains are largely pseudepigraphic. Treat FitzGerald-vs-literal as a per-quatrain attribution problem, and flag the carpe-diem cluster's convergence with Horace/Ecclesiastes/Siduri *through* the Victorian filter.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Free will is the cornerstone of Gathic ethics.** In Insler's verified 1975 translation, Yasna 30.2 commands each hearer to "Reflect with a clear mind — man by man for himself — upon the two choices of decision." Scholarship (Boyce; Encyclopaedia Iranica) treats this as an explicit *rejection* of predestination — a remarkable stance for a text of c. 1200–1000 BCE.

2. **The asha/druj (truth/lie) opposition is ethical and psychological, not merely cosmic.** Twenty-first-century scholarship increasingly reads Gathic dualism as a moral struggle within a unified creation rather than two co-eternal substances. This makes the resonance with "truth above all" real but qualifies it: it is a claim about moral order, tagged metaphysical/normative.

3. **Ferdowsi contradicts himself productively.** The Shahnameh preserves a Zoroastrian worldview (sacredness of truth, cosmic good-vs-evil) yet stages tragic fatalism (Rostam kills his son Sohrab; the tragedy is "written in the stars"). Nöldeke and Khaleghi-Motlagh note Ferdowsi's ambivalence; Dabashi reads the moral edifice as pivoting "toward a sense of reason and sanity against the cruelty of fate."

4. **FitzGerald's Rubaiyat is a collaboration across nine centuries, not a translation.** He rendered ~1/4 of attributed quatrains, fused several into single verses (A.J. Arberry showed ~40 of FitzGerald's stanzas are loose renderings of a single manuscript quatrain, others "mashed together" from two), reordered them into a life-arc, and suppressed the Sufi reading. The oldest dated source, Bodleian MS Ouseley 140 (Shiraz, 1460 CE — 312 years after Khayyam's death), holds only 158 quatrains; the Calcutta MS holds 516.

5. **Pre-Islamic Arabian hikma has a sober, minority peace-voice.** Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma (c. 520–c. 609 CE) is "the practical wisdom of a sober man of the world": his Muʿallaqa, composed after the ~40-year War of Dāhis and al-Ghabrā between the Banu ʿAbs and Banu Dhubyān, praises peacemakers who paid blood money and depicts War as a devouring monster — a dissenting voice against the era's glorified warfare.

6. **Saadi (adab angle) supplies the humanist convergence.** Bani Adam ("The children of Adam are limbs of one body") is the corpus's clearest statement of shared humanity; it is inscribed on a carpet in the UN and pairs with Zuhayr's generosity ethic.

## Details

**Scope discipline.** Rumi, Hafiz, and Al-Ghazali are held from the Near-Eastern/Abrahamic report and are not duplicated here. The misattribution quarantine for Coleman Barks' "Rumi" and Daniel Ladinsky's "Hafiz" is **extended** (see Watchlist), not repeated. Saadi is taken **only** from the practical-ethics/adab angle and every Saadi unit is flagged for merge. Attar is developed **beyond** the one unit held elsewhere (the Simorgh climax and the seven-valleys epistemics).

**Claim-type hygiene.** All Zoroastrian theological content (ZO-02, ZO-03, ZO-05) is tagged metaphysical or normative, never empirical. Literary units distinguish a character's view from any authorial endorsement (esp. FE-02 Rostam-Sohrab; NI-02 Majnun; the Khayyam voices).

**A recurring hazard.** Online Persian/Zoroastrian quotation is heavily corrupted: versified "Insler" renderings on Wikiquote do not match his actual 1975 prose; most "Shahnameh quotes" sites are loose paraphrases; "Zoroaster quotes" often trace to Victorian paraphrase without a verse number. Units below carry attribution_confidence tags accordingly, and paraphrases are explicitly marked (never dressed as verbatim quotes).

## C) CENTRAL DILEMMAS / FORKS

1. **Truth vs the Lie (asha vs druj).** The defining Zoroastrian fork (ZO-01–ZO-04): a personal, repeatable choice with cosmic stakes. Strong resonance with this project's own "truth above all" — flag as a convergence CANDIDATE, do not force: the Gathic claim is normative/metaphysical, not an empirical claim about honesty's payoffs.

2. **Fate vs Will.** The deepest internal contradiction of the Persian–Iranian corpus. Zoroastrianism (ZO-01) explicitly rejects predestination and makes free choice the foundation of ethics. Ferdowsi (FE-02) stages tragic fatalism (Rostam-Sohrab), tempered by "reason and sanity against the cruelty of fate." FitzGerald's Khayyam (KH-03) voices near-total determinism. Zuhayr (MU-02) says death finds you regardless. A genuine cross-text fork, not a consensus — preserve the dissent.

3. **Carpe diem vs Piety.** The Khayyam tension (KH-01, KH-04): present pleasure vs deferred religious reward ("take the Cash, and let the Credit go"). Converges with the existing Horace/Ecclesiastes/Siduri cluster — flag the Victorian filter (FitzGerald sharpened the hedonism, suppressed the Sufi reading).

4. **External vs Internal quest.** Attar (AT-01): the sought divine turns out to be the seeker's own self.

5. **Passion vs Prudence.** Nizami's Majnun (NI-02): all-consuming love as both self-destruction and mystical ideal.

6. **Self vs Community.** Saadi's Bani Adam (SA-01) and Zuhayr's generosity ethic (MU-03): solidarity as constitutive of being human.

7. **Word/deed integrity.** Zoroastrian humata/hukhta/hvarshta (ZO-04): alignment of thought, speech, action.

8. **Legacy vs Present-moment.** Ferdowsi's poetic immortality (FE-04) vs Khayyam's "today is all we have" (KH-04): rival responses to mortality.

## F) MISATTRIBUTION WATCHLIST

1. **FitzGerald's Khayyam is not Khayyam.** He called it "very un-literal" ("transmogrification"). He rendered ~1/4 of attributed quatrains and — as A.J. Arberry showed — some ~40 of his stanzas are loose translations of a single manuscript quatrain, while others are "mashed together" from two. He reordered them into a day/season/life arc, added his own imagery, and suppressed the Sufi dimension. Tag every famous English line (KH-01–04) attribution=dubious, credited to FitzGerald; credit the sentiment to "the Khayyam tradition." Use Avery & Heath-Stubbs (1979), Dick Davis (2010), or Juan Cole (2020) for per-quatrain literal comparison.
2. **"A Jug of Wine, a Loaf of Bread—and Thou"** exists in materially different 1st-edition ("Here with a Loaf of Bread beneath the Bough, / A Flask of Wine, a Book of Verse") and 5th-edition wordings, and was itself fused from Ouseley MS quatrains No. 149 and No. 155. Always cite the edition.
3. **Authorship of the Rubaiyat is contested.** Most quatrains are likely pseudepigraphic; "Khayyam" was a frame-author for later skeptical/libertine poets (Juan Cole). The oldest dated source (Bodleian MS Ouseley 140, Shiraz 1460 CE) postdates Khayyam by 312 years and holds only 158 quatrains. Do not assert Khayyam "wrote" any given quatrain.
4. **Coleman Barks' "Rumi" & Daniel Ladinsky's "Hafiz"** — already quarantined in the Near-Eastern report; EXTEND the quarantine here. Ladinsky's "Hafiz" poems are largely his own inventions with no Persian original.
5. **Saadi Bani Adam textual variant:** yekdīgar ("limbs of one another," in all early MSS) vs yek peykar ("one body"). The popular English "members of one frame/body" (Eastwick 1880, on the UN plaque) translates the LATER variant; the UN carpet's Persian uses yekdīgar. Note the variant; don't present one as definitively original.
6. **Ferdowsi fate quotes:** most circulating "Shahnameh quotes" online (bookey/tvtropes-type sources) are loose paraphrases. FE-02 and FE-04 wordings must be verified against Davis 2006 before quoting; FE-02 is flagged here as a paraphrase.
7. **Gathas Yasna 30.2–30.3 "Hearken with your ears…" versified renderings** attributed to Insler on Wikiquote do NOT match Insler's actual 1975 prose (a likely conflation). Use the prose wording verified in this report.
8. **"Zoroaster" popular quotes** (e.g., "Turn yourself not away from three best things…") circulate widely but usually trace to Victorian-era paraphrase (e.g., Liberty Fund/oll compilations), not a verse citation. Require a Yasna number.

## Caveats

- **Asha translation is unsettled.** Insler renders it "truth"; Bartholomae/Mills/traditional Parsi translators use "righteousness / cosmic order." The resonance with "truth above all" partly depends on this translation choice — note it explicitly rather than leaning on it.
- **Gathic dualism is contested.** Whether it is ethical/psychological (majority modern view) or ontological (older view) affects how ZO-02 should be presented. Tagged metaphysical and conditionality=contested for this reason.
- **The Rubaiyat is doubly unstable:** the Persian corpus is pseudepigraphic AND the famous English is FitzGerald's recomposition. No Khayyam quatrain should be treated as securely authorial.
- **Ferdowsi was a Muslim preserving a Zoroastrian worldview**, so Shahnameh "Zoroastrian" material is filtered through 10th-century Islamic authorship — do not treat it as a primary Zoroastrian source.
- **Character ≠ author.** The fatalism of Rostam-Sohrab, the obsession of Majnun, and the hedonism of the Khayyam voice are dramatic/poetic positions, not necessarily authorial endorsements.
- **Saadi overlaps the Near-Eastern report** — all three SA units are flagged for merge to avoid duplication.
- **One subagent-sourced item (ZO-05) remains fragmentary** and is tagged attribution_confidence=probable pending print verification of the full Insler Y31.8 text.
