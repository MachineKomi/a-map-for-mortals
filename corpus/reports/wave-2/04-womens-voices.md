# A Map for Mortals — Women's Wisdom Across Eras and Traditions

## TL;DR
- Women's wisdom converges independently across radically different traditions on a small set of recurring forks — **care vs. justice, voice vs. silence, autonomy vs. relation, and the examined life under constraint** — but the recurrence is *robustness*, not proof, and much of it survives only because men transmitted it (Diotima via Plato, Hypatia via Socrates Scholasticus, Rabia via Attar) or because women wrote inside constraints they could not name openly (Ban Zhao, Christine de Pizan, Astell).
- The highest-integrity quotable core is **verifiable and public-domain**: Enheduanna, Sappho fr. 31, Julian ("all shall be well"), Teresa ("nada te turbe"), de Pizan, Astell, Wollstonecraft, Fuller. The 20th-century ethics-of-care debate (Gilligan, Noddings, Beauvoir, Murdoch, Midgley) and most good modern translations are **in-copyright** and must be paraphrased or quoted minimally pending rights review.
- The biggest data-integrity risk for this target is **meme-corruption**: women's quotes are heavily misattributed — "well-behaved women seldom make history" is Laurel Thatcher Ulrich (1976), not Monroe; most circulating "Rabia" poems are loose modern renderings; and "mono no aware" is Motoori Norinaga's later term, not Murasaki's.

## Key Findings
1. **Cross-tradition convergence on non-instrumental devotion / unselfing.** Rabia (8th-c. Sufi), Julian (14th-c. Christian), Akka Mahadevi (12th-c. Bhakti), and Iris Murdoch (20th-c. secular Platonist) independently arrive at the claim that the highest orientation strips away reward and fear and attends to the Real/God/Good for its own sake. Strong candidate convergence — but the claim types differ (metaphysical for the mystics, normative/prudential for Murdoch), so it must not be flattened into one node.
2. **The "voiced-through-men" problem is structural, not incidental.** Diotima reaches us only through Plato's Socrates; Hypatia left no surviving writing; Rabia left nothing written (her sayings come from Attar's *Tadhkirat al-Awliya*, c. 1200, four centuries later); Sappho survives largely in fragments quoted by male critics (Longinus preserves fr. 31 in *On the Sublime*). This is the central justification for a dedicated women's-wisdom query.
3. **The ethics-of-care fork is genuinely contested, not settled.** Gilligan/Noddings articulated a "different voice" of care; critics charge essentialism and "slave morality." Marilyn Friedman ("Beyond Caring: The De-Moralization of Gender," *Canadian Journal of Philosophy* Supp. Vol. 13, 1987) argues care and justice do not in reality divide along gender lines and should be de-gendered. Encode care ethics as a *contested* fork, not a female-coded virtue.
4. **Several women wrote subversively inside constraint.** Ban Zhao's *Lessons for Women* preaches humility/submission yet argues for female education; Christine de Pizan undercuts her own catalogue with conventional wifely advice; Astell's "if all men are born free…" may be ironic Tory critique of Locke rather than a liberal manifesto. Character-view vs. author-endorsement discipline applies even to non-fiction here.

## Details

### Ancient / Medieval

**Enheduanna (c. 2285–2250 BCE), Akkad/Sumer — first named author in history.** High priestess of Nanna at Ur, daughter of Sargon. Her hymn *Nin-me-šara* ("The Exaltation of Inana," Inana B, 154 lines) narrates her expulsion from office by the usurper Lugal-Ane and her appeal to Inana for reinstatement — the first first-person authorial voice on record. Best current edition/translation: Sophus Helle, *Enheduana: The Complete Poems of the World's First Author* (Yale UP, 2023); foundational scholarly edition Hallo & van Dijk, *The Exaltation of Inanna* (Yale, 1968); German critical edition Annette Zgoll, *Der Rechtsfall der En-hedu-Ana im Lied nin me šara* (Ugarit-Verlag, 1997). CARE: authorship of the *Temple Hymns* collection is debated — Jeremy Black concluded that at most only the final hymn (42) can reasonably be claimed as her authorship/editorship. Public domain: the modern translations are in-copyright; the Sumerian text is not.

**Sappho (c. 630–570 BCE), Lesbos.** Only one complete poem survives (fr. 1, Hymn to Aphrodite, quoted by Dionysius of Halicarnassus); fr. 31 ("He seems to me equal to gods") survives because Longinus quoted it in *On the Sublime*. Canonical modern translation: Anne Carson, *If Not, Winter: Fragments of Sappho* (Knopf, 2002) — IN COPYRIGHT. Public-domain translations exist (e.g., Henry Wharton 1885). Register: poem/lyric; the wisdom unit is the phenomenology of desire ("no speaking is left in me… tongue breaks and thin fire is racing under skin," trans. Carson).

**Hypatia of Alexandria (c. 355–415 CE).** FLAG: **none of her own writings survive.** She is known to have produced a commentary on Diophantus's *Arithmetica*, a commentary on Apollonius's *Conics*, and work on Ptolemy (she edited Book III of the *Almagest*; her father Theon's inscription records "the edition having been prepared by the philosopher, my daughter Hypatia"). Transmission: the Byzantine *Suda* (from Hesychius, c. 1000) lists her works; her student Synesius of Cyrene's letters are the only contemporary first-hand source; Socrates Scholasticus (*Historia Ecclesiastica* VII.15) gives the fullest near-contemporary account, framing her murder as political (the rivalry of Bishop Cyril and the prefect Orestes), not doctrinal. Verbatim (Socrates Scholasticus, *Hist. Eccl.* VII.15, trans. A.C. Zenos, *Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers* 2nd ser. vol. 2, public domain): "There was a woman at Alexandria named Hypatia… who made such attainments in literature and science, as to far surpass all the philosophers of her own time… For all men on account of her extraordinary dignity and virtue admired her the more." CAUTION: online "Synesius letters" quotes ("You alone have the power to calm the storm of my soul…") are unverified as to translator/edition and use suspect letter-numbering; if used, cite A. Fitzgerald, *The Letters of Synesius of Cyrene* (1926). Scholarly authorities: Alan Cameron & Jacqueline Long, *Barbarians and Politics at the Court of Arcadius* (1993); Michael Deakin, *Hypatia of Alexandria* (2007).

**Diotima of Mantinea (as transmitted).** Appears only in Plato's *Symposium* 201d–212c, where Socrates relays her teaching on love — the "ladder of love" (*scala amoris*) ascending from a single beautiful body, to all beautiful bodies, to beauty of souls, to laws and knowledge, and finally to the Form of Beauty itself. FLAG: her historicity is contested. Per the standard reference summary, "Most modern scholars believe that Diotima is a fictional character invented by Plato. Marsilio Ficino, in the 15th century, was the first to suggest she might be fictional." Martha Nussbaum reads her as a literary construct (the name ≈ "honor the god," pointedly contrasting Alcibiades' "Timandra," "honor the man"); Mary Ellen Waithe (*A History of Women Philosophers*, vol. 1, 1987) and, more recently, Deretić & Smith defend possible historicity; Armand D'Angour models her on Aspasia. The paradigm case of a woman's wisdom possibly voiced *only* through a man, doubly mediated (Plato writing Socrates reporting Diotima). Claim type: metaphysical/normative.

**Ban Zhao (c. 45/49–117/120 CE), Han China.** *Nüjie* ("Lessons for Women," c. 100 CE) — the earliest Chinese work by a woman scholar for women's education; she also completed the *Han shu* after her brother Ban Gu's death. Read critically: the text preaches humility, yielding (yin), and the "Three Obediences and Four Virtues," and was used for centuries to school women in submission — yet it also argues that girls should be educated, criticizing the practice of teaching only boys. Scholarship is split: Nancy Lee Swann, *Pan Chao: Foremost Woman Scholar of China* (American Historical Association, 1932), "recognized Ban Zhao as the first thinker to formulate a single complete statement of feminine ethics"; the Cambridge *Ancient Women Philosophers* (2023) volume argues against the "simplistic anti-feminist charge" (contributor/verbatim not confirmed — flag for verification). Best edition: Swann 1932 (likely public domain or near). Claim type: normative/prudential; conditionality: contested.

**Sei Shōnagon, *The Pillow Book* (Makura no Sōshi, c. 1002), Heian Japan.** 164 "lists" and observations; the wisdom register is aesthetic discrimination (*okashi* — the charming, the delightful) as a mode of attention. Translations: Ivan Morris (1967); Meredith McKinney (Penguin, 2006, judged more accurate by Machiko Midorikawa) — BOTH IN COPYRIGHT. Verbatim ("Hateful Things," trans. Morris): a lover who leaves at dawn and fusses noisily for his fan and paper — "What charmless behaviour! 'Hateful' is an understatement." Register: list/aphorism. CAUTION: verify exact pagination against a print edition before encoding verbatim.

**Murasaki Shikibu, *The Tale of Genji* (c. 1008) & Diary.** In the "Fireflies" chapter (25), Genji delivers a defense of fiction (*monogatari*): tales begin "when all those things the teller longs to have pass on to future generations… overflow the teller's heart" (trans. Royall Tyler, Viking, 2001 — IN COPYRIGHT). CARE: "mono no aware" is NOT Murasaki's phrase — it is Motoori Norinaga's (1730–1801) later interpretive concept; Tyler notes "the expression does not occur in the tale." Distinguish character (Genji) from author. Her *Diary* (*Murasaki Shikibu Nikki*) contains the famous remark on her rival: "Sei Shōnagon… was dreadfully conceited. She thought herself so clever and littered her writings with Chinese characters; but if you examined them closely, they left a great deal to be desired." (standard scholarly translation: Richard Bowring, *Murasaki Shikibu: Her Diary and Poetic Memoirs*, Princeton, 1982). The two served rival empresses (Sei → Teishi; Murasaki → Shōshi) and likely never overlapped at court.

**Hildegard of Bingen (1098–1179).** Benedictine abbess, composer, visionary. Central concept: *viriditas* ("greenness," the divine greening life-force), contrasted with *ariditas* (dryness/aridity). In *Scivias* II.5.46, God asks: "I had a green field in my power [*Viridem agrum in potestate mea habui*]. Did I give it to you, O human, so that you would make it put forth whatever fruit you wished?" — an early ecological-theological caution against human domination of creation. Editions: *Scivias*, trans. Columba Hart & Jane Bishop (Paulist Press, 1990). Claim type: metaphysical/normative; an early ecospiritual wisdom unit.

**Julian of Norwich (1342–after 1416).** *Revelations of Divine Love* — earliest surviving English-language book by a woman. Canonical line (ch. 27): "It behoved that there should be sin; but all shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well." And on meaning (ch. 86): "Love was our Lord's meaning." Register: metaphor/parable; claim type: metaphysical (theodicy under suffering). Public-domain translation: Grace Warrack (1901); modern scholarly Barry Windeatt (OUP, 2015, in copyright).

**Teresa of Ávila (1515–1582).** *Nada te turbe* ("Let nothing disturb you… todo se pasa… solo Dios basta / God alone suffices"), found in her breviary in her own hand. Also *The Interior Castle* and *The Way of Perfection*. FLAG on authenticity: Teresian scholar Kieran Kavanaugh (ICS Publications, 1985 collected works) notes the poetry corpus has general authentication problems (lack of autographs, mixture with other Carmelites' verse); *Nada te turbe* is among the best-attested (autograph). Public domain: E. Allison Peers translation. Claim type: prudential/metaphysical — equanimity under change.

**Christine de Pizan (c. 1364–1430).** *The Book of the City of Ladies* (1405), a response to misogynist authority (Jean de Meun's *Roman de la Rose*). Lady Reason argues (Part 1, §9.3) that a person's sex is not a fair measure of character, and defends women's education: "not all opinions of men are based on reason… moral education amends and ennobles [women]." CARE: she also counsels wifely subjection — likely a strategic dilution to avoid censure or punishment; encode the tension rather than resolving it. Editions: Earl Jeffrey Richards trans. (Persea, 1982, in copyright); Rosalind Brown-Grant (Penguin). Claim type: normative/argument.

### Early-Modern / Modern

**Mary Astell (1666–1731).** *Some Reflections upon Marriage* (1706 preface): "If all men are born free, how is it that all women are born slaves?" CONTESTED reading: Springborg and Kinnaird read it as ironic Tory critique of Lockean/Whig contractarianism; Broad reads it as a genuine claim that wives are wrongfully subject to the "inconstant, uncertain, unknown, arbitrary will" of husbands. Also *A Serious Proposal to the Ladies* (1694) — proposing a women's academy. Public domain. Claim type: normative/argument; conditionality: contested.

**Mary Wollstonecraft (1759–1797).** *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792): reason as the antidote to tyranny — "I do not wish [women] to have power over men; but over themselves"; and "My own sex, I hope, will excuse me, if I treat them like rational creatures, instead of flattering their fascinating graces." Argues female "weakness" is artificial, produced by mis-education. Public domain. Claim type: empirical + normative.

**Margaret Fuller (1810–1850).** *Woman in the Nineteenth Century* (1845) — first major work of American feminism. "But if you ask me what offices [women] may fill, I reply — any. I do not care what case you put; let them be sea-captains, if you will." Public domain (Greeley & McElrath, 1845). Claim type: normative.

**Simone de Beauvoir (1908–1986).** *The Second Sex* (1949): "On ne naît pas femme: on le devient" — "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" (1953 trans. Parshley; 2010 Borde & Malovany-Chevallier: "One is not born but becomes woman"). A descriptive-phenomenological account of the social construction of "woman"; per the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, it should be read as phenomenological description, not biological essentialism. IN COPYRIGHT (quote minimally). Claim type: descriptive/observational. Treated fully here per scope (not previously held).

**Edith Stein (1891–1942).** Phenomenologist (Husserl's assistant), Carmelite, killed at Auschwitz; her dissertation *On the Problem of Empathy* (*Zum Problem der Einfühlung*, 1917) is the key early work. Sourcing thin — flagged as a lead. Claim type: metaphysical/normative.

**Iris Murdoch (1919–1999).** *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970): "unselfing" — attention to beauty (the hovering kestrel) dissolves the "fat relentless ego"; and "Love is the extremely difficult realisation that something other than oneself is real" ("The Sublime and the Good," *Chicago Review*, 1959). IN COPYRIGHT. Claim type: normative/prudential. Convergence candidate with Weil's "attention" (held by the modern-philosophy report — flag for merge; take Murdoch only for what the Weil unit omits).

**Mary Midgley (1919–2018).** Philosophy "is best understood as a form of plumbing" — invisible until the concepts go wrong, "until… the smell coming up from below is so bad that we are forced to take up the floorboards"; anti-reductionist and anti-scientism (*Beast and Man*, 1978; *Wickedness*, 1984). IN COPYRIGHT. Claim type: observational/normative.

### Across Traditions — Mystics & Poets

**Rabia al-Adawiyya (c. 717–801), Basra, Sufi.** Left no writings; sayings transmitted by hagiographers, chiefly Farid ud-Din Attar's *Tadhkirat al-Awliya* (c. 1200), four centuries after her death. Core teaching: love God for God's own sake, not from fear of Hell or hope of Paradise — the pivot from asceticism to *mahabbah* (divine love) in Sufi thought. Best-attested prayer (in many forms): "O God, if I worship You from fear of Hell, burn me in Hell; and if I worship You in hope of Paradise, exclude me from Paradise; but if I worship You for Your own sake, withhold not Your everlasting beauty." FLAG: many circulating "Rabia poems" (e.g., "In my soul there is a temple, a shrine, a mosque, a church…") are loose modern renderings (Ladinsky-style) — treat as dubious/apocryphal; tag transmission as posthumous-hagiographic and never mark verified. Claim type: metaphysical/normative.

**Akka Mahadevi (c. 1130–1160), Kannada, Lingayat/Bhakti.** Per the standard reference, "about 350 lyric poems or vachanas are attributed to Akka Mahadevi" via her *ankita* (signature) "Chennamallikarjuna"; her fuller corpus of "430 vachanas" plus the prose works *Mantrogopya* and *Yogangatrividhi* are "considered her known contributions to Kannada literature." Radical renunciation (walked out of a forced marriage, renounced clothing). "Take these husbands who die, decay — and feed them to your kitchen fires!" Best translation: A.K. Ramanujan, *Speaking of Śiva* (Penguin, 1973), who renders the ankita "Lord white as jasmine" — IN COPYRIGHT; critiqued by Tejaswini Niranjana (*Siting Translation*, 1992) as universalizing for a Western audience. A new translation by Vanamala Vishwanatha is forthcoming (Murty Classical Library). Claim type: metaphysical/normative; register: poem.

**Mirabai (c. 1498–1547), Rajasthan, Bhakti.** Krishna-devotion, defiance of marital and caste norms. Sourcing thin — flagged as a lead. Note: Indigenous women's wisdom is better handled by the parallel Indigenous query; cross-reference there.

### The Themes / Forks — Ethics of Care

**Carol Gilligan, *In a Different Voice* (1982).** Empirical-psychological claim (contra Kohlberg) that a "care" orientation — contextual, relational — appears more often in women's moral reasoning and has been undervalued next to a "justice" orientation. "While an ethic of justice proceeds from the premise of equality — that everyone should be treated the same — an ethic of care rests on the premise of nonviolence — that no one should be hurt" (ch. 6). IN COPYRIGHT. Claim type: empirical (contested).

**Nel Noddings, *Caring* (1984).** Grounds ethics in the relation between "one-caring" and "cared-for"; distinguishes natural caring ("I want") from ethical caring ("I must"). IN COPYRIGHT. Claim type: normative.

**Critics (kept honest).** Care ethics is charged with essentialism, parochialism, and functioning as a "slave morality." Marilyn Friedman, "Beyond Caring: The De-Moralization of Gender" (*Canadian Journal of Philosophy* Supp. Vol. 13, 1987, pp. 87–110; repr. in Held, ed., *Justice and Care*, Westview, 1995, pp. 61–77), argues care and justice "do not, in reality, divide along [gender] lines" and should be de-gendered. Sarah Hoagland argues Noddings's model risks an unequal giver/taker relation. Gilligan's own 1987 reply says care should be understood *within* a framework of justice. Encode as a contested fork, not a resolved consensus.

## C) Central Dilemmas / Forks Identified
1. **Care vs. Justice** — relational/contextual responsiveness vs. impartial rights and equality. Poles: Gilligan/Noddings (care) ↔ Friedman/Kantian critics (de-gender; justice frames care). *Contested; do not gender-code.*
2. **Voice vs. Silence** — to speak/write publicly at real risk vs. safety in silence or coded speech. Poles: Enheduanna, Hypatia, Fuller, Astell (voice) ↔ Ban Zhao's yielding, de Pizan's strategic dilution (constrained/coded voice).
3. **Autonomy vs. Relation** — self-possession and independence vs. constitution-through-relationship. Poles: Wollstonecraft, Beauvoir, Astell (autonomy) ↔ Noddings, the mystics' union/self-loss (relation). Murdoch bridges: autonomy *through* attending to the other's reality.
4. **Instrumental vs. Non-instrumental devotion (motive fork)** — acting for reward/fear vs. for the good/God's own sake. Poles: conventional piety ↔ Rabia, Julian, Akka Mahadevi, Murdoch's "unselfing."
5. **The examined life under constraint** — pursuing wisdom/vocation when law, marriage, or custom forbids it. Recurs in Hypatia, de Pizan, Astell, Sei Shōnagon, Akka Mahadevi.

## D) Virtues Cultivated & Dangers Guarded Against
- **Virtues:** attention/unselfing (Murdoch, Weil-adjacent, Sei Shōnagon); equanimity (Teresa); hope against despair (Julian); sincerity of motive (Rabia); reason and self-governance (Wollstonecraft, Astell); dignity in public speech (Hypatia, Fuller); reverence for the living world (Hildegard); courage to testify (Enheduanna).
- **Dangers:** self-effacement/erasure; instrumental piety (worship for reward); egoism ("the fat relentless ego"); despair under suffering; gender-essentialism (the risk *inside* care ethics); domination of nature (Hildegard); conceptual "bad plumbing"/reductionism (Midgley).

## E) Candidate Cross-Tradition Convergences & Contradictions (to test later)
**Convergences (robustness, NOT proof):**
- *Non-instrumental orientation to the ultimate*: Rabia (Sufi) ↔ Julian (Christian) ↔ Akka Mahadevi (Bhakti) ↔ Murdoch (secular Platonist). Test: are the claim types commensurable (metaphysical vs. normative)?
- *Attention/unselfing as the ground of virtue*: Murdoch ↔ Weil (held elsewhere) ↔ Sei Shōnagon's aesthetic discrimination ↔ Teresa's recollection. Flag for merge with modern-philosophy report on Weil.
- *Education as the lever of equality*: Ban Zhao (Han) ↔ de Pizan (medieval French) ↔ Astell & Wollstonecraft (English) — striking independent convergence across 1,700 years.

**Contradictions / tensions to preserve:**

- Beauvoir's constructed-"woman" vs. essentialist readings of care ethics — potential internal tension in the female-wisdom corpus itself.

## F) Misattribution Watchlist (women's quotes are heavily meme-corrupted)
- **"Well-behaved women seldom make history"** — actually **Laurel Thatcher Ulrich**, "Vertuous Women Found: New England Ministerial Literature, 1668–1735," *American Quarterly* 28, no. 1 (Spring 1976): 20 — verbatim: "Well-behaved women seldom make history; against Antinomians and witches, these pious matrons have had little chance at all." Routinely misattributed to Marilyn Monroe, Eleanor Roosevelt, Anne Boleyn; also corrupted to "rarely make history."
- **Rabia al-Adawiyya "poems"** — "In my soul there is a temple, a shrine, a mosque, a church…", "Die Before You Die", "My Greatest Need Is You" — these are loose modern (Ladinsky-style) renderings, NOT authenticated translations. Only the fear-of-Hell/hope-of-Paradise prayer is well-attested (via Attar). Tag most as dubious/apocryphal.
- **"mono no aware"** — often attributed to Murasaki; it is **Motoori Norinaga's** (1730–1801) interpretive term and "does not occur in the tale" (Tyler). 
- **Synesius-on-Hypatia lines** ("You alone have the power to calm the storm of my soul…") — unverified translator/edition and dubious letter numbers; do not encode verbatim without Fitzgerald (1926).
- **Teresa of Ávila "Christ has no body now but yours"** — a widely circulated prayer of *disputed* Teresian authorship; verify before use.
- **Beauvoir translation variance** — "One is not born but becomes *a* woman" (1953 Parshley) vs. "becomes woman" (2010) — cite the specific edition; the older Parshley translation is widely criticized for cuts.
- **Hildegard "viriditas" pop-quotes** (e.g., Kim Stanley Robinson's sci-fi "holy greening power") — modern literary uses often masquerade as Hildegard's own words.
