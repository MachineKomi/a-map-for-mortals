# A Map for Mortals — Indigenous Philosophies & Oral Traditions (Research Report)

Land is repeatedly framed across many (though not all) named Indigenous peoples as kin or ancestor to whom humans owe reciprocal responsibility — a stance now embedded in living law, as in New Zealand's Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017, s14(1): *"Te Awa Tupua is a legal person and has all the rights, powers, duties, and liabilities of a legal person."* This report maps that and adjacent forks across specific peoples, flags rampant misattribution, and treats every tradition as living, not relic.

## TL;DR
- Across the Haudenosaunee, Lakota, Diné, specific Aboriginal Australian nations, Māori, Inuit, Sámi, and Andean Quechua/Kichwa peoples, a recurring — but **not universal** — fork is **stewardship-as-relationship vs. extraction-as-ownership**: land as kin/ancestor owed reciprocity, expressed today in ceremony, governance, and statute.
- This domain is saturated with fabricated "Native American proverbs" and rewritten speeches. The flagship case, **Chief Seattle's speech**, had its most-quoted ecological lines written by screenwriter Ted Perry for the 1972 film *Home*; treat the popular text as **apocryphal**. Only the 1887 Smith transcript has (still contested, embellished) standing.
- These are **living traditions**. Indigenous scholars (Kimmerer, Kuokkanen, Oskal, Gaski, Kahn-John, Denetdale) and community/government bodies (Onondaga Nation, Government of Nunavut, Ngāi Tahu, Whanganui iwi, Sámi institutions) actively define and defend these concepts. Scholarship (Krech's *The Ecological Indian*) warns against romantic "ecological Indian" essentialism — represent **tendencies, not destinies**.

## Key Findings
1. **Land as kin/ancestor, not property** recurs independently across Haudenosaunee, Lakota (*Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ*), Māori (*whenua*; Te Awa Tupua), Aboriginal (Country), Andean (Pachamama), and Sámi (*meahcci*) traditions — a strong cross-tradition convergence, though the metaphysics and specifics differ sharply by people. (Observational/metaphysical.)
2. **Intergenerational / seven-generation responsibility** is genuinely rooted in the Haudenosaunee Great Law tradition (the "faces yet beneath the ground"), but the popular slogan "consider the seventh generation" is **not** a verbatim quote from written transcriptions of the Great Law — flag as paraphrase/tradition. (Normative.)
3. **Reciprocity** is a named, explicit ethic: Andean *ayni*, Potawatomi "Honorable Harvest" (Kimmerer), Sámi *láhi/attáldat* (Kuokkanen) and *boazolihkku* "reindeer luck" (Oskal), Lakota relationality. This is the strongest candidate convergence with the parallel African-wisdom (communitarian) query — flag for testing, do not assume identity. (Prudential/normative.)
4. **Sacred/restricted knowledge exists and must be respected**: Diné winter-only stories; Aboriginal Dreaming access restrictions. Record existence and source; do NOT extract or decontextualise. (CARE.)
5. **Dissent/nuance must be preserved**: *sumak kawsay* is contested (a 1990s systematized concept vs. ancient one); Krech contests ecological essentialism; Māori scholar Hana Tapiata contests the anthropocentric reading of "he tangata." (Contested.)

## Details

### Haudenosaunee (Six Nations / Rotinonhsión:ni / "Iroquois" Confederacy)
The **Great Law of Peace (Kaianere'kó:wa)** is an oral constitution. The seventh-generation ethic is authentically rooted in it, but the exact slogan is a modern distillation. The Parker rendering (Arthur C. Parker, *The Constitution of the Five Nations*, 1916; hosted by Portland State University) reads: *"Look and listen for the welfare of the whole people and have always in view not only the present but also the coming generations, even those whose faces are yet beneath the surface of the ground—the unborn of the future Nation."* Wikipedia's "Seven generation sustainability" entry correctly notes that the famous "in every deliberation we must consider the impact on the seventh generation" is NOT in written transcriptions; the only "seven" passage concerns the qualities of leaders ("the thickness of your skin shall be seven spans").

Chief **Oren Lyons** (Onondaga Faithkeeper) is a key living voice. In his address opening the UN International Year of the World's Indigenous People (delivered 10 December 1992, opening the 1993 year), he stated: *"When we walk upon Mother Earth we always plant our feet carefully because we know the faces of our future generations are looking up at us from beneath the ground."* He also glossed the Two Row relationship: *"Even though you and I are in different boats, you in your boat and we in our canoe, we share the same River of Life — what befalls me, befalls you."*

The **Two Row Wampum (Guswenta / Kaswentha, c.1613)** encodes a coexistence ethic — two vessels on one river: *"Neither of us will make compulsory laws or interfere in the internal affairs of the other. Neither of us will try to steer the other's vessel."* (Onondaga Nation and Oneida Indian Nation community texts.) *Scholarly caveat:* Wikipedia and historians debate the authenticity of the later **written** versions (the "Tawagonshi document"), even as the oral tradition and the belt's meaning are well attested and revered. Keep the oral tradition and the disputed written artifact distinct.

### Lakota
***Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ*** ("all my relations / we are all related") expresses the interconnection of humans, animals, land, and cosmos; it originates in Lakota prayer/song and appears in Lakota ceremonial life. Sicangu Lakota elder **Albert White Hat** cautioned (via the Tapestry Institute) that the phrase's layered meaning resists English translation and must not be misappropriated. **CARE/misattribution flag:** the widely circulated expanded "prayer" ("To the mineral nation that has built and maintained my bones…") is a **modern devotional composition**, not verified traditional text. (Metaphysical/observational; communal attribution.)

### Diné (Navajo)
***Hózhó*** — beauty, balance, harmony, order, health — is the central philosophy; "Walking in Beauty" and the Blessingway (*Hózhǫ́ǫ́jí*). Peer-reviewed source: **Michelle Kahn-John (Diné) & Mary Koithan, "Living in Health, Harmony, and Beauty: The Diné (Navajo) Hózhó Wellness Philosophy," *Global Advances in Health and Medicine* 4(3), 2015** (in-copyright). Kahn-John notes Hózhó holds that humans are "self-empowered through responsible thought, speech, and behavior" and can also "self-destruct" through irresponsibility. Diné historian **Dr. Jennifer Nez Denetdale** (Univ. of New Mexico) is a living scholarly voice: "Hózhó isn't merely an aesthetic; it's a profound way of life." **CARE flag:** many Diné stories are winter-only and sacred — record, do not extract. (Normative/metaphysical.)

### Aboriginal Australian (naming specific peoples)
**The Dreaming** — the English term was coined by Frank Gillen (1896) and popularized via Spencer & Gillen (1899); anthropologist **W.E.H. Stanner's** 1956 essay "The Dreaming" is a key (in-copyright) reference. It is **not** pan-Aboriginal-homogeneous: Warlpiri *Jukurrpa / Tjukurrpa*; the concept varies among Arrernte, Tiwi, Wiradjuri and others (some see it as past reality, others as concurrent past/present/future). **Country** and **custodianship**: Warrwa-Noongar woman **Louise O'Reilly** frames being on Country as "not about our right to own land, it is about our right to protect that land… a deeply imprinted sense of connection and responsibility." **CARE flag:** the Dreaming is place-based and access-restricted; do not decontextualise. (Metaphysical/normative; contested-by-group specifics.)

### Māori
***Kaitiakitanga*** (guardianship/stewardship, from *tiaki*), bound to *mana whenua*, *mauri*, *tapu*, *rāhui*, and *whakapapa*; statutorily recognized in the Resource Management Act 1991 (s7). The **Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017** (enacted 20 March 2017) makes the river a legal person — s14(1): *"Te Awa Tupua is a legal person and has all the rights, powers, duties, and liabilities of a legal person"* — reflecting the pepeha *"Ko au te awa, ko te awa ko au"* (I am the river and the river is me). Lead negotiator **Gerrard Albert**: *"we consider the river an ancestor and always have… treating the river as a living entity is the correct way to approach it, as an indivisible whole, instead of… ownership and management."* The Crown provided ~NZ$80 million in redress.

Whakataukī: *"He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata"* ("What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people"). This is a **whakatauākī** (known author), attributed to **Meri Ngāroto** (Ngāti Whātua), from a fuller saying about the harakeke (flax) and where the bellbird would sing. **Dissent to preserve:** Māori scholar **Hana Tapiata** argues the anthropocentric reading is incomplete within a te ao Māori view that centres land and whakapapa ("It is not people… It is connection, it is life"). Te Ara Encyclopedia (**Te Ahukaramū Charles Royal** entries) is a strong Māori-authored source. (Normative/prudential.)

### Inuit
**Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit (IQ)** — "that which Inuit have always known to be true" — is codified by the Government of Nunavut as guiding societal values, including *Avatittinnik Kamatsiarniq* (respect and care for land, animals, environment), *Inuuqatigiitsiarniq* (respect/relationships), *Pijitsirniq* (serving/providing — a stewardship concept), and *Aajiiqatigiinniq* (consensus decision-making). Primary sources: Government of Nunavut, *Incorporating Inuit Societal Values* (2013); **Karetak, Tagalik & Tester, *Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit: What Inuit Have Always Known to Be True* (Fernwood, 2017)** (in-copyright). (Normative/prudential; living governance.)

### Sámi
Core value ***birgejupmi*** (self-sufficiency, coping, "making do" — taking only what is necessary so as to be able to harvest again). Sources: **Keskitalo et al., "Birgejupmi – Life Skills, the Sámi Approach to Inclusion and Adapted Education," *Morning Watch* (Memorial Univ. of Newfoundland)**; **Nystad et al., *Education Sciences* 14(11):1246, 2024** (linking *birgen/birgejupmi* to *árbediehtu*, traditional knowledge). ***Meahcci*** — relational, productive land, mistranslated as "wilderness": **Solveig Joks, Liv Østmo & John Law, "Verbing meahcci: Living Sámi lands," *The Sociological Review* 68(2): 305–321, 2020.** **Philosophy of the gift**: **Rauna Kuokkanen (Sámi), "Láhi and Attáldat: The Philosophy of the Gift and Sami Education," *Australian Journal of Indigenous Education* 34 (2005): 20–32**, and *Reshaping the University: Responsibility, Indigenous Epistemes, and the Logic of the Gift* (UBC Press, 2007); *Boaris dego eana* (ČálliidLágádus, 2009). **Reindeer luck (*boazolihkku*)**: **Nils Oskal (Sámi), "On Nature and Reindeer Luck," *Rangifer* 20(2–3): 175–180, 2000** — on the pastoral worldview, herders *"should not conquer the world but try to get along with it and come to an understanding with it."* **Yoik** as oral memory/relationship to beings and place: **Harald Gaski, "The Reindeer on the Mountain, the Reindeer in the Mind: On Sámi Yoik Lyrics," in *Rights to Language* (Routledge, 2000)**; **Krister Stoor, *Juoiganmuitalusat / Jojkberättelser* (PhD, Umeå Univ., 2007)**. Earliest Sámi-language primary source: **Johan Turi, *Muitalus sámiid birra* (1910)**. **CARE flag:** many online "Sámi proverbs" are unattributed — flag as unverified; consult **Harald Gaski (ed.), *Time Is a Ship That Never Casts Anchor: Sámi Proverbs* (ČálliidLágádus, 2006)** for verbatim, sourced proverbs. (Normative/prudential; mostly in-copyright.)

### Andean (Quechua / Kichwa / Aymara)
***Ayni*** — sacred reciprocity, *"hoy por ti, mañana por mí"* ("today for you, tomorrow for me"), including reciprocity with **Pachamama** and the *apus*; core to the *ayllu* (community) and to communal labor (*minka*). The Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian is a strong source: "Ayni is a core value that has served generations of Andean people since pre-Inka times." ***Sumak kawsay / buen vivir*** — Kichwa "good living" — was enshrined in **Ecuador's 2008 Constitution**, whose Preamble resolves to "build a new form of public coexistence, in diversity and in harmony with nature, to achieve the good way of living, the *sumak kawsay*," and whose Article 14 recognizes the right to "a healthy and ecologically balanced environment that guarantees sustainability and the good way of living (*sumak kawsay*)"; the term/idea (Buen Vivir) appears roughly two dozen times in the text. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution parallels this (*suma qamaña*), alongside Rights of Nature/Pachamama. **Dissent to preserve:** scholars (Javier Cuestas-Caza; Norman & Dorothea Whitten) argue *sumak kawsay* ≠ *buen vivir* (not mere translation); Wikipedia and Eduardo Gudynas note it was **systematized/neologized** by Indigenous organizations (e.g., OPIP in Pastaza) in the 1990s — some critics call parts an "invention of tradition." (Normative/metaphysical; contested origin.)

## C) Central Dilemmas / Forks
1. **Stewardship vs. use** — take only what is given (Honorable Harvest; *birgejupmi*; *ayni* with Pachamama) vs. extraction/accumulation/ownership.
2. **Individual vs. collective-and-land** — personhood embedded in kin AND land (*Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ*, whakapapa, *ayllu*, *siida*) vs. the atomized individual property-holder.
3. **Tradition vs. change** — living adaptation (IQ in modern governance; Te Awa Tupua in statute; *sumak kawsay* in constitutions) vs. reification/relic-making.
4. **Coexistence vs. domination** — Two Row Wampum's parallel non-interference vs. "steering the other's vessel."
5. **Sacred/restricted knowledge vs. open sharing** — consent and context (Diné winter stories; the Dreaming) vs. extraction/appropriation.

## D) Virtues cultivated & Dangers guarded against
- **Virtues (cultivates_virtues):** reciprocity, gratitude, guardianship/kaitiakitanga, humility before the more-than-human, consensus, long-term foresight, hospitality/manaakitanga, self-sufficiency, balance/harmony (hózhó).
- **Dangers (guards_against):** greed/over-harvest, short-termism, arrogance/domination of nature, cultural appropriation, forgetting ancestors and the unborn, and false essentialism (the romantic "ecological Indian" stereotype).

## E) Candidate cross-tradition convergences & contradictions to test later
- **Reciprocity/relationality**: Andean *ayni* ↔ Sámi *láhi/attáldat* ↔ Potawatomi Honorable Harvest ↔ Lakota *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ* ↔ African communitarian *ubuntu* (parallel query). **Test:** genuine independent convergence, or analytic over-generalization imposed by outsiders? (Recurrence ≠ proof.)
- **Intergenerational responsibility**: Haudenosaunee seven-generation ↔ Māori *mō ngā uri whakatipu* (for the generations to come) ↔ intergenerational-ethics material elsewhere in the graph.
- **Contradiction to hold**: relational-holism claims vs. Krech's evidence (*The Ecological Indian*, Norton, 1999) that specific peoples did alter and sometimes over-exploit environments. Tendencies, not destinies; robustness, not proof.

## F) Misattribution watchlist
- **Chief Seattle's speech — APOCRYPHAL (popular version).** The famous ecological lines ("How can you buy or sell the sky"; "The earth does not belong to man…"; "the web of life") were written by **Ted Perry** for the film *Home*, produced by the Southern Baptist Radio and Television Commission (aired on ABC, 1972), building on **William Arrowsmith's** 1969 re-translation of the **1887 Henry A. Smith** transcript. As *Newsweek* summarized in 1992: "Chief Seattle did give a speech in 1854, but he never said 'The earth is our mother'… He never saw a buffalo." Perry himself later said: "Why are we so willing to accept a text like this if it's attributed to a Native American? It's another case of placing Native Americans up on a pedestal and not taking responsibility for our own actions." The 1855 "letter to President Franklin Pierce" is unhistorical (historian **Jerry L. Clark**: "an unhistorical artifact of someone's fertile literary imagination"). Even the 1887 Smith text is a filtered, embellished reconstruction produced 33 years later via Lushootseed→Chinook Jargon→English; the closing "There is no death, only a change of worlds" was added later (Bagley 1929; variously attributed to A.C. Ballard or John Rich).
- **"We do not inherit the earth from our ancestors; we borrow it from our children" — APOCRYPHAL.** NOT Chief Seattle and NOT a "Native American proverb." Quote Investigator tentatively credits **Wendell Berry, *The Unforeseen Wilderness* (1971)**, ch. "The One-Inch Journey": "a man who knows that the world is not given by his fathers, but borrowed from his children"; earliest attributed printed variant is May 1976 (credited to Dennis Hall and, separately, to Berry). Also linked to Lester Brown, Helen Caldicott, and the IUCN — a 1970s environmentalist coinage.
- **Generic "Native American proverbs"** attributed to unnamed or loosely named "tribes" ("Cherokee," "Sioux," "Minquass," etc.) on quote-aggregator sites — **DUBIOUS** unless tied to a named person or documented text.
- **The seventh-generation slogan** ("in every deliberation consider the seventh generation") — a real ethic, but **not** a verbatim Great Law quotation. Flag as tradition/paraphrase.
- **Expanded "Mitakuye Oyasin prayer"** ("To the mineral nation…") — **modern devotional composition**, not verified traditional Lakota text.
- **Unattributed "Sámi proverbs"** circulating online — **UNVERIFIED**; use Gaski (2006) for sourced examples.

## Recommendations (staged, with thresholds)
- **Stage 1 — Ingest only verified units now.** Mark every wisdom unit with `attribution_confidence`. Ingest as **verified/probable** only those tied to a named person/text/statute (Te Awa Tupua Act s14; Oskal's *boazolihkku*; Kuokkanen's *láhi/attáldat*; the Parker Great Law passage; Kahn-John on hózhó). Ingest *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ*, *ayni*, kaitiakitanga, IQ as **communal**. Quarantine all Chief Seattle "eco" lines and the "borrow it from our children" proverb as **apocryphal** with a visible provenance note. *Threshold to promote from dubious→probable:* a named primary edition with section/page.
- **Stage 2 — Consent & CARE gate before publishing any unit sourced to a living community.** For sacred/restricted material (Diné winter stories, Dreaming specifics), record existence + source only; do NOT reproduce content. *Threshold to publish content:* explicit community/consent documentation or an established public, community-authored source (e.g., Onondaga Nation, Te Ara, Govt of Nunavut).
- **Stage 3 — Test convergences empirically, don't assert them.** Create explicit `candidate_convergence` edges (reciprocity cluster; intergenerational cluster) flagged as hypotheses. *Threshold to upgrade to a stated convergence:* independent primary sources from ≥3 peoples AND at least one Indigenous scholar per tradition affirming the reading — plus a recorded counter-case (Krech) attached to preserve dissent.
- **Stage 4 — Prefer Indigenous authorship and public-domain primaries.** Where an in-copyright translation is the only source (Stanner, Kahn-John, Kuokkanen, Karetak et al.), tag `copyright` and quote minimally. Recruit an Indigenous reviewer per tradition before the book/website release.

## Caveats
- **No pan-Indigenous essentialism.** Every generalization above is a *tendency across specific named peoples*, not a universal Indigenous doctrine; the peoples differ profoundly in cosmology, language, and practice.
- **Recurrence ≠ truth.** Cross-tradition convergence is *robustness*, not proof; Krech's work is deliberately retained as counter-evidence to the "ecological Indian" trope.
- **Claim types kept distinct.** Religious/cosmological claims (the Dreaming, Pachamama, Hózhó's metaphysics, "the river is my ancestor") are tagged **metaphysical/normative**, never empirical. Legal facts (Te Awa Tupua Act; Ecuador 2008 Constitution) are empirical/documentary.
- **Translation loss is real.** *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ*, *meahcci*, *sumak kawsay*, and the Dreaming are all attested by their own communities to resist English translation; glosses here are approximations.
- **Oral vs. written tension.** For the Great Law and Two Row Wampum, the **oral tradition** is well attested and revered even where specific **written** artifacts are contested; keep these distinct in the graph.
- **A character's/interpreter's view ≠ endorsement.** Smith's 1887 "Seattle," Perry's script, and even some modern devotional expansions are the words of intermediaries, not verbatim Indigenous speakers — always attribute to the actual writer.
