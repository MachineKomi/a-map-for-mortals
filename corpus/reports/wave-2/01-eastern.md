# A Map for Mortals — East Asian & Indian Wisdom Module (Source-Critical Research Report)

## TL;DR
- The scoped Chinese, Indian, Buddhist, and Japanese corpus yields a coherent set of **recurring forks** — engagement vs. withdrawal, effort vs. acceptance, desire vs. non-attachment, self-cultivation vs. spontaneity, duty-to-kin vs. duty-to-truth — each anchorable to *verified* primary verses (Gita 2.47, Daodejing 64/37, Dhammapada 1–5, Mencius 2A6, Xunzi 23, Analects 15.24) with public-domain translations available.
- **Misattribution is the single largest data-integrity risk**: fabricated "Buddha," "Laozi," and "Confucius" quotes dominate search results; verified genuine anchors exist but must be pulled from primary editions (Buddharakkhita, Legge, Müller, Watson) and cross-checked against fakebuddhaquotes.com and taoistic.com.
- Keep **claim types rigorously separated**: karma/rebirth/moksha/nibbana are **metaphysical**; the "tranquility" and "non-attachment" payoffs are **prudential/observational**; only meditation-outcome claims may cite empirical studies, and must not lend their authority to the metaphysics.

## Key Findings

1. **The Confucian–Daoist fork is a first-class dilemma with textual teeth on both sides.** Confucius/Mencius/Xunzi prescribe effortful cultivation (*xiushen*), ritual (*li*), and role-duty; the Daodejing and Zhuangzi prescribe *wu-wei* (non-forcing) and naturalness (*ziran*). This is not a vague "East is holistic" cliché — it is a live disagreement about whether the good life is *built* or *unblocked*, and both positions are quotable to section/verse.

2. **Confucianism itself splits internally on human nature** — Mencius (nature is good, "four sprouts," 2A6) vs. Xunzi (nature is bad/detestable, ch. 23). This is preserved dissent, not a footnote: it maps directly onto the effort-vs-acceptance and cultivation debates and is the philosophical seedbed from which Han Feizi's Legalism (Xunzi's student Li Si; Han Feizi drew on Xunzi) grew its hard-nosed counter-voice.

3. **Cross-tradition convergence candidates are real but must be flagged, not asserted as proof.** Gita 2.47 (act without attachment to fruits) ↔ Stoic dichotomy of control (already covered) ↔ Buddhist non-attachment is the strongest convergence triad. Tranquility-as-byproduct ↔ ataraxia is a second. These are **robustness signals**, not truth-claims, and independent origination is the point.

4. **Japanese aesthetic wisdom (mono no aware, wabi-sabi) is observational, not prescriptive** — and its canonical formulation is a *reception*, largely fixed by Motoori Norinaga's treatise *Genji monogatari Tama no Ogushi* ("The Tale of Genji: A Little Jeweled Comb," 1799), which argued that mono no aware was the central theme of Genji — a polemical claim that rejected the then-dominant Buddhist/Confucian moral readings, not a timeless doctrine. Hagakure's "bushido" must be treated critically: per Oleg Benesch's *Inventing the Way of the Samurai* (Oxford UP, 2014), "all modern bushidō theories are later constructs with no direct continuity from pre-Meiji history," and the discourse spread "only after 1900, especially after the Russo-Japanese War of 1904–5."

5. **Public-domain anchors exist for nearly every unit** (Legge, Müller, Buddharakkhita, Pope), but several distort: Legge's Christianising register (rendering *Tian* as "Heaven"/God-like), Müller's "No, no!" for *neti neti*, and Victorian moralizing in Pope's Kural. Name the in-copyright modern standard (Watson, Ivanhoe, Bodhi, Olivelle, Slingerland) for comparison in each case.

## Details

### Chinese tradition

**Confucius, *Analects* 15.24 (the negative Golden Rule / *shu*).** Verified. Legge (numbered 15.23 in his edition): "Is not RECIPROCITY such a word? What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others." *Shu* is variously rendered reciprocity (Legge), consideration, sympathetic understanding, altruism (Chan). Note the numbering discrepancy (Legge 15.23 = Watson/Slingerland 15.24) and that 5.12 shows Zigong claiming to have mastered this and Confucius telling him he has not — a cultivation-is-hard datum.

**Mencius 2A6 (four sprouts; child-at-the-well).** Verified via SEP and multiple translations. The spontaneous alarm anyone feels seeing a child about to fall into a well is offered as evidence that humans have innate "sprouts" (*duan*) of the four virtues (ren, yi, li, zhi). Scholarly caution (Van Norden, Warp/Weft/Way): *duan* as "sprout" vs. "tip" is contested, and 2A6 alone underdetermines the cultivationist reading. D.C. Lau (Penguin) and Bryan Van Norden (Hackett) are the in-copyright standards; Legge (1895) is public domain.

**Xunzi ch. 23 "Human Nature is Evil/Detestable."** Verified. Watson (Columbia, in-copyright): opens by naming Mencius and declaring him wrong; "since human nature is evil, it must await the instructions of a teacher and the model before it can be put aright." Note the scholarly minority (Tang Siufu and others) arguing *e* is better rendered "detestable" or that Xunzi's view is "human nature is simple/uncarved, not evil." Preserve this dissent.

**Daodejing 64 ("journey of a thousand miles").** Verified genuine, Ch. 64. Legge (1891): "The journey of a thousand li commenced with a single step." Literal Chinese *qiān lǐ zhī xíng, shǐ yú zú xià* = "a journey of a thousand li begins beneath one's feet." One *li* ≈ 0.31 mi, so "thousand miles" is idiomatic, not literal. **Frequently misattributed to Confucius** — it is Laozi. The wording "begins with a single step" is a legitimate idiomatic rendering; "begins beneath one's feet" is more literal.

**Daodejing 37 (wu-wei).** Verified. D.C. Lau: "The way never acts yet nothing is left undone." Legge: "The Tao in its regular course does nothing for the sake of doing it, and so there is nothing which it does not do." Pair with ch. 48 (decrease and decrease again) and ch. 2. This is the textual core of the self-cultivation-vs-spontaneity fork.

**Zhuangzi (butterfly dream, ch. 2; useless tree, ch. 1/4).** Verified. Watson (Columbia, standard): "he didn't know if he was Zhuang Zhou who had dreamt he was a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming he was Zhuang Zhou... This is called the Transformation of Things." The "useless tree" preserves itself precisely by being useless — a pointed counter to Confucian usefulness/duty. Watson and Ziporyn (Hackett) are the modern standards; Legge is public domain.

**Han Feizi (Legalism, "The Two Handles").** Verified. Watson: the ruler controls ministers by "two handles" — punishment (*xing*) and reward (*de*). Descriptive/prudential political realism; explicitly rejects Confucian *ren*/*yi* as useless for statecraft. Treat as descriptive counter-voice. Han Fei was Xunzi's student; forced to suicide 233 BCE.

### Indian tradition

**Bhagavad Gita 2.47 (karma yoga).** Verified, most-quoted Gita verse. *karmaṇy evādhikāras te mā phaleṣu kadācana* — "You have a right to your action alone, never to its fruits; let not the fruit of action be your motive, nor let your attachment be to inaction." Edgerton (1944) and Besant are public domain; note that the verse is *action without attachment to results*, NOT "do not care about results." Gandhi (Young India, 1925) and Shankara/Ramanuja commentaries diverge on nuance. **Prime convergence candidate** with Stoic dichotomy of control — flag for merge, do not re-extract Western side.

**Dhammapada 1–5 (Yamakavagga, "The Pairs").** Verified. Buddharakkhita (Buddhist Publication Society, 1985): v.1 "Mind precedes all mental states. Mind is their chief; they are all mind-wrought." v.5 "Hatred is never appeased by hatred in this world. By non-hatred alone is hatred appeased. This is a law eternal." **Translation caution:** Bhikkhu Dhammanando notes Buddharakkhita's insertion of "all" is not in the Pali; K.R. Norman renders *manopubbaṅgamā dhammā* as "Mental phenomena are preceded by mind." Müller (SBE, public domain) renders v.5 "hatred ceases by love." Thanissaro: "Phenomena are preceded by the heart." Do not over-read v.1 as idealism ("we are what we think," Byrom) — that is a loose paraphrase.

**Four Noble Truths (Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta, SN 56.11).** Verified via Bodhi (Wisdom Publications). The four truths each carry a task: suffering to be fully understood; craving (its origin) to be abandoned; cessation to be realized; the eightfold path to be developed. Metaphysical/normative framing for the desire-vs-non-attachment fork.

**Yoga Sutras of Patanjali 1.2, 1.12.** Verified. 1.2: *yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ* — "yoga is the cessation/stilling of the fluctuations of the mind-stuff." 1.12: *abhyāsa-vairāgyābhyāṃ tan-nirodhaḥ* — the fluctuations are stilled by practice (*abhyasa*) and non-attachment (*vairagya*). Practice units. Note: no physical postures in the definition. In-copyright standard: Edwin Bryant (2009).

**Tirukkural (Tiruvalluvar).** Verified structure: 1,330 couplets in three books — Aram (virtue), Porul (wealth/statecraft), Inbam (love). G.U. Pope's 1886 verse translation is public domain and the best-known English rendering. **Translation caution:** Pope was a Christian missionary and scholars note occasional interpretive overlays aligning to Western/Christian virtue-frameworks; the Porul section is pragmatic, consequentialist statecraft (cause→effect in rulership), distinct from Kautilya's amoral realpolitik. Secular, non-denominational — names no deity in its ethics.

**Upanishads (selected).** Verified via Müller SBE (public domain):
- *Katha* 1.3.14: "Rise, awake! having obtained your boons, understand them! The sharp edge of a razor is difficult to pass over; thus the wise say the path (to the Self) is hard." **Flag:** the popular "Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached" is **Vivekananda's paraphrase, not scripture**; Maugham's *Razor's Edge* epigraph inserts "to Salvation," not in the Sanskrit. Olivelle's signature choice is "poets say" (*kavayaḥ*) vs. Müller's "the wise say."
- *Chandogya* 6.8.7: "It is the True. It is the Self, and thou, O Svetaketu, art it" (*tat tvam asi*). One of the four mahavakyas. **Flag:** Brereton/Olivelle argue the grammar does not support "That thou art" and render "that's how you are, Svetaketu" — a significant divergence from the Advaita reading.
- *Brihadaranyaka* 2.3.6 (primary locus of *neti neti*): Müller renders it "No, no!" — **the familiar "not this, not this" comes from later translators (Madhavananda, Hume), not Müller.**
- *Brihadaranyaka* 1.3.28: "Lead me from the unreal to the real! Lead me from darkness to light! Lead me from death to immortality!" (*asato ma sad gamaya*).
- *Isha* 1: "When thou hast surrendered all this, then thou mayest enjoy. Do not covet the wealth of any man!" **Flag:** *tena tyaktena bhuñjīthāḥ* is one of the most contested lines in the corpus — "enjoy through renunciation" vs. "enjoy what is allotted." Olivelle (Oxford) is the in-copyright modern standard for all of these.

### Buddhist beyond India

**Shantideva, *Bodhicaryavatara* ch. 6 (Patience/*kshanti*).** Verified. v.1: a single flash of anger destroys merit gathered over a thousand aeons. v.2: "There is no evil like hatred, and no fortitude like patience." The determinism argument (vv.22–30): since aggressors are themselves driven by conditions/afflictions, anger at them is as irrational as anger at a stick or a wind-blown tree. Crosby & Skilton (Oxford, 1995) is the in-copyright scholarly translation. SEP (Goodman) notes interpretive dispute over Shantideva's exact form of determinism. Convergence candidate with Stoic/CBT reframing of anger.

**Nagarjuna, *Mulamadhyamakakarika* (selectively).** Core doctrines: emptiness (*sunyata*), dependent origination (*pratityasamutpada*), two truths (conventional/ultimate). Jay Garfield's translation (Oxford, 1995) is the standard in-copyright English. Treat metaphysically; flag interpretive difficulty — Nagarjuna is deliberately apophatic and heavily contested among Madhyamaka interpreters. *Primary-verse wording not verified in this pass — mark units drawn from him as "probable" pending edition check.*

**Zen/Chan (Dogen, koan tradition).** Dogen's *Genjokoan* ("to study the self is to forget the self") is widely cited; **flag interpretive difficulty honestly** — Dogen's 13th-c. Japanese is notoriously multivalent and translations (Tanahashi; Waddell & Abe; Nishijima) diverge substantially. *Exact wording not verified in this pass — mark "probable."* Koans resist propositional paraphrase by design; represent them as *practices/provocations*, not as insight-claims.

### Japanese aesthetic wisdom

**Mono no aware.** Verified via SEP (Japanese Aesthetics) and multiple sources. "The pathos of things" — a sensitivity to impermanence (*mujo*) and gentle sadness at transience. The concept existed in Heian literature but was given theoretical precision by **Motoori Norinaga** (1730–1801) in *Genji monogatari Tama no Ogushi* (1799), where he held it the central theme of *The Tale of Genji*. His actual tripartite formulation (Marra/Harper translation) states Genji was written "to depict the varieties of human emotion (mono no aware), to acquaint the reader with the workings of human emotions (*mono no aware o shirashimuru koto*), and to depict people who are deeply sensitive to human emotions (*mono no aware o shiru hito*)." Observational/aesthetic claim type. Cherry blossoms are the prototypical object.

**Wabi-sabi.** Aesthetic appreciation of imperfection, impermanence, incompleteness. Observational. (SEP Japanese Aesthetics is the reputable anchor.)

**Yoshida Kenko, *Tsurezuregusa* (Essays in Idleness), c. 1330–1332.** Verified. Donald Keene's translation (Columbia UP, 1967) is the standard (in-copyright); George Sansom's 1911 is older/public domain. Keene, sec. 7, in full context: "If man were never to fade away like the dews of Adashino, never to vanish like the smoke over Toribeyama, but lingered on forever in the world, how things would lose their power to move us! The most precious thing in life is its uncertainty." Kenko celebrates impermanence as a *source of beauty* — Keene notes this differs from both the Greek view (uncertainty as grief) and orthodox Buddhism (which rarely saw impermanence as desirable). Distinguish Kenko-the-aesthete from doctrinal Buddhism.

**Hagakure (Yamamoto Tsunetomo, early 18th c.) — treat critically.** "The Way of the Samurai is found in death." **Mandatory caveat:** Hagakure was obscure until the 20th century; its cult status can be dated precisely. Per Oleg Benesch, *Inventing the Way of the Samurai* (Oxford UP, 2014), an "early Bushido Boom" ran 1894–1905 and a "late Bushido Boom" 1905–1914, both driven by the Sino-Japanese and Russo-Japanese Wars, feeding a later "Showa Bushido Resurgence" tied to pre-WWII militarism. Benesch: "all modern bushidō theories are later constructs with no direct continuity from pre-Meiji history, while it is precisely the claims to such continuity that make bushidō an invented tradition" (p. 8). The systematized "code" was substantially popularized for Western and modern-Japanese audiences by **Inazo Nitobe's *Bushido: The Soul of Japan*** (first published in English, New York, 1899; the widely cited "1900" edition), which opens: "Chivalry is a flower no less indigenous to the soil of Japan than its emblem, the cherry blossom." Scholars call Nitobe's account "a romanticized interpretation... the morals defined by Nitobe do not represent all of bushido." Represent Hagakure as one idiosyncratic voice, not "the samurai code," and never as empirical history of samurai practice.

## Recommendations (staged)

**Stage 1 — Lock the verified core (do now).** Ingest the ~20 fully-verified units (Gita 2.47; Daodejing 37/64; Analects 15.24; Mencius 2A6; Xunzi 23; Dhammapada 1–5; Yoga Sutras 1.2/1.12; Han Feizi Two Handles; Shantideva 6.1–2; the five Upanishad verses; Kenko; mono no aware). Each has a public-domain anchor plus a named in-copyright modern standard. Tag every religious/soteriological payoff metaphysical or normative.

**Stage 2 — Quarantine the "probable" units (Nagarjuna, Dogen, koans)** until primary-edition wording is verified against Garfield / Tanahashi / Crosby-Skilton print editions. Do not publish their quotes at "verified" confidence. Benchmark that would promote them: a section/verse match against a named scholarly edition.

**Stage 3 — Wire the convergence edges as testable hypotheses, not conclusions.** Create explicit "convergence_candidate" edges (Gita 2.47 ↔ Stoic control; non-attachment ↔ ataraxia; Shantideva anger ↔ Stoic/CBT reframing; Seneca imagined-suffering ↔ Buddhist *papañca*). Flag each `FOR MERGE` with the prior Stoic report. Threshold to upgrade from "candidate" to "robust": independent origination + no direct historical transmission + matching mechanism.

**Stage 4 — Run every candidate quote through the misattribution gate** (fakebuddhaquotes.com, taoistic.com, Legge/Müller/Buddharakkhita primary text) before it enters the graph. Any quote failing to match a section/verse gets attribution_confidence ≤ dubious.

**What would change these recommendations:** if primary-edition checks fail for the "probable" units, drop them rather than downgrade the whole graph's credibility; if a convergence's historical-transmission channel is found (e.g., Hellenistic–Buddhist contact), reclassify it from "independent convergence" to "possible borrowing."

## Caveats
- **Recurrence ≠ proof.** Every cross-tradition echo in this report is a robustness signal at most; several may reflect shared human psychology or (rarely) transmission, not independent confirmation of truth.
- **Tendencies, not destinies.** All prudential claims (non-attachment reduces suffering; patience defuses anger) are conditional and probabilistic, not laws.
- **Claim-type leakage is the subtlest danger.** The literature constantly slides from "meditation calms the mind" (empirical/prudential) to "and therefore rebirth/no-self is true" (metaphysical). Hold the line.
- **Translation is interpretation.** Legge Christianises; Müller predates modern philology; Pope moralizes; Buddharakkhita inserts "all." Every unit carries a translator/edition field for a reason.
- **Character ≠ author.** In the Gita, Krishna's counsel to Arjuna is a character's speech in an epic; in Zhuangzi, the parables' "speakers" are literary devices. Do not read endorsement flatly.
- **Nagarjuna, Dogen, and koan units are unverified in this pass** and marked "probable"; do not treat their wordings as confirmed.

## C) Central Dilemmas / Forks

1. **Engagement vs. withdrawal** — Confucian role-duty & Gita's call to act (2.47) vs. Zhuangzi's "useless tree" & the hermit ideal in Kenko. Consequence tendency: engagement builds social goods but courts entanglement/burnout; withdrawal preserves integrity/peace but risks irrelevance and abandoning others.
2. **Effort vs. acceptance** — Xunzi/cultivation & Katha's "razor's edge" & Daodejing 64's persistence vs. wu-wei (DDJ 37) & acceptance of impermanence (Kenko, mono no aware). Tendency: striving achieves and improves but generates anxiety and can "spoil things on the verge of success" (DDJ 64); acceptance yields peace but can shade into passivity.
3. **Desire vs. non-attachment** — Four Noble Truths, Yoga Sutras 1.2/1.12, Isha 1 vs. ordinary desire and Han Feizi's frank reliance on desire (reward/punishment). Tendency: non-attachment reduces suffering (prudential/observational) but its soteriological payoff (nibbana/moksha) is metaphysical.
4. **Self-cultivation vs. spontaneity** — the headline Confucian–Daoist fork. Building character through ritual/learning vs. unblocking natural flow. Both quotable; neither refutes the other.
5. **Duty to family/lord vs. duty to truth/others** — Confucian graded love & filial priority and Hagakure's lord-loyalty vs. Mohist/impartial concern and the Buddhist universalization of compassion. Tendency: kin-priority sustains stable relationships but can license nepotism/factionalism; impartiality is fairer but humanly harder to sustain.

## D) Virtues & Dangers

**Virtues cultivated:** reciprocity/shu, ren (benevolence), yi (rightness), patience/kshanti, non-attachment/vairagya, equanimity, mindfulness, forbearance, naturalness/ziran, self-knowledge, sensitivity (mono no aware), persistence, contentment.

**Dangers guarded against:** egoism & cruelty (Analects), cynicism (Mencius), complacency (Xunzi, Katha), over-striving & controlling (Daodejing), outcome-anxiety & paralysis (Gita), revenge & escalation (Dhammapada, Shantideva), greed/covetousness (Isha, Yoga Sutras), reification/nihilism (Nagarjuna), self-preoccupation (Dogen), numbness to transience (Kenko/mono no aware), naive idealism & being manipulated (Han Feizi), and — critically — **fanaticism** (Hagakure, as a danger the unit itself can represent, not only guard against).

## E) Cross-Tradition Convergences & Contradictions to Test

**Convergence candidates (robustness, not proof — FLAG FOR MERGE with prior Stoic report):**
- Gita 2.47 karma-yoga ↔ Stoic dichotomy of control ↔ Buddhist non-attachment. Strongest triad.
- Tranquility/equanimity as *byproduct* of non-grasping ↔ Stoic *ataraxia*.
- Shantideva ch. 6 (anger is irrational because aggressors are conditioned) ↔ Stoic & CBT cognitive reframing of anger.
- Buddhist *papañca* (mental proliferation) ↔ Seneca on imagined/anticipated suffering (held in prior report).
- Dhammapada 5 (non-hatred ends hatred) ↔ Shantideva ch. 6 — *intra*-Buddhist convergence.
- Impermanence: Buddhist *anicca* ↔ Kenko/mono no aware ↔ Stoic *memento mori* (aesthetic vs. ethical framing differs — a productive contrast).

**Contradictions / tensions to test:**
- Mencius (nature good) vs. Xunzi (nature bad) — unresolved within Confucianism.
- Confucian effortful cultivation vs. Daoist wu-wei — the headline fork; genuinely opposed prescriptions.
- Gita's call to *act* (even in war) vs. Buddhist/Jain *ahimsa* and withdrawal — duty vs. non-harm.
- Han Feizi's amoral statecraft vs. Confucian virtue-rule and Kural's moral statecraft — do rewards/punishments or virtue produce order?
- Confucian graded/filial love vs. Mohist impartial care vs. Buddhist universal compassion.

## F) Misattribution Watchlist (mandatory)

1. **"When the student is ready, the teacher will appear."** NOT Laozi, NOT Buddha. Traced to Theosophical origin (often Blavatsky); no match in Daodejing (Gia-Fu Feng/English) or Pali canon. Confidence: apocryphal.
2. **"A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step."** GENUINE Laozi, Daodejing 64 — but **frequently misattributed to Confucius**. Verify wording; literal is "begins beneath one's feet." Confidence: verified (Laozi).
3. **Fake Buddha quotes** — the single worst attribution swamp. Flagged fakes per fakebuddhaquotes.com include: "The mind is everything. What you think you become"; "Holding on to anger is like grasping a hot coal..."; "Believe nothing... unless it agrees with your own reason"; "Three things cannot be long hidden: the sun, the moon, and the truth"; virtually all "Buddha quotes on friendship." Check every candidate against the site + primary suttas.
4. **"We are what we think / All that we are arises with our thoughts."** (Byrom's loose Dhammapada 1 paraphrase.) The verse actually reads "Mind precedes all mental states" (Buddharakkhita); do not use the idealist paraphrase as the quote.
5. **"Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached."** This is **Vivekananda's paraphrase**, not Katha Upanishad. Only "Rise, awake!" is scriptural (Katha 1.3.14).
6. **"Tat tvam asi = That thou art."** Genuine (Chandogya 6.8.7) but the standard translation is contested (Brereton/Olivelle: "that's how you are"). Flag translation, not authenticity.
7. **Einstein "Buddhism is the religion of the future..."** — spurious; commonly bundled with Buddhist quote-lists. Confidence: apocryphal (not Einstein).
8. **Confucius "fortune-cookie" quotes** ("Choose a job you love and you will never work a day...", "The man who moves a mountain begins by carrying away small stones") — mostly unverifiable against the Analects; treat as dubious unless section-matched.
9. **Hagakure as "the bushido code."** Not a misquote but a misframing: per Benesch (Oxford UP, 2014), the systematized code is a Meiji-era/20th-c. construction with no direct pre-Meiji continuity, popularized by Nitobe (1899/1900). Flag whenever Hagakure is cited as representative samurai doctrine.
