# African Philosophy & Oral Wisdom Traditions — A Map for Mortals Research Report

*(Written from the perspective of a scholar of African philosophy and oral tradition, attentive to provenance and to living, plural traditions — not a monolithic "African wisdom.")*

## TL;DR
- African philosophy offers a rigorously debated (not monolithic) family of positions on the individual-vs-community fork, anchored by the Ubuntu maxim "a person is a person through other persons" — but this is a *contested normative* claim (Menkiti's radical vs. Gyekye's moderate communitarianism), not a settled "African wisdom."
- The great proverb and oral traditions (Yoruba, Akan/Ashanti with Adinkra, Zulu/Nguni, Igbo, Swahili utendi, Anansi folktales) are genuine wisdom units, but must be attributed to specific peoples/languages with `attribution_confidence: communal`, and guarded against colonial-era distortion (Tempels) and pan-continental fakery ("it takes a village," "go fast/go far").
- Central forks: individual vs community/personhood; destiny (Ori/Ayanmo) vs effort (ese); deference to elders vs questioning; hospitality/reciprocity. These resonate with — and should be flagged for merge against — the Western engagement-vs-withdrawal fork and the prior Golden Rule/one-body cluster.

## Key Findings

1. **Ubuntu is scholarly-contested, not a slogan.** The Nguni maxim *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* ("a person is a person through other persons") appears across Bantu languages (Zulu, Xhosa, Shona *munhu munhu nevanhu*, Sotho *motho ke motho ka batho*, Venda *muthu ndi muthu nga vhathu*, Tsonga). Desmond Tutu popularised it theologically; Thaddeus Metz formalised it as a relational moral theory (right acts promote communal harmony/shared identity + solidarity). But critics argue the normative definition is "purposely fuzzy" (N. Mboti, 2015), and Bernard Matolino & Wenceslaus Kwindingwi, "The end of ubuntu," *South African Journal of Philosophy* 32(2): 197–205 (2013), DOI 10.1080/02580136.2013.817637 — argue that ubuntu, though "seen as the authentic African ethical concept… a definition of life itself," should be resisted as a "concerted 'ubuntu-isation'"; rebutted by Thaddeus Metz, "Just the beginning for ubuntu," *SAJP* 33 (2014). Tag all Ubuntu ontological claims **metaphysical/normative**, never empirical.

2. **The personhood debate has a clear, dissent-rich structure.** Mbiti's axiom → Menkiti's *radical* communitarianism (personhood is *attained*, processual, duties primary) → Gyekye's *moderate* communitarianism (community only *partly* constitutes the person; individual retains intrinsic worth and rights) → Wiredu's consensus/sympathetic-impartiality model → Molefe/Matolino/Ikuenobe extensions.

3. **Yoruba thought supplies a rigorous destiny-vs-effort fork.** *Ori* (inner head/destiny), chosen before birth (*akunleyan/akunlegba/ayanmo*), is balanced by *ese* ("leg" = effort/struggle) and *iwa* (character). Scholars (Gbadegesin, Makinde, Abimbola, Oladipo) mostly read this as *soft determinism*, not fatalism — destiny is conditional. Sophie Oluwole recovered Orunmila/Ifá as philosophy comparable to Socrates.

4. **Colonial distortion is a real provenance hazard.** Placide Tempels' *Bantu Philosophy* (1945/1959) was written for missionaries/colonial administrators; Paulin Hountondji's critique labelled it "ethnophilosophy" guilty of "unanimism" (a single immutable collective African philosophy) and "extraversion" (written for outsiders). Treat Tempels critically and never as a primary African voice.

5. **Proverbs are communal wisdom units with real, often public-domain editions.** Akan proverbs are collected in J.G. Christaller, *Twi mmebusɛm, mpensã-ahansĩa mmoaano: A Collection of three thousand and six hundred Tshi proverbs, in use among the Negroes of the Gold Coast speaking the Asante and Fante language* (Basel: Basel German Evangelical Missionary Society, 1879), a public-domain scan held by the U.S. Library of Congress (loc.gov/item/43019087); R.S. Rattray's *Ashanti Proverbs* (1916, public domain); Akrofi's *Twi Mmebusem* (1958). Igbo proverbs are foregrounded in Achebe. Swahili classical verse (*Utendi wa Tambuka*, 1728; *Utendi wa Mwana Kupona*, 1858) survives in Ajami manuscripts and Allen's *Tendi* (1971).

## Details

### The Ubuntu / communitarian-personhood debate

**John Mbiti (Kenya, 1931–2019)** — *African Religions and Philosophy* (1969). Coined the influential axiom "I am because we are, and since we are, therefore I am." This is a foundational descriptive–normative statement of Afro-communitarianism. Note: it is Mbiti's *scholarly gloss*, not a verbatim traditional proverb.

**Ifeanyi Menkiti (Nigeria, 1940–2019)** — "Person and Community in African Traditional Thought" (1984). Radical/processual communitarianism: personhood is *attained* through discharge of communal obligations, moving from the "it-status of early childhood" to "person-status of later years"; duties are primary, rights secondary; "the reality of the communal world takes precedence over the reality of individual life histories" (p. 171). The famous passage is verified verbatim at p. 176: "personhood is the sort of thing which has to be attained, and is attained in direct proportion as one participates in communal life through the discharge of the various obligations defined by one's stations… transforms one from the it-status of early childhood, marked by an absence of moral function, into the person-status of later years."

**Kwame Gyekye (Ghana, 1939–2019)** — *Tradition and Modernity* (1997); "Person and Community in Akan Thought" (1992). Moderate communitarianism: the communal constitution of the person "can only be partial" (1997: 53); the individual has natural mental/rational capacities the community "only discovers and nurtures," plus intrinsic worth and rights. Directly attacks Menkiti's radical view. (In-copyright; verify p. 53 against a print copy for final publication.)

**Kwasi Wiredu (Ghana, 1931–2022)** — "conceptual decolonization"; consensus (non-party) democracy rooted in Akan political practice; Akan truth-as-consonance vs Western correspondence. Personhood is socially conferred through language, ethics, reciprocity yet remains vulnerable to moral loss.

**Sophie Oluwole (Nigeria, 1935–2018)** — first female philosophy PhD in Nigeria; *Socrates and Orunmila: Two Patron Saints of Classical Philosophy* (2014). Recovered the Yoruba oral Ifá corpus as secular philosophy; championed *complementarity* (matter/non-matter as complementary, not opposed) and women's status in Yoruba thought. Argued that if Socrates (who wrote nothing) is "father of Western philosophy," Orunmila (who likewise transmitted orally) deserves parallel standing.

**Placide Tempels (treated critically)** — *Bantu Philosophy* (1945). The "vital force" ontology; criticised by Césaire and Fanon as part of the colonial project, and by Hountondji as ethnophilosophy. Positive readings by Mudimbe and Kagame. Do not present his "vital force" as *the* African philosophy.

### Proverb & oral traditions (attribute to people/language, never a single sage)

- **Yoruba (Nigeria):** *iwa* (character/being/nature); Ori/destiny; *ese* (effort); complementarity.
- **Akan/Ashanti (Twi, Ghana):** proverbs encoded in **Adinkra** symbols — *Sankofa* ("Se wo were fi na wosankofa a yenkyi" = "it is not taboo to go back for what you forgot"), *Gye Nyame* ("except God"), *Dwennimmen* (ram's horns; humility of the strong), *Ti koro nko agyina* ("one head does not hold council"). Elder-authority framing: proverbs are prefixed "nananom/mpanyinfoɔ se" ("the elders/ancestors say").
- **Zulu/Nguni (South Africa):** *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu*.
- **Igbo (Nigeria):** "Proverbs are the palm-oil with which words are eaten" (Achebe, *Things Fall Apart*, ch. 1) — a literary rendering, not folk verbatim; distinguish author from character.
- **Swahili (Kenya/Tanzania coast):** *Utendi wa Mwana Kupona* (1858, Mwana Kupona binti Mshamu, mother's advice to daughter); *Utendi wa Tambuka* (1728, Bwana Mwengo wa Athman — among the earliest dated Swahili documents). Islamic-inflected didactic verse in the utenzi form (four-line, eight-syllable stanzas).
- **Anansi (Akan Kwaku Ananse):** trickster who wins the sky-god Nyame's stories; wit over strength; "Pot of Wisdom" tale = wisdom grows when shared. Morally neutral archetype; survives in diaspora as Anancy/Aunt Nancy.

### Central forks
1. Individual vs community (Menkiti ↔ Gyekye ↔ Wiredu).
2. Destiny (Ori/Ayanmo) vs effort (ese) — soft determinism.
3. Deference to elders vs questioning (elder wisdom vs "a child who asks questions does not become a fool").
4. Hospitality/reciprocity/restorative justice vs retribution (Ubuntu, Truth and Reconciliation Commission).

### Virtues & dangers
Virtues: *iwa pele* (good character), generosity, hospitality, solidarity, humility, communal harmony, respect for elders, ingenuity, forgiveness. Dangers: selfishness, pride/greed, rupture of community, gerontocratic suppression of dissent, essentialism, colonial distortion, fatalism.

## Recommendations
1. **Ingest the personhood fork as a *contested* node** with both Menkiti (radical) and Gyekye (moderate) poles preserved, plus Wiredu's consensus variant; link to the Western engagement-vs-withdrawal fork (flag for merge, do not re-derive).
2. **Attribute every proverb to a named people/language** with `attribution_confidence: communal`; reject bare "African proverb" tags.
3. **Add a misattribution watchlist node** (Section F) that blocks pan-"African proverb" fakes at ingestion.
4. **Cross-reference the Egyptian instruction texts** (Ptahhotep, Amenemope) already held from the Near-Eastern report rather than re-extracting; flag the Golden Rule/one-body cluster convergence.
5. **Threshold to reclassify** a unit from `contested` → `conditional`: independent attestation in ≥2 peer-reviewed sources with a named language and a citable edition. Threshold to *demote* a proverb to `dubious`: no named people/language traceable in any scholarly source.

## Caveats
- Many web proverb sites are unreliable; several quotes here are verified only against secondary scholarship and are flagged accordingly.
- "I am because we are" is a scholarly axiom (Mbiti 1969), not a traditional verbatim proverb.
- Ubuntu ontological claims are metaphysical/normative and must never be tagged empirical.
- Gyekye 1997: 53 wording was confirmed via a peer-reviewed journal block-quote; verify against the OUP print page before publication.
- Swahili classical verse fuses Islamic theology with Swahili culture — tag its religious content normative/metaphysical.

## C) Central Dilemmas / Forks
1. **Individual vs community** — Menkiti (radical) vs Gyekye (moderate) vs Wiredu (consensus). *Flag for merge with the Western engagement-vs-withdrawal fork.*
2. **Destiny vs effort** — Ori/Ayanmo vs ese/iwa; soft determinism.
3. **Deference to elders vs questioning** — elder authority ("the words of an elder are more potent than a charm") vs the value of the questioning child.
4. **Hospitality/restorative justice vs retribution** — Ubuntu/TRC.
5. **Cunning vs force** — Anansi tales.
6. **Directness vs indirection** — proverbial (indirect) speech as the Akan/Igbo ideal.

## D) Virtues & Dangers
**Virtues cultivated:** *iwa pele* (good character), solidarity, hospitality, generosity, humility, forgiveness, respect for elders, ingenuity, communal harmony, diligence. **Dangers guarded against:** selfishness, pride/greed, isolation, revenge/resentment, community overreach & suppression of dissent, fatalism/passivity, cultural amnesia, essentialism, colonial distortion.

## E) Candidate Cross-Tradition Convergences & Contradictions (to test later)
- **Convergence (robustness, not proof):** Ubuntu solidarity ↔ prior Golden Rule / one-body cluster (Hillel · Sermon on the Mount · Saadi). *Flag; do not re-extract.*
- **Convergence:** Sankofa ("go back and get it") ↔ Confucian reverence for tradition.
- **Tension:** Menkiti's duties-first personhood ↔ Western liberal rights-first individualism ↔ engagement-vs-withdrawal fork.
- **Convergence:** Yoruba soft determinism (destiny + effort) ↔ Stoic fate/effort ↔ Hindu karma-with-agency.
- **Contradiction to preserve (minority wisdom):** Ubuntu harmony-as-duty (Metz) vs Mboti's claim that "there is value in regarding a broken relationship as being authentically human as much as a harmonious relationship."

## F) Misattribution Watchlist
- **"It takes a village to raise a child"** — no verified single-people origin. The *Dictionary of Modern Proverbs* (Doyle, Shapiro & Mieder, Yale UP, 2012) states: "The saying is often referred to as an 'African' or a 'West African' proverb; however, no prototype from Africa has been discovered—though several sayings from that continent do urge cooperation in child rearing." NPR's "It Takes A Village To Determine The Origins Of An African Proverb" (*Goats and Soda*, July 30, 2016) reached the same conclusion. Popularised by Hillary Rodham Clinton, *It Takes a Village: And Other Lessons Children Teach Us* (1996); the title predates it in Jane Cowen-Fletcher's 1994 children's book *It Takes a Village*, and the Yale Book of Quotations traces the phrasing only to Toni Morrison (*Essence*, 1981: "You really need the whole village"). The Igbo *"Oran a azu nwa"* and Swahili/Kijita analogues are cited but unconfirmed as the source. **Mark specific-origin attribution: apocryphal.**
- **"If you want to go fast, go alone; if you want to go far, go together"** — per NPR (July 30, 2016), Sen. Cory Booker quoted this at the 2016 DNC as "an African saying"; Johnnetta Cole, director of the Smithsonian National Museum of African Art, said she "could not tell you which particular people the proverb is associated with." Untraceable to any people. **Mark: apocryphal.**
- **Generic "African proverb"** attributions — essentialist; almost always require a named people/language. **Mark: dubious by default.**
- **"I am because we are"** — often quoted as an ancient African proverb; it is **Mbiti's scholarly gloss** (1969). **Mark "traditional proverb" attribution: dubious; verified as Mbiti.**
- **Tempels' "vital force" as "the African philosophy"** — ethnophilosophical unanimism (Hountondji); **flag as colonial-era distortion, not a primary African voice.**
