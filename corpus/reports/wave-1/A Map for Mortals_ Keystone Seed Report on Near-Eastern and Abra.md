# A Map for Mortals — Keystone Seed Report: Near-Eastern & Abrahamic Wisdom

## Methodological preface
Every unit tags claim_type strictly. Religious/theological assertions (God's nature, the soul, the afterlife) are tagged **metaphysical** or **normative**, never empirical. "Verified" attribution means the quote was checked against a named primary/public-domain edition; "dubious"/"apocryphal" flag fidelity or authorship problems, and any unverified quote starts at "dubious." Convergence across traditions is treated as a robustness signal, **not** proof, and lineage/cross-pollination is flagged where known (Amenemope→Proverbs; Hellenism→Qoheleth; Neoplatonism→Augustine and Sufism). This is a representative keystone seed (~50 units across the four streams weighted toward the most robust, well-attested passages), not an exhaustive corpus.

## (c) DILEMMAS / FORKS

**FORK-1 — Justice vs Mercy.** Pole A (strict justice / measure-for-measure): Proverbs 22:22–23; "with what judgment ye judge, ye shall be judged" (Mt 7:2). Pole B (mercy that suspends the ledger): "Blessed are the merciful" (Mt 5:7); love of enemies (Mt 5:44). **resolution_pattern:** genuine tension; the corpus holds both — justice as the floor, mercy as the surplus — without collapsing one into the other.

**FORK-2 — The prosperity of the wicked / why the righteous suffer (theodicy).** Pole A (deferred justice / restored trust): Psalm 73 resolves only "in the sanctuary of God"; Job silenced by the whirlwind (Job 38); Ludlul's sufferer delivered by Marduk. Pole B (protest / no answer): Babylonian Theodicy ("the god does not impede the way of a devil"); Job's protest never receives a *reason*. **resolution_pattern: NO winner.** Across four cultures the question outlives every answer; contradiction is the data. Job's God gives awe, not explanation.

**FORK-3 — Vanity/impermanence vs meaning.** Pole A (all is vapor): Ecclesiastes 1:2; Gilgamesh's failed immortality quest. Pole B (meaning despite/through impermanence): eat-drink-find-joy (Eccl 2:24; Siduri); à Kempis redirects "vanity" toward love of God. **resolution_pattern:** the same observation (impermanence) yields *opposite* prescriptions (seize ordinary joy vs renounce the world); fork preserved.

**FORK-4 — Fear vs Love as motive.** Pole A (fear/reverence): "the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge" (Prov 1:7). Pole B (love): love of enemies (Mt 5); Sufi love-mysticism (Rumi, Attar); Augustine's restless heart drawn by love. **resolution_pattern:** developmental in some readings (fear as beginning, love as maturity), but traditions genuinely weight them differently — not dissolved.

**FORK-5 — Law vs Grace.** Pole A (law/observance): Pirkei Avot, Maimonides, the mitzvot. Pole B (grace/interior transformation): à Kempis ("without grace and the love of God"); the Sermon on the Mount intensifies inner intent over external act. **resolution_pattern:** signed disagreement between and within traditions; not resolved.

**FORK-6 — Submission/acceptance vs striving (tawakkul vs effort).** Pole A (acceptance): "whoever relies upon Allah… He is sufficient" [Carleton College](https://www.carleton.edu/chaplain/news/trust-in-god-and-tie-your-camel/) (Qur'an 65:3); "take no thought for the morrow" (Mt 6:34); Job 1:21. Pole B (effort): "Tie it and rely upon Allah" [sunnah](https://sunnah.com/tirmidhi:2517) (Tirmidhi 2517); "neither art thou free to desist" (Avot 2:16); Maimonides' *active* charity. **resolution_pattern:** the "tie your camel" hadith is itself the synthesis-attempt — means AND trust — but the weighting remains contested across and within traditions.

## (d) CANDIDATE CROSS-TRADITION CONVERGENCES (robustness, NOT proof)

**CONV-1 — "Eat, drink, find joy in your labour" as response to mortality.** Ecclesiastes 2:24 ↔ Gilgamesh/Siduri. *Caption:* 2 traditions, but Qoheleth shows probable Mesopotamian/Hellenistic influence and the motif circulates widely in ANE wisdom — convergence is partly **NOT independent**. Robustness: moderate, lineage-suspect.

**CONV-2 — The righteous sufferer / theodicy protest.** Job + Psalm 73 ↔ Ludlul bēl nēmeqi + Babylonian Theodicy. *Caption:* 2 cultures (Hebrew + Mesopotamian), spanning ~5–10 centuries, with plausible cultural contact along the Fertile Crescent (both Lambert and Kramer note the Mesopotamian texts predate Job). Robustness: **high** as a recurring human question; independence only partial (shared regional milieu).

**CONV-3 — The Golden-Rule / one-body solidarity.** Hillel ("what is hateful to you, do not do to others") ↔ Sermon on the Mount ↔ Saadi's *Bani Adam*. *Caption:* 3 traditions, little direct contact between Saadi (13th-c. Persia) and Hillel (1st-c. Judea), ~13 centuries apart. Robustness: **high — among the most genuinely independent convergences here.**

**CONV-4 — Humility about one's own knowledge / wisdom found in anyone.** Ptahhotep Maxim 1 ↔ Proverbs 16:18 ↔ à Kempis I.5 ("attend to what is said, not who said it"). *Caption:* 3 traditions across ~3,500 years; shared *genre* (didactic instruction literature) inflates apparent convergence. Robustness: moderate.

**CONV-5 — Self-knowledge → knowledge of the divine.** Al-Ghazali ("he who knows himself knows God") ↔ Augustine's interior turn. *Caption:* both draw on a shared **Neoplatonic (Plotinus) lineage** — this is cross-pollination, **NOT independent convergence**. Flagged explicitly; counts as evidence of shared philosophical descent, not parallel discovery.

---

## (e) CONTRADICTIONS / TENSIONS (signed, honest)

- **T-1 (intra-Hebrew):** Proverbs' confident retribution theology (the righteous prosper, the wicked fall) vs Ecclesiastes' and Job's denial that desert reliably maps to outcome. Open contradiction *inside one canon*.
- **T-2 (intra-Mesopotamian):** Ludlul's eventual restoration by Marduk vs the Babylonian Theodicy's unresolved protest. One affirms deliverance; the other leaves the wound open.
- **T-3 (cross-tradition):** Ecclesiastes/Gilgamesh "seize ordinary joy" vs à Kempis "contempt of the world." Same datum (impermanence), opposite life-prescription.
- **T-4 (motive):** Fear-of-the-LORD framing (Proverbs) vs love-as-everything (Sufi poets, Sermon on the Mount).
- **T-5 (agency):** Strong-providence / "take no thought" (Mt 6) vs "tie your camel" / "not free to desist" (Tirmidhi, Avot). Within Islam itself, the balance of *tawakkul* and means (*asbab*) is a live, signed debate.

---

## (f) VIRTUES cultivated / DANGERS guarded against

**Virtues:** humility (Ptahhotep, Proverbs, à Kempis); justice and care for the poor (Amenemope, Proverbs, Maimonides, Sirach); mercy and love incl. of enemies (Sermon on the Mount); fidelity in friendship (Sirach, Saadi); patience/acceptance in suffering (Job, Ludlul, *tawakkul*); self-knowledge (al-Ghazali, Augustine); diligence balanced with trust (Avot, the hadith); restraint of speech (Ptahhotep, Proverbs 15:1); contentment amid impermanence (Ecclesiastes, Siduri).

**Dangers:** pride/arrogance (Prov 16:18, Ptahhotep); greed and exploiting the weak (Amenemope, Proverbs, Sirach); hot temper and bad company (Amenemope ch. 10, Prov 22:24); envy (Psalm 73); hypocritical judging (Mt 7); attachment to perishable wealth/honour (Ecclesiastes, à Kempis); presumptuous certainty about divine justice (Job, the Theodicy); lying and betrayal of friends (Sirach 6).

## (g) MISATTRIBUTION WATCHLIST

- **"Out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field. I'll meet you there." (popular "Rumi")** — A Coleman Barks *interpretive "version,"* not a literal translation. Barks does not read Persian and has said: *"Of course, as I work on these poems, I don't have the Persian to consult. I literally have nothing to be faithful to, except what the scholars give"* (Ajam Media Collective, 9 March 2015). Persian scholar Christopher Shackle calls Barks's output *"not so much a paraphrase as a parody of the wondrously wrought style of the greatest master of Persian art-poetry."* The underlying Masnavi lines run closer to "Beyond Islam and unbelief there is a desert plain…" **Status: loosely rendered / popularly distorted.**
- **General "Rumi" internet quotes** (e.g., "Do not feel lonely, the entire universe is inside you"; "You are not a drop in the ocean…") — frequently **apocryphal or Barksian paraphrase**; verify against Nicholson (1926) or the PD Whinfield (1887). **Status: dubious→apocryphal** until matched to a primary edition.
- **"Even after all this time, the Sun never says to the Earth, 'You owe me.'" and "Fear is the cheapest room in the house." (attributed to Hafiz)** — **Inventions of Daniel Ladinsky.** Omid Safi (Director, Duke Islamic Studies Center) writes that *"about 99.9% of the quotes and poems attributed to Hafez… are fake,"* naming both of these as *"the pure inventions of Daniel Ladinsky"* (Al Jazeera, 14 June 2020). Ladinsky cannot read Persian and based his work loosely on Wilberforce-Clarke's 1891 *Divan*; scholar Aria Fani: *"Ladinsky does not know Persian while his poems bear little or no resemblance to what Hafez has composed."* Ladinsky himself wrote that Hafiz appeared to him *"as an Infinite Fountaining Sun… who sang hundreds of lines of his poetry to me in English,"* and in a 2013 interview said, *"Is it Hafez or Danny? I don't know. Does it really matter?"* **Status: apocryphal (Ladinsky originals).** For genuine Hafez, use Gertrude Bell (1897) or Wilberforce-Clarke (1891).
- **"Tie your camel and trust in God" framed as a Bedouin parable** — the authenticated text (Tirmidhi 2517) is a brief question-and-answer, not the popular untied-camel storyline; and its grading is genuinely contested (*gharib*/*munkar* on the standalone Anas chain; *hasan* with corroboration). **Status: meaning sound; popular narrative form is paraphrase; chain contested.**
- **Augustine "restless heart"** — genuine (Confessions 1.1.1), but the smooth modern English "our hearts are restless until they rest in You" is a paraphrase; the PD Pusey (1838) reads "our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee." [Christian Classics Ethereal Library](https://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/npnf101.vi.I_1.I.html) **Status: verified; watch translation drift.**
- **Proverbs 22:20 "thirty sayings"** — this rendering rests on Adolf Erman's emendation of the Hebrew (*shilshom* → *sheloshim*) to match Amenemope's thirty chapters; the Masoretic *ketiv* is disputed and several scholars (e.g. Whybray late in life, Shaw) reject the reconstruction. **Status: flag as scholarly conjecture, not settled text.**
- **"God helps those who help themselves" (widely assumed biblical)** — **NOT in the Bible.** The exact English wording originates with Algernon Sidney (*Discourses Concerning Government*, 1698) and was popularized by Franklin's *Poor Richard's Almanack* (1736); the sentiment traces to Aesop's "Hercules and the Waggoner" (Perry Index 291). A 2017 Barna Group survey found 52% of practicing Christians believe it is biblical. Frequently confused with the *tawakkul* "tie your camel" teaching. **Status: non-canonical; common misattribution.**

### Bottom line for the project
The corpus's deepest, most decision-relevant structure is **not** a list of agreed maxims but a set of **durable forks** — above all theodicy (FORK-2) and submission-vs-striving (FORK-6) — where honest traditions disagree and where the strongest move is to preserve the tension as data rather than crown a winner. Convergences are real but mostly **lineage-entangled** (CONV-1, CONV-5) or **genre-inflated** (CONV-4); the cleanest case of near-independent agreement is the one-body / golden-rule cluster (CONV-3). And the single largest data-integrity hazard for ingestion is the **Rumi/Hafiz pseudo-quotation ecosystem**, which should be quarantined behind the watchlist and never seeded as primary text.
