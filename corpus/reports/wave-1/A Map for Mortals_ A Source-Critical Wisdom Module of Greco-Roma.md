```yaml
- id: insight.flux.river
  canonical_claim: "Reality is process; you cannot step into the same river twice, because fresh waters are ever flowing."
  quote: "Upon those who step into the same rivers, different and different waters flow."
  citation:
    author: Heraclitus
    work: Fragments
    reference: DK 22 B12 (cf. B49a, B91); Plato attributes flux at Cratylus 402a
    translator: after Kirk & Raven (paraphrase of Greek; original PD)
    language: Greek
    tradition: Pre-Socratic
    era: c. 500 BCE
  register: aphorism
  claim_type: metaphysical
  polarity: descriptive
  conditionality: contested
  life_domains: [self, adversity, aging, death]
  life_stages: [adult, midlife, elder]
  attribution_confidence: verified
  note: "The popular 'you cannot step in the same river twice' is a compression; B12 is the securest fragment. Plato's flux reading (Cratylus 402a; Theaetetus) may overstate Heraclitus, who emphasizes stability-through-change."

- id: insight.character.fate
  canonical_claim: "Character is one's guiding spirit; what you habitually are shapes what befalls you."
  quote: "ἦθος ἀνθρώπῳ δαίμων (ethos anthropoi daimon) — usually rendered 'character is fate.'"
  citation:
    author: Heraclitus
    work: Fragments
    reference: DK 22 B119
    translator: traditional rendering; daimon left untranslated by Kirk & Raven
    language: Greek
    tradition: Pre-Socratic
    era: c. 500 BCE
  register: aphorism
  claim_type: observational
  polarity: descriptive
  conditionality: contested
  life_domains: [self, work, adversity]
  life_stages: [youth, young-adult, adult]
  attribution_confidence: verified
  note: "Fragment is genuine; the English 'character is fate' is an interpretive translation — daimon ('divinity/guiding spirit') is not simply 'fate.' Treat the English gloss as contested."
```

## CENTRAL DILEMMAS / FORKS

No fork has a single winner; each pole carries a price.

**FORK 1 — Virtue vs. Pleasure as THE good.**
- *Pole A (virtue is the/a sufficient good):* Stoics (virtue alone suffices for eudaimonia); Aristotle (virtue is the core, but needs some external goods). **Price:** demanding; risks denying the reality of loss and bodily need.
- *Pole B (pleasure is the end):* Epicurus (pleasure = absence of pain/ataraxia, a "katastematic" state); Cyrenaics (bodily, "kinetic" pleasure of the moment). **Price:** charged with making the good fragile/self-centred; the Cyrenaic version is vulnerable to the hedonic treadmill.
- *Conditions:* Aristotelian/Stoic poles suit those seeking meaning under adversity; the Epicurean pole suits those whose suffering is driven by needless desire and fear.

**FORK 2 — Extirpation vs. management of the passions.**
- *Pole A (Stoic apatheia):* eliminate destructive passions (pathē) via correct judgment. **Price:** can shade into emotional suppression; critics doubt full extirpation is possible.
- *Pole B (Epicurean minimization / Aristotelian moderation):* Aristotle wants the mean — right emotion, right amount, right time (NE II.6); Epicureans reduce disturbance by limiting desire. **Price:** less robust against extreme adversity than the Stoic ideal.

**FORK 3 — Fate vs. agency.**
- *Pole A (Stoic determinism + compatibilism):* all is providentially determined, yet assent is "up to us." **Price:** tension between determinism and responsibility (see contradictions).
- *Pole B (Epicurean swerve/clinamen):* the atomic swerve (Lucretius, DRN II.216–293) breaks strict causation to preserve free will. **Price:** the swerve is ad hoc, criticized since antiquity.
- *Pole C (tragic fate):* Sophocles/Homer — fate constrains, but character chooses within it. **Price:** limited consolation.

**FORK 4 — Civic engagement vs. withdrawal.**
- *Pole A (engagement):* Stoic cosmopolitan duty; Cicero's vita activa (De Officiis). **Price:** exposure to fortune, corruption, loss of tranquility.
- *Pole B (withdrawal):* Epicurean "live unnoticed" (lathe biōsas); Cynic rejection of convention (nomos). **Price:** charged with shirking social responsibility; freedom bought by marginality.

**FORK 5 — Endurance vs. avoidance of suffering.**
- *Pole A (Stoic endurance/apatheia):* meet hardship head-on; "it matters how you bear it" (Seneca, De Prov. 2.4). **Price:** can rationalize remaining in harmful situations.
- *Pole B (Epicurean avoidance):* arrange life to minimize pain and disturbance; withdraw from what hurts. **Price:** can shrink the scope of a life.

**FORK 6 — The examined life vs. unreflective living; reason vs. emotion; the golden mean vs. extremes.** These cut across schools: Socrates/Plato/Aristotle prize examination and reason; the tragedians and Cynics warn that reason has limits and convention deceives. The mean (Aristotle, Horace) vs. the deliberate extremism of the Cynics is itself a fork.

## CANDIDATE CROSS-TRADITION CONVERGENCES (to test later)

> *Convergence is evidence of ROBUSTNESS, not proof of truth. Each is flagged as a CANDIDATE for later testing.*

1. **Dichotomy of control** (Epictetus, Ench. 1) ↔ Serenity Prayer ↔ Bhagavad Gita's action-without-attachment (karma yoga). CANDIDATE: independent traditions converge on "act on what's yours, release the rest."
2. **"Count no man happy until dead"** (Solon/Sophocles) ↔ general fragility-of-fortune motif across cultures. CANDIDATE for the conditional nature of eudaimonia.
3. **Memento mori / death-acceptance** (Epicurus, Lucretius, Seneca, Marcus, Cicero via Plato) — strong *intra*-tradition convergence with *divergent* metaphysics (atomist vs. providential).
4. **The golden mean** (Aristotle, NE II.6; Horace's aurea mediocritas; Hesiod's "due measure") ↔ doctrines of moderation elsewhere. CANDIDATE.
5. **Tranquility as a by-product, not a target** (Pyrrhonist ataraxia "as a shadow follows the body"; Epicurean ataraxia) — convergence on the indirectness of peace.
6. **Self-sufficiency** (Cynic autarkeia; Stoic indifference to externals; Epicurean "what is good is easy to get") — rival schools converge that happiness should not be hostage to externals.

## INTERNAL CONTRADICTIONS (to test later)

> *Contradiction is data, not embarrassment.*

1. **Stoic determinism vs. moral responsibility** — if all is fated/providential, how is assent "up to us"? Chrysippus' compatibilist reply (the "cylinder" analogy: external push starts the roll, but the cylinder's own shape determines how it rolls) is contested.
2. **Aristotle (external goods needed) vs. Stoics (virtue alone suffices)** — direct conflict over whether eudaimonia is fortune-proof. NE I.8–10 vs. Stoic orthodoxy.
3. **Seneca's wealth vs. his Stoic counsel of indifference to riches** — the ancient hypocrisy charge. Publius Suillius Rufus's attack of AD 58, reported in Tacitus, *Annals* XIII.42–43, charged that within four years of Nero's favour Seneca had amassed *three hundred million sesterces* (*trecenties sestertium*) through high-interest loans across Italy and the provinces. Seneca's defence is the *De Vita Beata* (esp. chs. 17–18, 22–23: the wise man may possess wealth without being possessed by it). Test whether "indifference" coherently permits possessing wealth without attachment.
4. **Epicurean withdrawal ("live unnoticed") vs. Stoic/Ciceronian civic duty** — rival accounts of the good life's relation to politics.
5. **Epicurean atomic swerve vs. Epicurean physical determinism** — the clinamen is introduced precisely to break the determinism the physics otherwise implies; criticized as ad hoc.
6. **Skeptic epoché vs. the need to act** — how does one live/act while suspending judgment? Sextus' answer (follow appearances and custom) is contested.

## MISATTRIBUTION WATCHLIST

> *Verdicts: verified / probable / dubious / apocryphal. Online misattribution is rampant; each claim below was checked against primary editions and reputable quote-investigations.*

1. **"Waste no more time arguing what a good man should be. Be one."** — **VERIFIED.** Genuine Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* 10.16. The crisp wording is Maxwell Staniforth's (1964) translation; George Long (PD, 1862) renders it more literally ("No longer talk at all about the kind of man that a good man ought to be, but be such"). Use Long for public domain.
2. **"The best revenge is not to be like that / to be unlike him who performed the injury."** — **VERIFIED.** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* 6.6 (Long: "The best way of avenging thyself is not to become like the wrongdoer").
3. **"You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength."** — **APOCRYPHAL** as a verbatim quote. Appears in NO standard scholarly translation (Long, Hays, Farquharson, Haines, Staniforth, Hard, Hammond). It is a modern paraphrase of the Stoic dichotomy of control; the closest genuine passage is *Meditations* 4.3 (and the theme across Book 12), and the idea is more directly Epictetus (Enchiridion 1). **Verdict: apocryphal-as-quote / probable-as-paraphrase.**
4. **"Everything we hear is an opinion, not a fact. Everything we see is a perspective, not the truth."** — **APOCRYPHAL.** PolitiFact (26 Sept. 2019) rated it misattributed; Donald Robertson, author of *How to Think Like a Roman Emperor*, told the Daily Caller News Foundation by email, "That's a fake quote." It appears in no Greek text. The genuine kernel is the ὑπόληψις ("all is opinion") theme — Wikiquote traces it to *Meditations* 2.15, 12.8, 12.22, 12.26 (Farquharson: "Everything is what you suppose it to be") and 4.3. The fake also distorts Marcus' meaning — he does not deny external reality.
5. **"Luck is what happens when preparation meets opportunity."** — **APOCRYPHAL** (modern). No source in Seneca's corpus; Wikiquote lists it under Seneca as "Disputed" and notes the circulating Latin ("Fortuna est quae fit cum praeparatio in occasionem incidit") is a back-translation. The nearest genuine Seneca passage, *On Benefits* VII.1, quotes Demetrius the Cynic on the wrestler "who…watches keenly for an opportunity," but makes no mention of luck.
6. **"We suffer more in imagination than in reality."** — **VERIFIED** (as a close rendering). Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius* 13.4 (Gummere, PD: "we suffer more often in imagination than in reality").
7. **"It does not matter what you bear, but how you bear it."** — **VERIFIED**, but commonly **MISFILED.** From Seneca, *De Providentia* 2.4 ("non quid sed quemadmodum feras interest"), NOT the Letters to Lucilius.
8. **"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit."** — **MISATTRIBUTED.** These are Will Durant's words (*The Story of Philosophy*, 1926), summarizing Aristotle. The genuine Aristotelian passage is *Nicomachean Ethics* II.1 (1103a–b: virtue is formed by habit) and II.4. Credit Durant summarizing Aristotle.
9. **"Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom."** — **APOCRYPHAL / unverified** as Aristotle. No located passage in the Aristotelian corpus; circulates only via quote-aggregators (Goodreads, AZQuotes, BrainyQuote). The "know thyself" maxim is the Delphic γνῶθι σεαυτόν, not Aristotle.
10. **"Educating the mind without educating the heart is no education at all."** — **APOCRYPHAL / unverified** as Aristotle. No source in the corpus; widely flagged as misattributed.
11. **"The only true wisdom is in knowing you know nothing."** — **APOCRYPHAL** as a verbatim Socrates/Plato quote. A loose compression; the genuine basis is Plato, *Apology* 21d (Socrates is wiser only in not thinking he knows what he does not).
12. **"Be kind, for everyone you meet is fighting a hard battle."** — **MISATTRIBUTED** to Plato/Socrates/Philo. Actually Ian Maclaren (pen name of Rev. John Watson), 1897 *British Weekly* Christmas message; original wording: "Be pitiful, for every man is fighting a hard battle" (Quote Investigator; Wikipedia).
13. **"The unexamined life is not worth living."** — **VERIFIED.** Socrates via Plato, *Apology* 38a (Greek: ὁ δὲ ἀνεξέταστος βίος οὐ βιωτὸς ἀνθρώπῳ). Genuine; Jowett (PD) and Fowler Loeb both attest, and Perseus confirms the Stephanus location.

## CAVEATS

- **The "Socratic problem":** we access Socrates only through Plato, Xenophon, and others; the historical Socrates' own views cannot be cleanly separated from Plato's literary Socrates. Treat all "Socrates" units as *Socrates-via-Plato*.
- **Fragmentary transmission:** many Pre-Socratic, Cynic, and early Stoic (Zeno, Chrysippus) teachings survive only as fragments and doxography (Diogenes Laertius, Stobaeus). Anecdotes (e.g., Diogenes and Alexander) are illustrative, not documentary, and several have no contemporary attestation.
- **Convergence ≠ truth.** Cross-tradition agreement is logged as a robustness signal only.
- **Claim types are kept distinct:** e.g., "death is nothing to us" is *metaphysical* (it rests on atomism and the mortality of the soul), whereas "we suffer more in imagination" is *observational/psychological*. The graph should not let one wear another's clothes.
- **Translation drift:** famous "quotes" are often a particular translator's phrasing (e.g., Staniforth's "Waste no more time…"). Where the punchy English is a modern rendering, the YAML `note` field records the more literal PD alternative.
- **Dating is approximate** for several works (Meditations, the Epicurean corpus, Aesop's collection); eras are given as scholarly consensus ranges, not certainties.
