# The Empirical Layer of Human Flourishing: A Graph-Ingestible Synthesis

## Methodology & Evidence-Grading Rubric

This report synthesizes the best available evidence on what correlates with — and where defensible, causally contributes to — a life going well. It is the EMPIRICAL LAYER only; every finding is `claim_type = empirical`. Prudential ("wisdom") claims are kept strictly distinct and appear only in the mapping table as targets that evidence SUPPORTS, QUALIFIES, or CONTRADICTS.

**Evidence grades:**
- **A** — Converging meta-analyses / multiple RCTs / large preregistered multi-lab replications. High confidence in the *existence* of the association; causal claims still depend on design.
- **B** — Single RCT or strong prospective longitudinal cohort with some replication.
- **C** — Consistent observational/longitudinal evidence but causally ambiguous (confounding plausible).
- **D** — Cross-sectional/correlational or mixed evidence.
- **E** — Contested, failed-to-replicate, or likely inflated by publication bias / p-hacking / weak controls.

**Cross-cutting caveats applied throughout:** (1) Most well-being research draws on WEIRD samples (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic), limiting generalizability. (2) Self-report dominates and is vulnerable to reporting biases. (3) Effects often shrink under preregistration, larger samples, active control conditions, and SES controls. (4) Correlation ≠ causation; reverse causation (e.g., healthy people are more social) is pervasive.

---

## TL;DR

- **The single most robust, decision-relevant finding is that the quantity and quality of social relationships predict longevity and health at a magnitude comparable to quitting smoking** (meta-analytic OR ≈ 1.5 for survival), but the evidence is observational and the famous Harvard Study is a narrow, non-representative cohort — so "invest in relationships" is strongly *supported* as a correlate while strict causality remains *qualified*.
- **Money matters more than the "$75k plateau" cliché implied:** the 2023 Kahneman–Killingsworth–Mellers adversarial collaboration found experienced well-being keeps rising with log-income for most people, flattening above ~$100,000 only for roughly the unhappiest 15–20% — so "money can't buy happiness" is *contradicted as stated* and *qualified* into "money reliably buys less misery, with diminishing proportional returns."
- **Several celebrated "self-help" effects are weak, contested, or failed to replicate** — ego depletion (failed multi-lab replication), the marshmallow test (attenuates ~two-thirds after background controls), gratitude interventions (small effects vs. active controls), and the Fredrickson–Losada "positivity ratio" (mathematically debunked and partially retracted) — so the prudential claims they were used to justify must be downgraded accordingly.

---

## Key Findings

The strongest, most decision-relevant evidence concerns **social connection** (large, consistent, causally suggestive associations with mortality), **money** (now reconciled and pointing to a roughly log-linear relationship), **health behaviours** (exercise as moderate antidepressant; sleep loss reliably worsens mood), and **trait self-control** (robust longitudinal prediction). Evidence is weaker and more cautionary for **positive-psychology interventions** (small effects, publication bias), **post-traumatic growth** (much reported growth appears illusory), and the **U-curve of ageing** (real on average but possibly disappearing as youth well-being declines).

## Details

### Focus Area 1 — Relationships & Social Connection

**1A. Social relationships and mortality (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010).** A meta-analysis of 148 prospective studies (308,849 participants) found that stronger social relationships were associated with a 50% increased likelihood of survival (random-effects weighted OR = 1.50, 95% CI 1.42–1.59). The association was strongest for complex measures of social integration (OR = 1.91, 95% CI 1.63–2.23) and weakest for binary "living alone" indicators (OR = 1.19, 95% CI 0.99–1.44). In the authors' own words: *"Individuals with adequate social relationships have a 50% greater likelihood of survival compared to those with poor or insufficient social relationships. The magnitude of this effect is comparable with stopping smoking and exceeds many well known risk factors for mortality such as obesity and physical inactivity."* *Evidence: meta-analysis of prospective cohorts (Grade C — strong and consistent but observational; reverse causation and confounding by baseline health remain).*

**1B. Loneliness, social isolation, living alone (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015).** A second meta-analysis (70 studies) found, after controlling for confounds, weighted average increased mortality likelihood of 29% for social isolation (OR = 1.29), 26% for loneliness (OR = 1.26), and 32% for living alone (OR = 1.32). Effects were stronger in samples younger than 65. *Evidence: meta-analysis (Grade C).*

**1C. The Harvard Study of Adult Development (Waldinger & Schulz, *The Good Life*, 2023).** The longest-running longitudinal study of adult life (begun 1938) reports that relationship satisfaction at age 50 predicted physical health at 80 better than cholesterol did, and that the people who "leaned into relationships" fared best. *Evidence: longitudinal observational cohort, popularized in a trade book (Grade C–D). Major limits: the original cohort was 724 men (Harvard sophomores plus a Boston inner-city sample); women were excluded; the sample is narrow, era-bound, and non-representative; the headline claims appear largely in a popular book and TED talks rather than as a single definitive peer-reviewed effect size. Treat as hypothesis-consistent illustration, not decisive proof.*

### Focus Area 2 — Money and Well-being

**2A. Kahneman & Deaton (2010) — the "$75k plateau."** Using >450,000 US survey responses, found that *experienced* emotional well-being rose with income only up to ~$75,000/year then plateaued, while *evaluative* life satisfaction kept rising. *Evidence: large cross-sectional survey (Grade C/D).*

**2B. Killingsworth (2021).** Using experience-sampling (the Track Your Happiness app), found experienced well-being rose steadily with log-income with no plateau, even above $75k. *Evidence: large experience-sampling cross-sectional (Grade C/D).*

**2C. The reconciliation — Killingsworth, Kahneman & Mellers (2023, PNAS), an adversarial collaboration.** Reanalysis showed both were partly right about *different people*. The flattening was confined to roughly the unhappiest 15–20%: per the authors, the 15th percentile *"rises relatively steeply below $100K but barely changes above $100K, consistent with the flattening pattern,"* whereas *"happiness rises significantly with Log(income) in all the [other] quantile regressions."* The original "plateau" reflected Kahneman & Deaton's measure hitting a ceiling and effectively measuring *un*happiness. *Evidence: adversarial-collaboration reanalysis (Grade B for the reconciliation; among the more credible work in the field). Caveat: Rohrer & Wenz (2024, PNAS, "Inappropriate causal assumptions underlie Killingsworth, Kahneman, and Mellers' conclusions") argue beyond $100K lower quantiles do not rise significantly with log-income (a widening inequality), and that the $100,000 threshold "was not estimated in Killingsworth's data but was based on Kahneman and Deaton." An arXiv reanalysis suggests an optimally chosen threshold yields flat well-being above ~$200,000. The relationship is log-linear — diminishing absolute returns.*

**2D. Easterlin paradox vs. Stevenson & Wolfers.** Easterlin (1974; updated 2010, PNAS): within a country, richer people are happier than poorer, but national average happiness does not rise as GDP grows over the long run — implying relative income matters. Stevenson & Wolfers (2008, *Brookings Papers*) counter that across and within countries, subjective well-being rises approximately log-linearly with GDP per capita with no satiation point. [IDEAS/RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/p/iza/izadps/dp3654.html) Debate unresolved. *Evidence: contested macro time-series (Grade D/E for any strong claim either way).*

**2E. Spending on others (Dunn, Aknin & Norton, 2008, *Science*).** The original experiment (N = 46) found people randomly assigned to spend a windfall on others were happier than those spending on themselves. *Replication status (Grade E/D): a registered replication report (Aknin et al., 2020) and a close replication (N = 133, 2022) failed to find a significant difference on broad well-being measures, though narrower happiness measures showed effects in the original direction.* The robust effect is much weaker than the famous original implied.

### Focus Area 3 — Habits, Self-Control, and Goal Pursuit

**3A. Trait self-control and life outcomes (Moffitt et al., 2011, PNAS — Dunedin Study).** Following 1,037 children to age 32, childhood self-control predicted adult physical health, substance dependence, personal finances, and criminal conviction along a gradient, [PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/abs/10.1073/pnas.1010076108) independent of IQ and social class. A sibling-comparison design (509 same-sex dizygotic pairs) confirmed the sibling with poorer self-control had worse outcomes. [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/49781540_A_Gradient_of_Childhood_Self-Control_Predicts_Health_Wealth_and_Public_Safety) *Evidence: large prospective cohort + sibling fixed-effects (Grade B/C — strong design, causally suggestive but still observational; trait self-control correlates with SES and other confounds).*

**3B. Ego depletion — FAILED REPLICATION (foregrounded).** The "willpower as a depletable resource" model (Baumeister et al., 1998), with an early meta-analytic d ≈ 0.6 (Hagger et al., 2010), collapsed under scrutiny. A preregistered multi-lab replication (Hagger & Chatzisarantis, 2016; 23 labs, N = 2,141) found essentially no effect, despite 22 of 23 labs predicting success. [Hogrefe](https://econtent.hogrefe.com/doi/10.1027/1864-9335/a000398) Carter & McCullough (2015) found the original meta-analysis was inflated by small-study/publication bias, with a probable true effect near zero. [Statistical Modeling](https://statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/Hagger_0-407863.pdf) *Evidence: large preregistered multi-lab replication (Grade E — likely not a real effect as classically operationalized).*

**3C. Habit formation — the "66 days" figure (Lally et al., 2010).** Tracking 96 volunteers (82 analysable; model fit for 62), the median time to reach 95% of automaticity was 66 days, but per the authors *"the time it took participants to reach 95% of their asymptote of automaticity ranged from 18 to 254 days."* A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis (Singh et al., 20 studies, 2,601 participants) reported medians of 59–66 days and means of 106–154 days, with individual variability of 4–335 days. [nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11641623/) *Evidence: small original observational study; meta-analytic synthesis (Grade C). Caveat: "66 days" is a much-abused average; the real finding is high variability. The original has not been directly replicated with its exact procedure.* [Peercommunityin](https://rr.peercommunityin.org/articles/rec?id=210)

**3D. Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006).** Meta-analysis of 94 independent tests (>8,000 participants) found "if-then" plans had a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4500900/) (d = 0.65, 95% CI 0.60–0.70). [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8149892/) *Evidence: meta-analysis (Grade B/A). Caveat: most studies compared to goal-intention controls; [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4500900/) later health-behaviour meta-analyses report smaller effects (d ≈ 0.14–0.37 for objectively assessed behaviour).* [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8149892/)

**3E. The marshmallow test attenuates (Watts, Duncan & Quan, 2018).** A conceptual replication (N ≈ 900, far larger and more diverse than Mischel's original) found that among children of mothers without college degrees, each additional minute waited at age 4 predicted only ~0.1 SD gain in age-15 achievement — about half the original bivariate correlation, and reduced by roughly two-thirds once family background, early cognitive ability, and home environment were controlled. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797618761661) Most predictive value came from waiting at least ~20 seconds. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797618761661) *Evidence: large conceptual replication (Grade B for the attenuation finding). Caveat: a later analysis (Sperber et al., 2024) found delay of gratification did not reliably predict adult functioning; [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31850833/) defenders note censoring may bias correlations toward zero.*

### Focus Area 4 — Meaning and Purpose vs. Momentary Happiness

**4A. Purpose in life and mortality (Alimujiang et al., 2019, JAMA Network Open).** In 6,985 US adults >50 (Health and Retirement Study), [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6632139/) those in the lowest life-purpose category had roughly double the all-cause mortality of those in the highest, after adjusting for sociodemographics, health behaviours, and baseline health. Replicated by Hill & Turiano (2014) and Kim/Shiba programs. *Evidence: prospective cohort (Grade C — robust association across cohorts, but purpose correlates with SES, health, and social ties; an E-value analysis suggests an unmeasured confounder would need RR ≈ 3 on both sides to nullify).* [Space Daily](https://spacedaily.com/d-older-adults-with-the-highest-sense-of-purpose-in-life-had-a-46-percent-lower-mortality-risk-over-four-years-compared-to-those-with-the-lowest-scores-according-to-a-2022-study-by-harvards-e/)

**4B. Happiness vs. meaning are distinct (Baumeister, Vohs, Aaker & Garbinsky, 2013, *J. Positive Psychology*).** In a national online survey (N = 397), satisfying needs/wants boosted happiness but was largely irrelevant to meaning; [Carlson School of Management](https://carlsonschool.umn.edu/sites/carlsonschool.umn.edu/files/2019-04/baumeister_vohs_et_al_jopp_meaning_hps_online2013_0.pdf) being a "giver" (vs. "taker"), integrating past/present/future, and self-expression were tied to meaning but not happiness; higher stress/worry went with more meaning but less happiness. [scottbarrykaufman](https://scottbarrykaufman.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/Baumeister-et-al.-2013.pdf) *Evidence: cross-sectional correlational (Grade D). Caveat: happiness and meaning measures overlap substantially (the authors describe roughly half-shared variance; an independent UK Biobank estimate puts the phenotypic correlation at r ≈ 0.53); the study is partly exploratory and self-defined.*

### Focus Area 5 — Gratitude, Savouring, and Positive-Psychology Interventions

**5A. Gratitude interventions have small effects vs. active controls (Cregg & Cheavens, 2021).** Meta-analysis (27 studies, N = 3,675) [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10902-020-00236-6) found gratitude interventions reduced depression and anxiety symptoms only modestly (g ≈ 0.27 for depression), with larger effects against *waitlist* than *active* controls; the authors recommend people seeking symptom relief use interventions with stronger evidence. [Johnsommersflanagan](https://johnsommersflanagan.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/gratitude-2021-meta-analysis.pdf) Davis et al. (2016) similarly found limited effects and urged that "enthusiasm…should be tempered." [Taylor & Francis Online](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/08870446.2024.2336042) *Evidence: converging meta-analyses (Grade D/E for clinically meaningful effects; small and control-dependent).*

**5B. Positive-psychology interventions (PPIs) are smaller than first reported (White, Uttl & Holder, 2019, PLOS ONE).** Reanalyzing the two most-cited PPI meta-analyses (Sin & Lyubomirsky 2009; Bolier et al. 2013) and correcting for small-sample bias, the effect on well-being shrank to about r = 0.10, and effects on depression became variable and often non-significant. *Evidence: meta-analytic reanalysis correcting bias (Grade E for the larger early claims; small but possibly real effects remain).*

**5C. The Fredrickson–Losada "positivity ratio" debacle (cautionary tale).** Fredrickson & Losada (2005) claimed a critical 2.9013:1 positive-to-negative affect ratio separating "flourishing" from "languishing." Brown, Sokal & Friedman (2013) showed the underlying nonlinear-dynamics mathematics was nonsense; *American Psychologist* partially retracted the paper, withdrawing the modeling and the specific ratios. *Evidence: debunked/partially retracted (Grade E). Lesson: superficially precise quantitative claims in positive psychology warrant scrutiny.*

### Focus Area 6 — Adversity, Resilience, and Post-Traumatic Growth (treated skeptically)

**6A. Resilience is the most common trajectory (Bonanno; Galatzer-Levy, Huang & Bonanno, 2018, *Clinical Psychology Review*).** A statistical review of 54 studies found *"the resilience trajectory was the modal response across studies (average of 65.7% across populations, 95% CI [0.616, 0.698]), followed in prevalence by recovery (20.8%), chronicity (10.6%), and delayed onset (8.9%)."* In other words, after adverse events roughly two-thirds of people maintain stable healthy functioning. *Evidence: statistical review of longitudinal trajectory studies (Grade B/C).*

**6B. Post-traumatic GROWTH may be largely illusory (Frazier et al., 2009, *Psychological Science*).** In a prospective study (~1,500 students, with 122 experiencing trauma between waves), retrospective reports of growth (the PTGI) did *not* correspond well to actual pre-to-post change in well-being; perceived growth was associated with *increased* distress while actual growth was associated with *decreased* distress. A meta-analysis (Gower et al., 2022) attributes the mismatch to cognitive biases. *Evidence: prospective longitudinal + meta-analysis (Grade B/C — the measurement critique is strong). Genuine resilience and illusory "growth" must be distinguished.*

**6C. Moderate adversity beats both none and extreme (Seery, Holman & Silver, 2010, JPSP).** In a multi-year national longitudinal sample, a U-shaped relationship emerged: people with *some* lifetime adversity reported lower distress, lower functional impairment, fewer PTSD symptoms, and higher life satisfaction than those with either *high* or *zero* adversity, and were less affected by recent adverse events. *Evidence: longitudinal national sample + lab studies (Grade C). "What doesn't kill you" holds only in moderation; this is a dose-response curve, not a blanket endorsement of suffering.*

### Focus Area 7 — Health Behaviours: Exercise, Sleep, Mood

**7A. Exercise reduces depression — moderate effect, bias concerns (Singh et al., 2023, *British Journal of Sports Medicine*).** An umbrella review (97 systematic reviews, 1,039 trials, 128,119 participants) found physical activity reduced depression, anxiety, and distress, with effects "comparable to or slightly greater than" psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy. A 2025 meta-meta-analysis (81 meta-analyses, 1,079 studies, 79,551 participants) reported SMD ≈ −0.61 (95% CI −0.69 to −0.54) for depression and −0.47 for anxiety. *Evidence: umbrella reviews of RCTs (Grade A for an effect existing; Grade B/C for magnitude). Crucial caveat: when analyses are restricted to low-risk-of-bias RCTs (Cooney et al., 2013 Cochrane; Krogh et al., 2017), the antidepressant effect shrinks substantially or becomes non-significant. Reverse causation (depression reduces activity) and self-report inflate observational estimates.*

**7B. Sleep loss reliably worsens mood and emotion regulation (Tomaso, Johnson & Nelson, 2021, *Sleep*).** Three meta-analyses (64 studies, 241 effect sizes) found sleep deprivation/restriction produced a large negative effect on positive mood, a moderate increase in negative mood (g = 0.45), a small impairment of adaptive emotion regulation (g = −0.32), and a small negative effect on emotional processing (g = −0.11 after publication-bias correction). [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33367799/) A 2024 *Psychological Bulletin* meta-analysis (154 studies, 5,717 participants) corroborated reduced positive affect and increased anxiety. [APA](https://www.apa.org/pubs/journals/releases/bul-bul0000410.pdf) *Evidence: converging meta-analyses (Grade A).*

**7C. Mechanism (Yoo, Gujar, Hu, Jolesz & Walker, 2007, *Current Biology*).** After one night of sleep deprivation (N = 26), per the authors, *"those in the sleep-deprivation condition exhibited a remarkable +60% greater magnitude of amygdala activation, relative to the control group (averaged left and right amygdala, t(24)=3.2, P=0.004)"* in response to aversive images, with a roughly threefold increase in the volume of amygdala activated and a loss of functional connectivity between the amygdala and medial prefrontal cortex. *Evidence: small experimental fMRI (Grade C/D — small N, suggestive mechanism).*

### Focus Area 8 — Ageing and Life Satisfaction Over the Lifespan

**8A. The U-curve of well-being — real on average but contested (Blanchflower vs. critics).** Blanchflower (2021) reports U-shapes (well-being high in youth, dipping to a midlife low ~mid-40s to 50s, then rising) in hundreds of datasets. Critics (Galambos et al., 2020; Bartram 2023) argue the U is an artifact of controls and is not robust or universal; Blanchflower & Graham (2021) rebut, claiming 618 studies find U-shapes. *Evidence: contested cross-sectional and panel data (Grade D — direction depends on controls and specification).*

**8B. The U may be flattening as youth well-being declines (Blanchflower, Bryson & Xu, 2024/2025).** Recent analyses (US BRFSS 2009–2022; international data) report that the deterioration of young people's mental health since ~2012–2013 has flattened or eliminated the U-shape, replacing the midlife "hump" in ill-being with a monotonic decline in ill-being by age across many countries. [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-Declining-Mental-Health-Of-The-Young-And-The-Of-Blanchflower-Bryson/0a34763917fc4256cb4e1861750f902375970245) Smartphones/social media are the leading proposed driver. *Evidence: repeated cross-sections, some in working-paper form (Grade C/D — robust trend, contested cause).*

**8C. Socioemotional selectivity & the positivity effect (Carstensen et al., 2011, *Psychology and Aging*).** A 10-year experience-sampling study (N ≈ 184, sampled 5×/day for a week in three waves) found the ratio of positive to negative emotional experience improved with age, with greater emotional stability and complexity, [UCLA Anderson School of Management](https://www.anderson.ucla.edu/faculty/hal.hershfield/resources/Research/Psychology-and-Aging-2011-Carstensen.pdf) robust to personality and health controls; more positive everyday emotion predicted lower mortality over ~13 years. [PubMed Central](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3332527/) *Evidence: longitudinal experience-sampling (Grade B/C). Caveat: cohort vs. age effects hard to separate.*

**8D. Terminal decline (Gerstorf et al., 2008, *Developmental Psychology*).** In 22-year SOEP data (N = 1,637 deceased adults aged 70–100), life satisfaction tracked distance-to-death more than chronological age, with a transition point ~4 years before death where decline roughly tripled. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18605841/?dopt=Abstract) *Evidence: large longitudinal panel (Grade B/C).*

## What We Do Not Know / Contested

1. **Causal direction of social connection and health.** The mortality associations are large and consistent, but no RCT can randomize lifelong relationships; reverse causation (healthier, wealthier people sustain relationships) and confounding by SES are not fully excluded.
2. **How much money matters, and for whom.** The 2023 reconciliation is elegant but its causal assumptions are challenged (Rohrer & Wenz, 2024), and where exactly returns flatten (~$100k for the unhappy minority? ~$200k overall?) is unsettled. The Easterlin paradox (relative vs. absolute income) remains genuinely unresolved.
3. **Whether positive-psychology interventions beat active controls.** Effects shrink toward r ≈ 0.10 once small-sample bias and active controls are accounted for; gratitude effects are control-dependent. Clinical usefulness vs. placebo is unclear.
4. **Whether post-traumatic growth is real.** Retrospective PTG scores correlate poorly with prospectively measured change and may reflect cognitive bias and coping rather than actual growth.
5. **Whether the U-curve of ageing is real, artifactual, or disappearing.** Specification-dependent; recent youth declines may be reshaping the age–well-being relationship.
6. **Cross-cultural non-generalizability.** The evidence base is overwhelmingly WEIRD; gratitude, money, purpose, and ageing effects may differ across cultures.
7. **Marriage and adaptation.** Lucas et al. (2003), analyzing >24,000 people in the German panel, found that on average people adapt back toward baseline life satisfaction [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12635914/) within ~2 years of marriage, with at most a small lasting boost and large individual differences — qualifying claims that marriage durably raises happiness.

## Recommendations (staged, with thresholds that would change them)

**Tier 1 — Act on now (strongest, most decision-relevant evidence):**
- **Prioritize social connection.** The mortality evidence is the most robust in this report. Even acknowledging confounding, the downside risk of investing in relationships is negligible. *Threshold to revise:* a large quasi-experimental or instrumental-variable study showing the association is mostly confounded by baseline health/SES would downgrade this.
- **Exercise for mood.** Use physical activity as an adjunct for low mood/mild-moderate depression; effects are at least moderate and the risk profile is favorable, *while noting* low-risk-of-bias RCTs show smaller effects. *Threshold:* if new low-bias RCTs converge near null, downgrade to "general health" rationale only.
- **Protect sleep.** Sleep loss reliably degrades mood and emotion regulation (Grade A). Treat adequate sleep as foundational.

**Tier 2 — Use with calibrated expectations:**
- **Build habits via implementation intentions** (if-then plans; d ≈ 0.65) and expect formation to take ~2 months but vary enormously (weeks to most of a year). Do not anchor on "21 days" or rigidly on "66 days."
- **Cultivate purpose/meaning**, which predicts lower mortality and is distinct from momentary pleasure — but recognize the evidence is observational and meaning overlaps heavily with happiness.
- **Spend on experiences and others** as a mild nudge, not a reliable lever — the prosocial-spending effect is weaker than popularized.

**Tier 3 — Low priority / manage expectations:**
- **Gratitude journals and PPIs** may yield small benefits but should not displace evidence-based treatment for depression/anxiety. Treat as low-cost supplements, not cures.
- **Do not romanticize adversity.** Moderate past adversity may build resilience, but extreme adversity harms, and "post-traumatic growth" self-reports may be illusory. Never prescribe suffering.

**Money:** Where feasible, higher income tends to buy lower misery and modestly higher well-being (diminishing proportional returns); it matters most for the least happy and the financially strained. Beyond comfortable sufficiency, additional income is a weak well-being lever relative to relationships, health, and sleep.

## Caveats

This synthesis is constrained by the dominance of WEIRD samples, near-ubiquitous self-report, and the observational nature of the strongest findings (relationships, purpose). Where famous claims rest on small or biased literatures (ego depletion, prosocial spending, gratitude, the positivity ratio, post-traumatic growth), I have graded them down and said so plainly. Effect sizes frequently shrink under preregistration, larger samples, active controls, and SES adjustment — a pattern that recurs across nearly every focus area. Several key sources are trade books (Waldinger & Schulz) or working papers (some Blanchflower analyses) rather than definitive peer-reviewed effect sizes, and are flagged accordingly. Nothing here is "scientifically proven"; these are associations of varying strength and design, and the prudential layer must weigh them as such.
