# A Map for Mortals — Research Report: The Moralists & Modern Philosophy

## (A) Orienting Header — Governing Principles
This report maps life-wisdom from the moralist/essayist tradition and modern Western philosophy as recurring forks inside a versioned property graph that feeds a book, website, and life-simulation game. It holds throughout to: **truth above all** (never reasoning backward from a desired conclusion); **wisdom as conditional and probabilistic** (tendencies and risks, not destinies); **recurrence ≠ proof**, while convergence across independent traditions = robustness, not truth; **strict separation of claim types** (empirical / normative / prudential / metaphysical / observational); **verification of every quote against primary editions**, with dubious or apocryphal items flagged honestly and nothing added to a thinker's mouth; **dissent and contradiction preserved as data**, not embarrassment; **preference for public-domain sources** with translator and edition/year credited; and **explicit copyright flags** on 20th-century figures and modern translations (including the standard Kierkegaard, Weil, Arendt, Camus, Sartre, and modern Montaigne/Pascal/Spinoza translations).

## (D) CENTRAL DILEMMAS / FORKS

1. **Reason vs. Passion.** *Passion/feeling pole:* Hume (passion sets ends; "reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions"), Vauvenargues ("les grandes pensées viennent du cœur"), Pascal (the heart's reasons). *Reason pole:* Spinoza, Kant (reason/duty governs). *Complicates:* Smith — sentiment is primary but disciplined by the impartial spectator's reason. **Conditional:** the fork concerns which faculty sets *ends* vs. selects *means*; almost no one denies reason any role.

2. **Self-interest vs. Benevolence.** *Self-interest as explanation:* La Rochefoucauld (amour-propre underlies apparent virtue), Bacon (prudence). *Genuine other-regard:* Smith (sympathy + impartial spectator), Mill (general happiness), Kant (duty). **Conditional:** La Rochefoucauld's claim is descriptive/observational, not normative — and he does not deny real virtue's possibility (Maxime 376: "L'envie est détruite par la véritable amitié").

3. **Authenticity vs. Social Convention.** *Authenticity pole:* Emerson, Thoreau, Schopenhauer (solitude preserves the self), Sartre (existential authenticity). *Convention/social pole:* Smith (society educates conscience), La Bruyère (the social comedy as data to be observed). **Conditional:** Smith and Schopenhauer are not simple opposites — both prize an inner judge but locate its *training* differently (strangers vs. solitude).

4. **Pessimism vs. Affirmation.** *Pessimist pole:* Schopenhauer (pain and boredom as the two foes of happiness), Chamfort. *Affirmation pole:* Spinoza (joy/amor of life), Emerson, Camus (affirm despite absurdity), Russell (cultivable happiness). **Conditional:** Camus and Spinoza affirm *without* denying suffering — affirmation is a stance, not factual optimism.

5. **Duty vs. Happiness.** Kant (duty even against inclination) vs. Mill (happiness as the criterion) and Russell. Spinoza dissolves the fork (blessedness = virtue itself). **Conditional:** Mill's "higher pleasures" smuggle duty-like rankings into the concept of happiness, blurring the divide.

6. **Freedom vs. Determinism.** *Determinist/compatibilist pole:* Spinoza (freedom = understanding necessity), Hume (compatibilism). *Radical-freedom pole:* Sartre ("condemned to be free"), Kant (noumenal autonomy). **Conditional:** Spinoza and Sartre both *redefine* "freedom" rather than asserting libertarian free will, so the contradiction is partly terminological.

7. **Solitude vs. Society.** *Solitude pole:* Montaigne, Thoreau, Schopenhauer; Pascal (our inability to sit alone diagnoses our misery). *Society pole:* Smith (strangers discipline conscience), Aristotle (man as political animal). Schopenhauer's porcupine parable is the explicit model of an *optimal middle distance*. **Conditional:** nearly every thinker endorses some oscillation; the fork is about default weighting.

## (E) VIRTUES CULTIVATED / DANGERS GUARDED AGAINST

**Virtues cultivated (mapped to thinkers):** self-knowledge (Montaigne; Socratic echo) · self-trust / nonconformity (Emerson, Thoreau) · self-command and prudence (Smith, Bacon) · equanimity before death (Spinoza, Montaigne, La Rochefoucauld) · attention and outward-directed interest (Weil, Russell) · patience and hope (Vauvenargues) · simplicity (Thoreau) · duty and autonomy (Kant) · life-affirmation (Spinoza, Camus, Emerson) · understanding rather than condemning (Spinoza).

**Dangers guarded against (mapped to thinkers):** self-deception via amour-propre (La Rochefoucauld) · diversion/distraction from the essential (Pascal) · conformity and the "joint-stock company" of society (Emerson, Schopenhauer) · catastrophizing over imagined evils (Seneca; pseudo-Montaigne) · deferral of living to the future (Pascal) · vanity and social masks (La Bruyère, Chamfort) · thoughtless complicity in evil (Arendt) · enslavement to lower pleasures and intoxication-as-escape (Mill, Russell).

## (F) CANDIDATE CONVERGENCES & CONTRADICTIONS (incl. ancient & Eastern)

**Strong cross-tradition convergences (independent origin → robustness signal, NOT proof):**
- *Imagined suffering exceeds real suffering:* pseudo-Montaigne ≈ **Seneca**, *Moral Epistles* 13.4 ("we suffer more often in imagination than in reality") ≈ Buddhist analysis of suffering as mental proliferation (*papañca*). Independent origin → robust.
- *Equanimity before death:* Spinoza (E4p67) ≈ Montaigne ("To philosophize is to learn to die," I.20) ≈ Stoics (Seneca, Marcus Aurelius) ≈ Epicurus ("death is nothing to us"). **Internal tension within the convergence:** Spinoza *inverts* the Stoic/Montaigne emphasis — the free man meditates on *life*, not death.
- *Happiness from within, not possessions:* Schopenhauer ("what one is") ≈ Stoic "the good is within" ≈ Epicurus (simple pleasures) ≈ Buddhist non-attachment ≈ Emerson/Thoreau. Robust.
- *Outward-directed interest cures self-absorption:* Russell ≈ Confucian relational ethics ≈ Smith's sympathy.
- *The optimal middle distance in relationships:* Schopenhauer's porcupines ≈ Confucian propriety (*li*) ≈ Aristotle's proportionate friendship.
- *Reason governing passion:* Spinoza/Kant ≈ Stoic *hegemonikon* ≈ Plato's charioteer — but CONTRADICTED by Hume/Vauvenargues (passion primary) and the Daoist critique of forced control (*wu wei*).

**Sharpest genuine contradictions:**
- *Reason vs. passion primacy:* Kant/Spinoza/Stoics vs. Hume/Vauvenargues — a real, unresolved fork about the springs of action.
- *Self-interest as the truth of virtue:* La Rochefoucauld (Hobbesian-leaning) vs. Smith / Mencius (innate moral "sprouts") / Kant. Genuinely opposed claims (and note the claim-type slippage: La Rochefoucauld is *observational*, Kant *normative*).
- *Society as corrupting vs. constituting the self:* Rousseau/Thoreau/Schopenhauer vs. Aristotle/Confucius/Smith.
- *Pessimism vs. affirmation:* Schopenhauer (existence as suffering) vs. Spinoza/Emerson.
- *Determinism vs. radical freedom:* Spinoza vs. Sartre.

## (G) MISATTRIBUTION WATCHLIST

1. **"I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it."** Attributed to **Voltaire**; actually **Evelyn Beatrice Hall** (pseud. S. G. Tallentyre), *The Friends of Voltaire* (1906), as a *paraphrase* of Voltaire's attitude toward the burning of Helvétius's *De l'esprit*. Hall later explicitly disowned the attribution: in a letter dated **9 May 1939** (quoted in Burdette Kinne, "Voltaire Never Said It!", *Modern Language Notes* vol. 58, no. 7, Nov. 1943, pp. 534–535) she wrote, "The phrase … is my own expression and should not have been put in inverted commas." The French "letter of 6 Feb 1770" version is spurious. *Status: misattributed; genuine author Hall.*

2. **"Go confidently in the direction of your dreams! Live the life you've imagined."** Attributed to **Thoreau** as a direct quote; actually a **paraphrase** (imperative mood) of *Walden*, "Conclusion": "If one advances confidently in the direction of his dreams, and endeavors to live the life which he has imagined, he will meet with a success unexpected in common hours." *Status: paraphrase, not verbatim.*

3. **"The mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation."** Attributed to **Thoreau** — **GENUINE**, *Walden*, "Economy." *Status: verified.*

4. **"My life has been full of terrible misfortunes most of which never happened."** Attributed to **Montaigne** (also floated for Seneca, Twain, Churchill). Not located in the *Essais*; the authentic source of the sentiment is **Seneca**, *Moral Epistles* 13.4 (Gummere/Loeb): "There are more things, Lucilius, likely to frighten us than there are to crush us; we suffer more often in imagination than in reality." *Status: apocryphal as Montaigne.*

5. **"It is not enough to be busy; so are the ants. The question is: what are we busy about?"** Attributed to **Thoreau** — a **paraphrase**; the genuine wording is from Thoreau's letter to H.G.O. Blake (16 Nov 1857): "It is not enough to be industrious; so are the ants. What are you industrious about?" [Walden Woods Project](https://www.walden.org/what-we-do/library/thoreau/mis-quotations/) *Status: paraphrase.*

6. **"In wildness is the preservation of the world."** Genuine Thoreau ("Walking"), but frequently corrupted to **"In wildness is the salvation of the world"** (that variant traces to Aldo Leopold's *A Sand County Almanac*) or with "wilderness" substituted for "wildness." *Status: genuine but commonly garbled.*

7. **"Consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds."** Attributed to **Emerson** — a **truncation** that drops the crucial qualifier. Genuine: "A *foolish* consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds…" (*Self-Reliance*, 1841). Often further misattributed (e.g., to Oscar Wilde or W. C. Fields). [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Emerson_and_Wilde_on_consistency) *Status: genuine but misquoted by omission — the omission inverts the meaning.*

8. **"Life can only be understood backwards; but it must be lived forwards."** Attributed to **Kierkegaard** — a **condensed paraphrase** of a longer 1843 journal entry (Journalen JJ:167). The neat aphorism is not verbatim. Relatedly, the "Kierkegaardian slogan" *"Life is not a problem to be solved but a reality to be experienced"* does **not** appear in his writings. *Status: paraphrase / partly apocryphal.*

9. **"Life is a tragedy for those who feel, and a comedy for those who think."** Attributed to **La Bruyère**; no source located in *Les Caractères*. Very close to **Horace Walpole**, letter of 31 Dec 1769 ("The world is a comedy to those that think, a tragedy to those that feel"). [Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jean_de_La_Bruy%C3%A8re) *Status: dubious as La Bruyère.*

10. **Voltaire — the "omelette" context.** The authentic Voltaire reaction that Hall was glossing is "What a fuss about an omelette!" — verbatim in Hall, *The Friends of Voltaire* (1906, pp. 198–199): "'What a fuss about an omelette!' he had exclaimed when he heard of the burning. How abominably unjust to persecute a man for such an airy trifle as that!" This is **Hall's reportage of Voltaire's exclamation**, not a sourced verbatim Voltaire text; flag so downstream units do not promote it to a verified Voltaire quotation.

### Caveats
- Several French maxims' **numbering is edition-dependent** (Vauvenargues, Chamfort, Joubert especially); numbers given follow the cited PD edition and must be re-checked against any alternate edition before display.
- The Schopenhauer **porcupine parable wording is Saunders's anthologized "Studies in Pessimism," not a 1:1 rendering of Parerga §396** — do not pair the Saunders text with the "§396" citation without the noted qualification.
- The Hume quote's **single-comma original** ("ought only to be the slave") is the authentic 1739 form; the familiar two-comma version is modernized.
- Cross-tradition convergences (Stoic/Epicurean/Buddhist/Daoist/Confucian) are flagged as **candidate edges for downstream merging** with sibling prompts; they signal **robustness, not truth**, and several (e.g., death-equanimity) contain internal inversions that must be preserved, not smoothed.
- Claim-type discipline is load-bearing here: La Rochefoucauld's cynicism is *observational*, Kant's imperatives *normative*, Schopenhauer's eudemonology *prudential*, Spinoza's freedom *metaphysical* — the graph must not let one masquerade as another when computing fork positions.
