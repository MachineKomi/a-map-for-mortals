# Modern Existential Risk, AI, and Our Obligations to the Long Future — Wave-3 Block 3b

*Perspective: philosophy of technology and risk. Throughout, an argument is distinguished from a scenario, and a scenario from a prediction. Existential-risk and AI claims here are prudential, normative, and frequently speculative; their serious critiques are carried as first-class dissent, not footnotes.*

---

## TL;DR

- **The core claim of this literature is prudential and normative, not predictive:** Bostrom, Ord, MacAskill, and Russell argue that a handful of emerging technologies (above all advanced AI) *could* threaten humanity's entire long-term potential, and that this *possibility* — not any forecast of doom — is a defining moral problem of our century. Ord's headline estimate of "around one in six" existential risk this century (*The Precipice*, p. 30) is explicitly an order-of-magnitude judgment under deep uncertainty, not a measurement.
- **The intellectual spine is four ideas plus one design proposal:** Bostrom's *orthogonality thesis* (intelligence and goals are independent) and *instrumental convergence thesis* (diverse goals imply similar power-seeking sub-goals) motivate the *control problem*; Bostrom's *Vulnerable World Hypothesis* generalizes the worry to technology at large; MacAskill's *longtermism* supplies the moral premise that future people count; and Russell's *provably beneficial AI* proposes building machines uncertain about human preferences that defer to us — a research aspiration, not an achieved result.
- **The dissent is serious and must be represented as such:** critics including Émile Torres and Alice Crary argue longtermism can license neglect of present suffering and elite capture; the "fanaticism"/Pascal's-mugging worry argues expected-value reasoning can be hijacked by tiny probabilities of vast payoffs; and quantitative critics (David Thorstad, James Fodor) argue Ord's AI and pandemic figures are too high. The defensible synthesis is that acting on *non-tiny* estimates like 1-in-6 does not commit one to fanaticism, and that concern for the future and justice in the present are not strictly opposed — but they can diverge, and that is where the stakes bite.

---

## Key Findings

1. **These are arguments about the space of possibilities, not forecasts.** Bostrom repeatedly stresses that scenarios like the paperclip maximizer are *illustrations* of a failure mode, not predictions of what will happen. Ord frames his numbers as showing "the right order of magnitude, rather than a more precise probability." Russell's "provably beneficial" is aspirational — he concedes that "safe and controllable" still lack precise formal definitions. Treating any of this as consensus fact would be a category error.

2. **The control problem follows logically from two theses, but each thesis is qualified and contested.** Orthogonality and instrumental convergence, *if* accepted, imply that unaligned superintelligence is a "default" (not certain) catastrophe. But both carry Bostrom's own hedges ("more or less any," "with some caveats," "wide range of"), and formal decision-theoretic work supports only part of the convergence picture — chiefly biases toward option- and survival-preservation, not Bostrom's full list of convergent drives.

3. **Longtermism's premises are presented as modest but are philosophically loaded.** MacAskill's "Future people count. There could be a lot of them. We can make their lives go better" is offered as near-uncontroversial; reviewers counter that the population-ethics commitments smuggled in are anything but, and that the three-premise "argument" is not, as stated, logically valid.

4. **The strongest dissent is not that the future doesn't matter, but that the framing has costs.** Torres, Crary, and others argue the ideology can deflect attention and resources from present, unequally distributed suffering, and can concentrate influence among wealthy technologists — a concern sharpened by the November 2022 collapse of Sam Bankman-Fried's FTX, a major longtermist funder.

5. **There is a genuine constructive middle.** Bostrom's *differential technological development* — slow the dangerous, speed the protective — is the shared answer to the progress-vs-safety fork, endorsed by Ord. Russell's redesign of AI around preference-uncertainty is the constructive answer to the control problem.

**U1**
- **id:** bostrom-orthogonality
- **type:** thesis
- **canonical_claim:** Intelligence and final goals are independent dimensions: in principle almost any level of intelligence can be paired with almost any final goal, so being highly intelligent does not entail having humane or wise goals.
- **original_quote:** "intelligence and final goals (purposes) are orthogonal axes along which possible artificial intellects can freely vary—more or less any level of intelligence could be combined with more or less any final goal" (named term: *orthogonality thesis*)
- **citation:** Bostrom, "The Superintelligent Will" (2012), *Minds and Machines* 22(2); restated in *Superintelligence* (2014), ch. 7
- **tradition:** contemporary/ai-alignment
- **era:** 2012–2014
- **register:** analytic-philosophical
- **claim_type:** metaphysical/conceptual (about the space of possible minds), not empirical prediction
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** holds "with some caveats"/"more or less"; some critics argue sufficiently reflective agents may revise incoherent goals
- **life_domains:** technology, governance, ethics
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety; hope-vs-vigilance
- **cultivates_virtues:** intellectual humility, foresight
- **guards_against:** anthropomorphism (assuming a smart AI will "naturally" be benevolent)
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** Foundational premise for AI risk; an argument, not a forecast.
- **notes:** Flag absolute renderings ("ANY intelligence can have ANY goal") as imprecise — Bostrom hedged.

**U2**
- **id:** bostrom-instrumental-convergence
- **type:** thesis
- **canonical_claim:** A wide range of final goals implies similar intermediate goals — self-preservation, goal-content integrity, cognitive enhancement, resource acquisition — because these help achieve almost any end; hence even a benign-seeming goal can motivate dangerous power-seeking.
- **original_quote:** "Several instrumental values can be identified which are convergent in the sense that their attainment would increase the chances of the agent's goal being realized for a wide range of final goals and a wide range of situations" (named term: *instrumental convergence thesis*)
- **citation:** Bostrom, *Superintelligence* (2014), p. 109 (ch. 7); precursor in Steve Omohundro, "The Basic AI Drives" (2008)
- **tradition:** contemporary/ai-alignment
- **era:** 2008–2014
- **register:** analytic-philosophical
- **claim_type:** prudential/conceptual argument, not prediction
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** qualified by "wide range," "likely to be pursued." Formal work gives only partial support: Alex Turner et al., "Optimal Policies Tend to Seek Power" (NeurIPS 2021, arXiv:1912.01683) — "the first formal theory of the statistical tendencies of optimal policies" — proves that "certain environmental symmetries are sufficient for optimal policies to tend to seek power" (their Theorem 6.13), supporting option/survival-preservation but not Bostrom's full list.
- **life_domains:** technology, governance
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety
- **cultivates_virtues:** foresight, prudence
- **guards_against:** complacency about "harmless" objectives
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** Paired with orthogonality to argue existential catastrophe is a "default" (not certain) outcome of unaligned superintelligence.
- **notes:** Credit Omohundro for the precursor "basic AI drives"; Bostrom named the thesis.

- **tradition:** contemporary/ai-alignment
- **era:** 2014
- **register:** analytic-philosophical
- **claim_type:** prudential

- **attribution_confidence:** high

**U5**
- **id:** bostrom-vulnerable-world
- **type:** hypothesis
- **canonical_claim:** Technological invention is like drawing balls from an urn; most are beneficial (white) or mixed (grey), but there may be a "black ball" — a technology that by default devastates civilization — and we have avoided drawing one so far by luck, not wisdom.
- **original_quote:** "a black ball [is] a technology that upon discovery by default causes civilizational devastation" (named terms: *black ball*, *Vulnerable World Hypothesis*)
- **citation:** Bostrom, "The Vulnerable World Hypothesis," *Global Policy* 10(4), 2019
- **tradition:** contemporary/philosophy-of-technology
- **era:** 2019
- **register:** analytic-philosophical
- **claim_type:** conditional hypothesis (its truth is "an open question," per Bostrom)
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** explicitly conditional ("if technological development continues... unless civilisation sufficiently exits the semi-anarchic default condition"); Bostrom does NOT assert we have drawn a black ball
- **life_domains:** technology, governance, security
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety; liberty-vs-security (his proposed stabilizations include intrusive preventive policing / global governance, which he does not straightforwardly endorse)
- **cultivates_virtues:** prudence, humility, foresight
- **guards_against:** techno-optimist complacency
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** Bostrom balances survival against Orwellian costs; a warning framework, not a policy endorsement.
- **notes:** The mass-surveillance implication is the most criticized element; flag as dilemma, not endorsement.

- **citation:** Bostrom, "Existential Risks" (2002); *Superintelligence* (2014), pp. 229–237; endorsed by Ord, *The Precipice* (2020)
- **tradition:** contemporary/philosophy-of-technology
- **era:** 2002–2020
- **register:** normative-prescriptive
- **claim_type:** prudential/normative
- **polarity:** constructive
- **conditionality:** assumes we can actually influence sequencing; compatible with mild technological determinism
- **life_domains:** technology, governance, policy
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety (a proposed synthesis of the fork)
- **cultivates_virtues:** prudence, strategic patience

**U8**
- **id:** ord-one-in-six
- **type:** estimate
- **canonical_claim:** Ord's rough personal estimate is about a one-in-six chance of existential catastrophe this century, dominated by anthropogenic risks — and he stresses these are order-of-magnitude judgments, not precise measurements.
- **original_quote:** "I put the existential risk this century at around one in six: Russian roulette."
- **citation:** Ord, *The Precipice* (2020), p. 30; risk table, ch. 6 (Table 6.1)
- **tradition:** contemporary/existential-risk
- **era:** 2020
- **register:** quantitative-estimative
- **claim_type:** empirical estimate under deep uncertainty (subjective Bayesian)
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** heavily caveated by Ord ("show the right order of magnitude, rather than a more precise probability"). The 1-in-6 is NOT business-as-usual; as Ord explained on the 80,000 Hours podcast, business-as-usual is "something like two bullets in the chamber," of which "we'll probably remove one," so "the headline figure should be one in three being the difference between the business as usual risk and how much of that we could eliminate if we really got our act together." Critics argue several figures are too high (see U9, Caveats).
- **life_domains:** governance, ethics, technology
- **life_stages:** intergenerational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** act-on-small-probability-of-vast-harm
- **cultivates_virtues:** calibrated judgment, honesty about uncertainty
- **guards_against:** false precision; also over-anchoring on his numbers
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** Table 6.1 per-risk estimates: unaligned AI ~1 in 10; engineered pandemics ~1 in 30; unforeseen anthropogenic ~1 in 30; nuclear war ~1 in 1,000; climate change ~1 in 1,000; total natural risk ~1 in 10,000.
- **notes:** The single most-cited and most-contested number in the field.

**U9**
- **id:** ord-natural-vs-anthropogenic
- **type:** taxonomy
- **canonical_claim:** Natural risks (asteroids, supervolcanoes, stellar explosions) are real but small — we've survived 2,000 centuries of them — whereas the dominant modern risks are anthropogenic, especially unaligned AI and engineered pandemics.
- **original_quote:** null
- **citation:** Ord, *The Precipice* (2020), chs. 3–5
- **tradition:** contemporary/existential-risk
- **era:** 2020
- **register:** analytic-scientific
- **claim_type:** empirical (natural-risk bounds are relatively well-grounded; anthropogenic estimates are far more speculative)
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** the survival-based bound on natural risk is more defensible than the AI/pandemic figures. Even the natural figures are contested: Jean-Marc Salotti (2022) calculated the odds of an extinction-level asteroid impact in the next century at roughly 1 in 300 million, versus Ord's ~1 in 1,000,000 (reported in The Conversation, "Is there really a 1 in 6 chance of human extinction this century?").
- **life_domains:** technology, governance, ethics
- **life_stages:** intergenerational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety
- **cultivates_virtues:** proportion, evidence-sensitivity
- **guards_against:** misallocating attention to vivid but minor natural risks
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** Ord uses anthropogenic dominance to date the "Precipice" to 1945.
- **notes:** Distinguish the well-grounded natural bounds from the speculative anthropogenic estimates.

**U11**
- **id:** macaskill-longtermism
- **type:** thesis
- **canonical_claim:** Longtermism is the view that positively influencing the long-term future is a key moral priority of our time, resting on three premises: future people count morally, there may be very many of them, and we can make their lives go better.
- **original_quote:** "Future people count. There could be a lot of them. We can make their lives go better." (also: "the idea that positively influencing the long-term future is a key moral priority of our time")
- **citation:** MacAskill, *What We Owe the Future* (2022), Introduction (~pp. 9–10)
- **tradition:** contemporary/longtermism
- **era:** 2022
- **register:** normative-ethical, popular
- **claim_type:** normative
- **polarity:** constructive
- **conditionality:** MacAskill calls the premises "not particularly controversial," but critics argue this understates disagreement, especially in population ethics. Kieran Setiya (*Boston Review*, Aug 2022): "*What We Owe the Future* is an instructive, intelligent book... But a moral arithmetic is only as good as its axioms. I hope readers approach longtermism with the open-mindedness and moral judgment MacAskill wants us to preserve." The *Utilitas* reviewer adds that the three-premise "case" is not a logically valid argument (the "philosopherism" parody: the same form would make almost anything a "key priority").
- **life_domains:** ethics, career, philanthropy, governance
- **life_stages:** intergenerational; also individual career choice
- **addresses_dilemmas:** present-vs-future generations
- **cultivates_virtues:** impartial benevolence, responsibility
- **guards_against:** temporal parochialism
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** MacAskill distinguishes "moderate" longtermism (his book) from "strong/radical" longtermism.
- **notes:** NYT op-ed variant: "And we can make their lives better." Use book wording for the book.

**U12**
- **id:** macaskill-value-lock-in
- **type:** concept
- **canonical_claim:** Values are contingent and malleable now but could become "locked in" — an event causing a single value system to persist for an extremely long time — especially via AGI, world government, or space settlement; hence preserving plasticity and moral option-value matters.
- **original_quote:** null (named term: *value lock-in*)
- **citation:** MacAskill, *What We Owe the Future* (2022), ch. 4
- **tradition:** contemporary/longtermism
- **era:** 2022
- **register:** normative-ethical
- **claim_type:** prudential/normative + speculative
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** relies on the contested "early plasticity, later rigidity" model of cultural evolution; Robin Hanson argues MacAskill conflates two distinct lock-in mechanisms (a stable-values power "taking over the world" vs. evolutionary value-convergence)
- **life_domains:** ethics, governance, technology
- **life_stages:** intergenerational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** present-vs-future generations; hope-vs-vigilance
- **cultivates_virtues:** humility about one's own values, open-mindedness
- **guards_against:** premature moral certainty; totalitarian entrenchment
- **attribution_confidence:** high
- **speaker_context:** MacAskill's central historical case is the contingency of abolition (itself criticized as not clearly meeting his own significance/persistence/contingency criteria).
- **notes:** Connects to Bostrom's singleton and instrumental "goal-content integrity."

- **original_quote:** null (named terms: *standard model*, *King Midas problem*, *gorilla problem*)
- **citation:** Russell, *Human Compatible* (2019), esp. chs. 1–5
- **tradition:** contemporary/ai-alignment

- **register:** analytic / popular-science
- **claim_type:** prudential/technical argument
- **polarity:** cautionary
- **conditionality:** premised on continued progress toward highly capable AI; Russell rebuts "it'll never happen" as a foolhardy bet against human ingenuity
- **life_domains:** technology, governance
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety
- **cultivates_virtues:** intellectual honesty, engineering humility

**U14**
- **id:** russell-provably-beneficial

- **citation:** Russell, *Human Compatible* (2019), p. 173
- **tradition:** contemporary/ai-alignment
- **era:** 2019
- **register:** normative-prescriptive / technical
- **claim_type:** prudential/normative proposal (a research direction, not an established result)
- **polarity:** constructive/hopeful
- **conditionality:** Russell concedes "safe and controllable" lack precise formal definitions; the approach is a program, not a finished guarantee
- **life_domains:** technology, governance
- **life_stages:** civilizational
- **addresses_dilemmas:** progress-vs-safety; hope-vs-vigilance
- **cultivates_virtues:** humility (in the machine and the designer), deference

### C. Forks (Great Tensions/Dilemmas)

**Fork 1 — Push progress vs. hold back for safety.** Bostrom's Vulnerable World Hypothesis and Russell's critique of the standard model both counsel caution, yet neither endorses blanket stagnation. The proposed synthesis is Bostrom's *differential technological development* (U6): change the *order*, not the fact, of progress — slow the dangerous, speed the protective. The tension is real because sequencing requires coordination we may not have, and because "dangerous vs. protective" is often knowable only in hindsight.

**Fork 2 — Present generation vs. future generations.** MacAskill and Ord argue future people "count" and are numerous, so their interests weigh heavily now. Torres and Crary reply that this abstraction can eclipse the concrete suffering of the living, disproportionately of the marginalized. The honest position: future-concern and present-justice are not strictly opposed (decarbonization helps both — MacAskill calls it a "proof of concept" for longtermism, though critics note it is precisely a case where short- and long-term reasoning already coincide), but they *can* diverge when resources and attention are scarce, and the divergence is where the moral stakes bite.

**Fork 3 — Hope/optimism vs. vigilance/precaution.** Ord's "in optimism lies urgency" and Russell's "provably beneficial" program are hopeful; the black-ball hypothesis and the control problem are vigilant. These are complementary moods, not contradictions — but the balance matters: too much doom breeds apathy ("we'll all be gone anyway"), too much optimism breeds complacency.

**Fork 4 — Act on a small probability of vast harm vs. refuse to be "mugged" by tiny probabilities.** This is the deepest philosophical fork. Expected-value reasoning says a 1-in-10 chance of unaligned-AI catastrophe obviously warrants action; but the same logic, pushed to 1-in-a-quadrillion payoffs, yields Pascal's mugging and "fanaticism." The defensible middle: existential-risk estimates like Ord's ~1-in-6 are *not* Pascalian (they are non-tiny), so acting on them need not commit one to fanaticism — but the boundary is contested, and the movement's critics argue the fanatical structure lurks in the foundational documents even where the popular books avoid it.

### D. Virtues & Dangers

**Virtues these ideas cultivate:** foresight and scope-sensitivity (caring about consequences distant in time and large in number); intellectual humility (orthogonality warns against assuming intelligence implies wisdom; value lock-in warns against assuming our values are final); prudence and preparation (solve control *before* takeoff); calibrated honesty about uncertainty (Ord's insistence his numbers show orders of magnitude, not precision); moral seriousness about the stakes; and hope as an active, agency-preserving stance.

**Dangers they guard against:** anthropomorphic complacency about AI; misspecified objectives (King Midas); the treacherous turn; techno-optimist recklessness (the black ball drawn by luck); scope neglect and short-termism; irreversible concentration of power (singleton, value lock-in).

**Dangers the ideas themselves can introduce (per the dissent):** abstraction that erases present injustice; fanaticism and Pascal's mugging; elite/technocratic capture of philanthropy and policy; and the illiberal temptations of the black-ball framing (mass surveillance, world government). These are first-class cautions.

### F. Misattribution-Watchlist

1. **"The AI does not hate you, nor does it love you, but you are made out of atoms which it can use for something else."** — Frequently misattributed to **Bostrom**. It is **Eliezer Yudkowsky's**, from "Artificial Intelligence as a Positive and Negative Factor in Global Risk" (2008), in the Bostrom/Ćirković-edited volume *Global Catastrophic Risks*. Two wording variants exist: the published-chapter form ("neither hates you, nor loves you... that it can use") and the popular form ("does not... which"). The misattribution arises because Bostrom co-edited the volume and the theme matches his paperclip argument.
2. **Paperclip maximizer origin.** Often mis-dated to *Superintelligence* (2014). The scenario originates in **Bostrom's 2003 paper** "Ethical Issues in Advanced Artificial Intelligence"; the 2014 book elaborates it. The catchy label was popularized on LessWrong. Bostrom stresses it is an illustration, **not a prediction**.
3. **"Whoever becomes the leader in this sphere will become the ruler of the world."** — This is **Vladimir Putin**, from his Knowledge Day satellite open-lesson (Yaroslavl, 1 September 2017): "Artificial intelligence is the future, not only of Russia, but of all of mankind... Whoever becomes the leader in this sphere will become the ruler of the world," adding that it would be "strongly undesirable if someone wins a monopolist position." Sometimes loosely conflated with Bostrom's decisive-strategic-advantage thesis. Do not attribute to Bostrom.
4. **Orthogonality over-simplified.** Renderings that drop Bostrom's hedge ("more or less any... with some caveats") and state strict independence ("ANY intelligence can have ANY goal") are imprecise; flag.
5. **Instrumental convergence sole authorship.** Crediting Bostrom as sole originator overlooks **Steve Omohundro's** "The Basic AI Drives" (2008), the named precursor. Bostrom named the *thesis*; Omohundro described the *drives*.
6. **"Provably beneficial" as an achieved result.** Russell's phrase is a research aspiration; treat any claim that beneficial AI has been "proven" as a misreading. Also note his principles shifted from "values" (2017) to "preferences" (2019 book).

## Caveats

- **Nothing here is a prediction.** The decisive-strategic-advantage scenario (U4), the treacherous turn, the black ball, and the AI-catastrophe "default outcome" are conditional arguments and scenarios. Presenting them as consensus about the future would misrepresent every author cited, several of whom (Bostrom on the paperclip case; Ord on his own numbers) explicitly disclaim prediction.
- **Claim-type discipline matters here more than usual.** Orthogonality is conceptual/metaphysical; instrumental convergence is a contested conceptual-plus-empirical claim; Ord's estimates are subjective empirical judgments; longtermism and provably-beneficial AI are normative/prudential proposals. Collapsing these into one "expert warning" would be an error.
- **Sourcing asymmetry:** natural-risk bounds (survival over 2,000 centuries) are relatively well-grounded; the AI and engineered-pandemic figures that drive the headline number rest on judgment, and are precisely where quantitative critics concentrate their fire.
- **Page numbers to confirm:** MacAskill's Introduction passage and Ord's p. 30 / p. 58 were corroborated via secondary scholarly citations and reviews rather than a direct read of the print editions; treat the page attributions as high-but-not-certain pending a primary-edition check.
- **The movement's reputational context is real but distinct from the arguments' merits.** The FTX collapse damaged longtermism's public standing and funding; this is sociologically important (and fuels the elite-capture critique) but does not by itself refute or confirm the philosophical claims. Keep the *ad hominem* and the *argument* separate.
- **Two documented wording variants** exist for the Yudkowsky "atoms" line and for MacAskill's third premise (book vs. NYT op-ed); use the book/print form for book citations and note the variant.
