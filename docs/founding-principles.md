# The Founding Principles
### The north star of the project — *working title: The Map of a Life*

These are the commitments that govern this work: how it gathers wisdom, how it weighs it, and how it renders it. They are written to be returned to — a fixed point to steer by when the work grows complicated.

They are not commandments. Most of them are open to revision as the research deepens, as we meet new thinkers, and as better framings emerge. **One is not.** The first principle stands above the rest, and above the project itself. It does not move.

---

## The North Star

### 1 · Truth above all else

Truth-seeking comes before ideology, before aesthetics, before comfort, before morality and the wish to appear virtuous, before sentiment, before preference, before marketability — and before whatever we would like to be true.

This project never begins with a conclusion and reaches back for wisdom to support it. It does not lean toward goodness, virtue, success, or happiness. It may well turn out that honest, disciplined, just, or compassionate lives tend to go better — and if so, the work will show it, with evidence, in the open. But it will never *assume* it.

We let the data lead, and we follow where it goes — especially when we would rather it went elsewhere. Better uncomfortable and right than comforted and wrong.

## The Principles

These protect the north star. Each is a way of keeping the first one honest.

**2 · Begin with questions, not answers.**
We start from what we want to understand, not from what we hope to prove. The evidence leads; we follow. A conclusion reached before the inquiry is not a finding — it is a bias wearing a finding's clothes.

**3 · No ideology owns the work.**
No school, faith, party, or fashion decides in advance what we are allowed to find. Every tradition is a witness to be weighed; none is a master to be obeyed.

**4 · Intellectual honesty in everything.**
We represent each thinker as they would recognise themselves, put the strongest case to the views we doubt, and report what surprised us — or what we hoped not to find. We change our minds in public.

**5 · No false certainty.**
We do not manufacture confidence we have not earned. *Often*, *tends to*, *under these conditions*, and *we don't yet know* are usually the truer words, and we are not ashamed to use them.

**6 · Contradiction is data, not embarrassment.**
Where the wise disagree, we map the disagreement honestly rather than dissolve it into a comfortable average. A real tension, shown plainly, teaches more than a false peace.

**7 · Probabilities, not guarantees.**
Few choices have certain consequences. Most wisdom is conditional and probabilistic — a choice tilts the odds, it does not command the outcome. We must still generalise in order to be useful; we simply refuse to let a generalisation pretend to be a law. We draw tendencies and risks, never destinies.

**8 · Every trade-off shows both its costs.**
Wisdom usually asks you to pay for what you gain. We name the price as plainly as the prize, and never sell one side of a choice by hiding the other.

**9 · Usefulness is the test — but never at truth's expense.**
This is a guide for living, not a museum of ideas. A truth that cannot help a real person at a real fork is unfinished work. Yet we would rather be useful *and* honest than useful and wrong: clarity must never be bought with a quiet lie.

**10 · Beauty in the service of clarity.**
We make the work beautiful so the truth is easier to see and harder to forget — never to make a finding look better, surer, or simpler than it is. The design serves the meaning; it does not paper over it.

**11 · Tested across time — never called proven.**
We give weight to what recurs independently across eras, cultures, and lived consequences. But recurrence is a signal, not a verdict: age and popularity show that an idea *resonated*, not that it is *correct*. Convergence is treated as evidence of robustness — never as proof of truth.

**12 · Three kinds of truth, kept distinct.**
What *is* the case, what we *ought* to do, and what merely *tends to work* are three different questions. We keep them apart, and never let one quietly put on the clothing of another.

**13 · We honour the source.**
Every voice is credited, every quotation checked, every doubtful attribution flagged as doubtful. We add nothing to a wise person's mouth, and we do not borrow authority we have not earned.

**14 · We refuse to flatten a life.**
No diagram contains a whole human being. Person and temperament, time and place, culture and class, luck and biology, wound and opportunity and history — all bear on every choice, and we keep saying so. The map is never the territory, and we will not pretend otherwise.

**15 · We show our work.**
How the corpus was built, where it is thin, what we are unsure of, and what we have not yet done — all of it stays in the open. The whole project remains revisable as it grows.

---

## A note on revision

Every principle here but the first is a draft — open to sharpening, splitting, or change as the research deepens. That openness is itself part of the method.

One principle is not a draft, and never will be: **truth above all else.** Everything else exists to protect it.
