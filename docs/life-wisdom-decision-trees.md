# A Map of the Forks in the Road
### A book of life-wisdom decision trees, to grow up alongside

*Volume I — a living first draft to refine, illustrate, and pass down.*

---

I wish someone had drawn me these when I was small. Not lectures — **maps**. Pictures of the forks you'll meet, the two ways each one tends to go, and the small handful of things that the people who thought hardest about living — the Stoics, Nietzsche, Aristotle, and a few wise modern voices — figured out along the way.

None of this is gospel. Most of it has no stronger backing than honest experience and a few real statistics, and where there are numbers I've said so. You'll write your own map in the end. But here's a head start.

**How to read a tree**
- A **rounded box** is the situation you're standing in.
- A **diamond** is a *choice* — your fork in the road.
- **Green** is the path that tends to turn out well; **amber** is the harder road; **red** is the one with a rare but serious downside.
- The **purple box** at the bottom is the lesson, and who it comes from.

The colours show *tendencies*, not certainties. Life is, as the Sunscreen song put it, half chance. But you steer the other half.

## Sources, and a few honest caveats

A book like this should be honest about where it leans on evidence and where it leans on the accumulated experience of thoughtful people. Sanity-check anything before you print it — figures and findings drift over time.

**The thinkers behind the lessons**
- **Mary Schmich**, *"Advice, like youth, probably just wasted on the young"* (Chicago Tribune, 1 June 1997) — voiced to the world by **Baz Luhrmann** as *Everybody's Free (To Wear Sunscreen)* (1997/1999). The ideas about guarding hearts, not over-worrying about the future, your own timing for a family, and yes, sunscreen, are paraphrased from its spirit, not quoted.
- **The Stoics** — Epictetus (the dichotomy of control), Seneca (*On the Shortness of Life*; time as the one thing we can't buy back), Marcus Aurelius (*Meditations*), and the practice of *premeditatio malorum*, picturing the bad outcome in advance.
- **Friedrich Nietzsche** — *amor fati*, the love of one's fate, and the idea that suffering, met well, can build rather than break us.
- **Aristotle** — virtue and character as habits, and true friendship.
- **Carol Dweck** — the growth mindset, and the power of "yet".
- **Theodore Roosevelt** is widely credited with "comparison is the thief of joy" (attribution is debated, but the idea is sound).
- **Jeff Bezos** — the "regret-minimisation framework" behind the 80-year-old test.
- **Cal Newport** — "be so good they can't ignore you"; rare, valuable skills over titles.

**The statistics, and their limits**
- **Marriage timing (5.1, 6.3):** Nicholas Wolfinger's analysis of US survey data (and a parallel European analysis) finds divorce risk is far higher for those marrying in their teens or early 20s, falls sharply through the late 20s, is lowest in roughly the late 20s to early 30s, then ticks up modestly after the early 30s, the so-called "Goldilocks" pattern. This is **correlation, not destiny**: selection effects matter, and plenty of early and late marriages thrive. Treat it as a tendency.
- **Getting good at things (4.2):** Anders Ericsson's research shows *deliberate* practice, focused, effortful, feedback-driven, at the edge of your ability, is what builds expertise, not mere hours. The popular "10,000-hour rule" was a simplification Ericsson himself disliked. Later work (Macnamara and colleagues; David Epstein's *Range*) shows **talent, starting age, and broad exploration also matter** — practice explains a lot, but not everything.
- **Compound interest (7.1):** the figures are a clean illustration at about 7% annual growth, compounded monthly, before fees, tax, and inflation. The early investor (200/month for ages 22–32 = 24,000 in, then left to grow) reaches roughly 346,000 by 65; the later investor (200/month for ages 32–65 = 79,200 in) reaches roughly 309,000. Real returns vary year to year, and this is not a guarantee, but the *direction* — time beats size — is rock-solid maths.
- **Early parenthood (5.5):** that starting a family very young tends to correlate with interrupted education and tighter finances is well established, and it is **a tendency, not a verdict on anyone**. Framed here around readiness, not shame.

**A note on the whole thing.** Even the author of "Sunscreen" admitted her advice had no firmer basis than her own meandering experience. So will yours. These maps are a starting point, not a cage. Your child will redraw them, and that's exactly as it should be.
