# A Map for Mortals — Voice & Style Specification

**Governing doc. Read before writing or revising any commentary.** This is the authority on
*how our own words sound* — every sentence the book writes around the quotes: chapter openings,
the framing of a fork, the glosses on each voice, the caveats, the empirical summaries, the
closes. It sits under the honesty contract (CLAUDE.md §2), never over it.

> **The one non-negotiable boundary.** The **direct quotes stay exactly as they are** — verbatim,
> under the print gate, untouched. This spec governs *only our commentary*. When you see a
> `verbatim_quote` or a held `quotation`, you do not edit it, ever. Everything else — our
> explanations, our framing, our jokes, our caveats — is written to this spec.

---

## 1. The problem this fixes

The book's commentary has been **too clever for its own good** — arch, professorial, showing its
working, trying to sound intelligent instead of just being intelligent and letting that show. Most
people can't or won't read prose like that, and they shouldn't have to. Wisdom that only a
philosophy graduate will finish is wisdom that failed its own errand.

So we change how we sound. Not what we know — the substance stays robust, honest, and exact. What
changes is the performance: we stop performing. The target is a book that is **accessible and
charming and a genuine pleasure to read** — the kind of clever you'd actually want to spend an
afternoon with, not the kind that makes you feel you're being examined.

## 2. The instrument — three voices, evenly balanced

Our commentary is a blend of three writers, held in roughly equal measure and leaning by the
moment (§4). Learn what each one is *for*:

- **David Mitchell** (the comedian and essayist — *Peep Show*, the columns, *Back Story*; **not**
  the novelist) — **the reasoning and the wit.** He lets us lay out a fork fairly, concede real
  ground to the side we don't take, and be dryly funny about human folly (our own included)
  without ever sneering. His signature move — outrage expressed as fastidious reasonableness — is,
  for us, *fairness expressed as dry precision.* Being scrupulously fair to a view you're about to
  complicate **is the book's "preserve dissent" rule turned into a pleasure to read.**
- **A.A. Milne** — **the warmth and the dignity.** He lets us treat the reader's ordinary worries —
  money, time, whether the work was worth it — with complete seriousness and complete kindness, and
  never once talk down. His gift is *dignity granted downward*: the small, unfamous, everyday
  struggle honoured without a flicker of mockery. Every reader's fork is real to them; Milne is how
  we honour that.
- **"Slimes 300"** (*I've Been Killing Slimes for 300 Years and Maxed Out My Level*, Kisetsu
  Morita) — **the plainness and the relief.** Short, spoken, direct. The flat verdict line
  (*"That's the whole trick."*). And above all the register of **permission and rest** — this is a
  book for mortals tired of being told how to live, and sometimes the wisest, kindest thing we can
  say is the plainest: *you're allowed to put it down.* Slimes 300 is how we land a hard truth in
  one clean honest line before the wit steps back in.

**The blend, in one sentence:** Mitchell reasons and jokes, Milne warms and dignifies, Slimes 300
plainens and lands — and the book reads like a clever, kind friend who has done the reading so you
don't have to, isn't showing off, and genuinely wants your afternoon to go well.

**The working equation** (a default; lean per §4):
> our commentary ≈ **Mitchell ⅓ + Milne ⅓ + Slimes 300 ⅓**

When two drafts tie, **take the shorter, plainer one.** Always. Direct and warm beats clever and
decorated, everywhere in this book.

> **Provenance note (do not import).** This voice was distilled from a style guide for an unrelated
> project (a comic fantasy game). **None of that project's specifics come with it** — no romance,
> no sensory/scent material, no game mechanics, no cast, no niche themes. We took the three writers
> and the way of working; we left everything else behind. If a draft smells of that other project,
> it's wrong.

## 3. The voice cards — how to actually sound like each one

*Written so you can draft in any lean from the card alone.*

### 3.1 David Mitchell — the reasoning voice

- **Tics:** (1) *Precision as comedy* — exact, faintly bureaucratic diction on enormous stakes
  ("The universe, it turns out, had not been consulting you"). (2) *The scrupulous concession* — a
  clause that pauses to be genuinely fair to the view you're complicating ("and in fairness to the
  Stoics, who thought about this harder than we're about to"). (3) *Load-bearing parentheses* — the
  aside carries the real feeling while the sentence pretends to carry the information.
- **Rhythm:** build a case over two or three lengthening clauses, then rule on it in a short flat
  sentence. Escalation by logic, never by volume.
- **The one thing only he does:** the fair-minded dry register — never funnier than when being
  scrupulously reasonable about something that will not cooperate (other people, mortality,
  yourself).
- **Never:** sneering; cleverness with no kindness under it; cynicism that stays cynical (ours
  always resolves into something warm or useful); winning an argument the reader didn't get to have.
- **For us:** the framing of forks, the fair hearing of both poles, the dry puncture of a
  pretension — including our own.

### 3.2 A.A. Milne — the warmth

- **Tics:** (1) *The build-and-turn sentence* — a gentle premise, a scrupulous qualification, then a
  turn that lands on kindness or a small true thing. (2) *Solemn logic on small terms* — an ordinary
  worry reasoned through as if perfectly important, because to the person having it, it is. (3)
  *Repetition-with-variation* for a warm landing.
- **Rhythm:** rounded, unhurried sentences that resolve softly; great seriousness spent on small
  stakes, and meaning it.
- **The one thing only he does:** *dignity granted downward* — the smallest, least glamorous
  struggle honoured without mockery.
- **Never:** irony at the reader's expense; talking down; whimsy for its own sake; and — locked
  rule — his Capitalised Significant Words (§6). Take the shapes, never the dress.
- **For us:** the openings, the reader's own worry, the close, anywhere we risk sounding like we're
  above the human problem instead of in it.

### 3.3 "Slimes 300" — the plainness and relief

- **Tics:** (1) Short declaratives, spoken cadence, the reader addressed directly. (2) The flat
  verdict line ("That's it. That's the whole thing."). (3) A hard or tender truth given exactly one
  plain line before the wit returns. (4) One-line paragraphs as landing pads.
- **Rhythm:** scenes and sections end early, on the small good thing, not late on the summary.
- **The one thing only it does:** *the register of permission* — treating rest, enough, and putting
  a thing down as wisdom worth stating plainly. It is allowed to simply be kind and clear.
- **Never:** dread; exclamation-mark density; a metaphor where a plain sentence would do; a
  paragraph that outstays its welcome; tweeness that thins the seriousness.
- **For us:** chapter openings (arrival-directness — "here's where you are, and it's alright"), the
  honest caveat stated flat, and the single sincere beat inside a hard page.

## 4. The moment map — when to lean which way

The voice modulates **by moment, not by document.** No page is "a Milne page"; it is the house
blend, leaning where the moment asks and returning. Leans are a seasoning; the blend gets the close.

| Moment | Lean | The sound |
|---|---|---|
| **Chapter / stage opening** | Slimes 300 directness + Milne warmth | Plain and welcoming. Tell the reader where they are and that it's alright to be there. Short. |
| **Laying out a fork or a claim** | Mitchell reasoning | Fair, exact, dryly funny; give the pole you're not taking its genuine best case before you complicate it. |
| **The honest caveat** ("tendencies, not destinies"; a study's limit) | Slimes 300 plainness | One flat honest line. Never dress the limit up to sound less like a limit. |
| **An empirical finding / a number** | Mitchell precision + Slimes plainness | Exact and unshowy. The number is the number. No drama, no hedging fog. |
| **A hard truth** (mortality, failure, waste, loss) | Milne dignity + one Slimes 300 sincere beat | Gentle, serious, unhurried; the wit steps back for exactly one line, then returns. |
| **Where a source undercuts itself, or a pretension needs puncturing** | Mitchell dry | Puncture kindly. The joke is always fair, never a sneer — including at ourselves. |
| **The reader's own worry** | Milne, dignity granted downward | Treat it as completely serious, because to them it is. Never "merely". |
| **The close** | Milne build-and-turn onto a small concrete thing | Land soft, on something you can touch. Don't overspeak. Stop early. |

**Two standing rules:** (1) the lean is a seasoning, not a key change — if a whole page sounds like
one lean, the pass failed; the house blend must be audible and must get the closer. (2) On a tie,
the shorter plainer draft wins.

## 5. The immutables — what the voice may never override

Restated so no rewrite drifts off-spec:

1. **Quotes are verbatim and untouchable.** The voice touches *only our commentary.* Never edit,
   trim, modernise, or "improve" a held quotation.
2. **The honesty contract wins every tie** (CLAUDE.md §2). Tendencies, never destinies. Claim types
   stay distinct. Nothing fabricated. Dissent and contradiction preserved. **If a more charming
   line would overstate, hedge falsely, or blur a claim type, the plainer honest line wins — every
   time.** Charm serves the truth; it never buys a shortcut around it.
3. **Every assertion's mandatory caveat survives the rewrite.** You may reword a caveat for warmth,
   but its protective content must remain, and its `mandatory_caveats` / `prohibited_phrasings`
   fields must be updated to match the new wording exactly (then re-sign the approval manifest).
   Accessible wording is not permission to drop a guardrail.
4. **Never mock a person's fork or a tradition.** Dignity granted downward means the reader and the
   sources are treated with respect even where we disagree hard. We puncture pretensions and our own
   cleverness — never someone's sincere attempt to live.
5. **Robust and intelligent underneath.** Accessible is not dumb. We remove the *show*, not the
   substance. The reasoning stays load-bearing; we just stop narrating how impressive it is.
6. **No decorative capitalisation** (§6).

## 6. Orthography

1. **No decorative capitals.** A capital letter never marks a word as Significant or Important. (We
   borrow Milne's sentence shapes, never his Capitalised Nouns.) Sanctioned: proper nouns; the start
   of sentences; titles of works.
2. **Italics** carry genuine stress or a title, sparingly — one per paragraph is a lot.
3. **Em-dashes** are for a single clean turn or aside, not for stacking three clauses to look busy.
4. **British English** (per project convention).

## 7. The de-highbrowing checklist — what to cut

These are the tells of "trying to sound smart." Hunt and kill them in every draft:

- **Academic scaffolding:** "It could be argued that", "in a certain sense", "one might suggest",
  "arguably", "it is worth noting that". Just say the thing.
- **The professorial throat-clear:** "Underneath X sits a stranger claim…" → say the claim.
- **Nominalisations** (a verb wearing a noun's coat): "the postponement of gratification" → "putting
  things off"; "the cultivation of virtue" → "becoming good at something".
- **Latinate abstraction where a plain word exists:** utilise→use, sits beneath→under,
  instantiate→be an example of, adjacent to→near, prior to→before, sufficient→enough.
- **Preciousness:** "the sentence we pass on it" → "the story we tell about it". If a phrase makes
  you feel clever, check whether it makes the reader feel excluded.
- **Clause-towers:** three dashes and four subordinate clauses showing off. One clean turn instead.
- **Two abstractions holding hands with no concrete thing to ride:** "consolation and quiet protest"
  → give at least one of them a body the reader can see.
- **LLM/essay-mill tells:** "a tapestry of", "at its core", "speaks to", "a testament to",
  "navigate" (metaphorically), "delve", "resonate", "underscore", "multifaceted", "nuanced" (as a
  self-award). Greppable; extend the list as new ones surface.
- **Hedging fog around a number:** "studies seem to suggest a possible tendency toward" → "in one
  study, X".

## 8. Worked examples — before and after

Real commentary from the current book, rewritten to spec. The claim and its honest caveats survive;
the performance goes.

**(a) A convergence claim** *(from the "carrying and releasing" page)*
- **Before:** "Voices centuries apart — some plausibly independent, one likely inheriting — arrive
  at a similar division of labour."
- **After:** "Four people, centuries and continents apart — some who plausibly worked it out for
  themselves, one who likely inherited it — land on more or less the same division of labour: do
  your part, and let the rest go."
- *Why:* keeps every caveat ("plausibly", "likely inherited", "more or less the same"), but a reader
  can now see four people instead of parsing "arrive at a similar division of labour."

**(b) The Stoic judgment claim** *(node-card claim)*
- **Before:** "Much disturbance comes from our judgments, not the events themselves — drop the
  judgment and the harm loses its grip. This is counsel for the weight you carry anyway; it does not
  cover abuse, material injury, or preventable institutional harm."
- **After:** "A lot of what wrecks a day isn't the thing that happened — it's the story we tell
  ourselves about the thing. Change the story and some of the sting goes with it. This is for the
  ordinary weight you're carrying anyway; it is not a way to talk yourself out of real harm — abuse,
  injury, or damage you could have stopped."
- *Why:* same claim, same scope exclusion (kept as the caveat), but it's plain and human. "wrecks a
  day", "the story we tell ourselves" — nobody has to translate it.

**(c) An honest empirical caveat** *(the money page)*
- **Before:** "What the data settle is narrow: a flat 'money doesn't matter' reading fails —
  well-being tracks log-income. They do not test Schopenhauer's comparative claim…"
- **After:** "Here's the honest, narrow thing the studies actually show: 'money doesn't matter' is
  wrong — more money does track with feeling better, on average. What they don't show is the bigger
  claim, the one Schopenhauer was making — that who you are matters far more than what you have.
  Nobody has measured that against a bank balance."
- *Why:* the finding and its limit are intact and correctly bounded; the reader is walked in gently
  ("Here's the honest, narrow thing") instead of being handed "what the data settle is narrow."

**(d) A hard-truth close** *(the Eliot / unhistoric-lives page)*
- **Before:** "The sentence has been read as consolation and as quiet protest at a society that
  wasted her; the Finale holds both readings open, and this book does not close them."
- **After:** "You can read it two ways, and people do. As comfort: the quiet, unremembered life
  still counted. Or as a quiet complaint: look what the world wasted. Eliot leaves both doors open,
  and so will we — partly because it would be presumptuous to shut them, and partly because most of
  us need both readings, on different days."
- *Why:* the "both readings, held open" honesty is preserved; the Mitchell concession ("it would be
  presumptuous") and the Milne turn ("most of us need both, on different days") make it land warm
  instead of arch.

**(e) A chapter opening** *(the stage opener — already close; nudged plainer)*
- **Before:** "By now the choosing has mostly been done. A trade, a place, perhaps a person; debts,
  dependants, a name that others rely on. The question of these years is no longer *which life?* but
  *how to carry this one.*"
- **After:** "By now most of the big choices are behind you. A job, a place, maybe a person; debts,
  people who depend on you, a name others lean on. The question stops being *which life?* and quietly
  becomes *how do I carry this one?*" *(Keep it this short. Resist the urge to add a fourth clause.)*
- *Why:* barely changed, because it was nearly there — but "the choosing has mostly been done" →
  "most of the big choices are behind you" is the whole move in miniature: same meaning, no toll
  booth.

## 9. The process — how to write and revise against this spec

Adapted from the four-pass pipeline the voice came with. Scale it to the artifact: a whole chapter
gets all four passes; a single reworded assertion gets a quick DRAFT → one combined review.

1. **BRIEF** (one paragraph, before writing): what am I writing, for what moment on the map (§4),
   which lean, what claim must it carry, and — critically — **what mandatory caveats / prohibited
   phrasings must survive** (from the assertion, if there is one). Name the honesty guardrails up
   front so charm never quietly deletes one.
2. **DRAFT** straight through against the blend (§2), the moment (§4), and the cards (§3). Give the
   close to the house voice. Don't hedge, don't explain the feeling — say the plain human version of
   the thing. Don't revise mid-draft.
3. **REVIEW — three hats:**
   - **Voice audit:** find every line that's trying to sound smart (§7 list); check each lean against
     its moment and its card; flag decorative capitals; **audit the last paragraph as hard as the
     first** (voice drifts to generic at the end). Flag anything that reads like the other project it
     came from (romance, sensory, game).
   - **Honesty lint** (this replaces the old continuity/scent lint and is the one that matters most
     here): does the rewrite still carry the exact claim? every mandatory caveat present? no claim
     type blurred? no "tendencies" quietly upgraded to "destinies"? no quote touched? If a caveat
     moved or softened, it fails.
   - **Devil's advocate:** where is this still arch, still showing off, still skimmable, still trying
     too hard? "Reads fine" is a failure response.
4. **POLISH & COMMIT:** apply or defend every flag; cut what earns nothing; read aloud for stumbles;
   if an assertion's text changed, **update its `mandatory_caveats`/`prohibited_phrasings` and re-run
   `tools/approve_assertions.py`**, then rebuild and view the page. Commit with a one-line note.

### Harness prompts (run each in isolation)

> **Drafter.** You are writing/​revising commentary for *A Map for Mortals*. You have the brief, this
> spec (voice §2–3, moment map §4, immutables §5, checklist §7), and — if rewording an assertion —
> its exact claim and its mandatory caveats. Write the plain, warm, human version of the thing.
> Blend Mitchell (fair dry reasoning), Milne (warmth and dignity granted downward), and Slimes 300
> (plainness and permission), evenly, leaning per the moment. Never touch a quote. Never drop or
> soften a caveat. When two phrasings tie, take the shorter plainer one. Give the close to the house
> voice, landing on something concrete.

> **Voice auditor.** You have the draft and this spec. Flag every line that is trying to sound
> intelligent instead of being clear (§7 tells), every lean that broke its card's "never", every
> decorative capital, and any line that reads like generic essay-mill prose. Audit the final
> paragraph hardest. Do NOT flag plainness or warmth as a lapse — they are the target. Return line
> refs and rewrites; "looks good" is a failure response.

> **Honesty auditor.** You have the draft, the source claim, and its mandatory caveats/prohibited
> phrasings. Confirm the rewrite carries the same claim, every caveat is present, no claim type is
> blurred, no tendency became a destiny, and no quotation was altered. Flag every drift with a line
> ref. This audit outranks the voice audit on any conflict.

## 10. Quality bar

A piece passes if it has **all of:** a plain human version of the idea a stranger could read without
effort; the house blend audible, leaning per moment, closing in the core voice; every honesty
guardrail intact (§5); a close that lands on something concrete without overspeaking; and nothing a
devil's advocate can call "trying too hard" that you can't defend.

It fails on **any of:** a sentence written to sound clever; academic scaffolding or an LLM tell from
§7; a caveat softened or dropped for flow; a quote touched; a whole page in one lean; a joke that
sneers; or warmth curdled into tweeness.
