## I. So I Died of Overwork, Got Hit by Two Trucks, and Honestly Things Are Looking Up

The first thing to say about the day I died is that I was ahead on my deliverables.

I want that on the record. Whatever else gets said — and the goddess who handled my intake said a great deal, most of it while stamping things — nobody can say I left a mess. The Q3 reconciliation was done. The deck for Thursday's alignment meeting — a meeting held, as far as I could ever establish, to align on the agenda for Friday's acquisitions process review meeting — was finished, polished, and saved in four places, the way you bury acorns: so that whatever happened, somebody somewhere would have an acorn.

I had been at my desk eleven hours, which was a good day. I had slept four, which was a normal night. I had eaten half a convenience-store egg sandwich, and this is the detail that comes back to me at odd hours, the way the truly important things do: I was saving the other half as a reward. For after. That is where I was, as a human being, at thirty-one — the egg sandwich was the bright spot on the horizon, and I had rationed it.

At some point past nine the office discovered it needed a birthday card for a director I had never met, and I was the one who could no longer say no in a way anyone could detect, so I put on my coat. The night outside was warm and wet and smelled of rain on concrete, and the street in front of the convention centre was full of people who were happy in public, which I observed the way you'd watch a documentary about a country you will never visit.

The convention was for an anime I had loved at fifteen and had not had time to watch at thirty-one. Above the hall, two storeys tall, the franchise had put up a billboard of its most famous joke: the truck. *The* truck. The one that hits the office worker so the story can finally start — rendered mid-charge in cheerful colours, grille gleaming, barrelling forever at a little painted salaryman who looked, and I say this without self-pity because it was simply true, exactly like me.

And coming through the crowd was a stunning woman in full fantasy cosplay — one of the characters from the show, bikini armour, the lot — wielding an oversized greatsword. She was attempting to pass through a station turnstile, and the turnstile was winning, and she was taking it with the gravity of a woman who has decided that if she cannot have dignity she will at least have process: rotating, reversing, presenting her pass to the scanner with one gauntleted arm while the sword went everywhere a sword could go. On the fourth rotation the turnstile relented, and she raised both arms — and the enormous sword, which I could now see was foam — above her head in silent, total triumph.

I laughed. I had not laughed in a long time — eleven weeks, if you were counting, and I was the sort who counted — and it was such a large and surprising laugh that several things inside me, and one or two things above me, decided all at once that they had done quite enough.

Two things then happened in close succession, and the order matters to nobody but the paperwork.

My heart — which had spent the autumn sending increasingly formal letters of concern, all of which I had marked *read* — tendered its resignation, effective immediately.

And the guy-wires holding the billboard, which had been creaking through my laugh in a way I had registered as atmosphere, let go.

There is a window, between the laugh and the rest of it. A clean second. I look up at the cartoon truck descending on me at the speed of comedy, and I have time to think, with something close to wonder: *I am being run over by a meme.* Not even a real truck. The real truck arrived slightly afterwards, swerving around the fallen billboard, full of vending machines and ahead of schedule. If I had known it was ahead of schedule — and had I not been otherwise engaged — I would have been professionally impressed on the driver's behalf.

What I am saying is: I died laughing, of overwork, under a cartoon truck, marginally before an actual truck full of vending machines. The intake form, afterwards, had a box for *vehicular (comedic)* and a box for *cardiac (occupational)*, and the goddess on duty looked at me over it for a long time.

"You can't have both," she said.

"I appear to have had both."

She had the eyes of someone three centuries past the last interesting case, and for a moment I had the distinct sense of having improved her shift. She stamped the form *MISCELLANEOUS* — I have since been told it is the rarest stamp — and issued me, by way of settlement: a language I had no business being able to read; directions to a fishing village called Puteolae; and a small bronze hound with its nose lifted, which she described, already looking past me at the queue, as "for your particular education."

"The next world has a vacancy," she said, in the voice of someone closing a ticket. "Try not to fill it the way you filled the last one."

And then I woke up under a fig tree.

It was, by some margin, the nicest fig tree I had ever woken up under, though the bar was low; the previous record-holder was a desk. The light coming through the leaves was the colour of the daylight office workers only ever see through glass. Above me, a hand-painted sign in careful brushwork marked the edge of the grove:

> **SANCTUARY — PLEASE DO NOT FEED THE SLIMES UNLESS THEY ASK POLITELY.**

A blue, glossy, faintly sweet-smelling something was asking. Politely, as far as I could judge. Below the grove, the land fell away in terraces to a curve of pale houses around a harbour, and the sea beyond was doing something with the light that I had previously assumed was an exaggeration by painters. I had no money, no shirt, no job title, and no clear idea of what country — or, possibly, what century — I was in. Warm sun. Sea down the hill. A breeze with nowhere to be, and no reason I could see not to join it.

Ten out of ten. No notes.

I lay still and made a list of what I had, and the list was: some warm air, one fig tree, one polite slime, and nowhere in particular to be. It was a very good list.

Much later, the bronze hound would teach me that sweat comes in two kinds — the ordinary working kind, and the kind your body makes when it is afraid — and that somewhere under that fig tree mine had quietly changed from the second kind to the first. The body had decided before I did. It usually does.

I thought: this might be alright. And for once, nothing arrived to argue.

## II. In Which Puteolae Takes Some Getting Used To

Puteolae is a fishing village with a farm attached, a harbour attached, and a tower attached, in descending order of how much anyone wants to talk about them.

It sits on the south shore of its island like a cat in the sun: a curve of sandstone houses stepping down to the water, the old arches gone soft at the edges, nets drying between columns that once held up something more ambitious. There is one street, two if you count the harbour. The farm feeds the tavern; the tavern feeds the village; the village supplies contentment, and a little dried fish, to a world that has repeatedly failed to interest it.

The tower is the exception. It stands north of the village, a polite distance up the headland — older than the village, and taller than it looks, which I would eventually learn to take literally. The guild ledger is very dry on the subject of its contents: slimes, bats, imps, and the occasional succubus, ascending in cheerfulness, floor by floor, nobody knows how far. No one remembers which mage built it, or why. The village has had several centuries to develop a theory, and has instead developed fishing.

The adventurers' guild branch is one room by the harbour: a counter, a notice board, a bell, and a clerk who handles the island's entire complement of registered adventurers — both of them — with the unhurried competence of a woman herding two large cats. It is on the guild's doorstep, my first morning, that I am introduced to Masha — sun-bleached blonde hair, bronze glistening skin, an axe over one shoulder and the morning's bounty over the other — by being shoved out of her way. In fairness to her, and I have had cause since to be fair to her at length, I was standing where she intended to be, and she did check, afterwards, with one backward glance, that I had landed on something soft. The glance lasted half a second and would deny everything.

I am introduced to Lyra by being apologised to. The apology is so sincere, so structurally complete — context, remorse, restitution offered — that I check myself for injuries. I have none. As far as I can reconstruct it, she is apologising on behalf of the other woman.

The tavern is Marcella's — *The Many Grand Adventures*, according to the faded gilt sign over the door, though nobody has called it that in living memory, Marcella least of all. She is somewhere in her forties, built on the generous scale the old fertility mosaics consider correct — abundant, unhurried, certain — sweet as preserved figs to anyone under her roof and harder than the harbour stones beneath it. Everyone in Puteolae is polite to Marcella. This is partly the stew, and partly the bear. Nobody tells the bear story the same way twice, but every version agrees on the ending: a rampaging bear came down the farm road, in living memory of everyone present, and Marcella — one year retired from adventuring, and entertaining strong views about being interrupted mid-service — met it at the gate and put it down with a single blow to the throat. Instantly. The bear is a rug now. The rug is kept in the back room, because displaying it would be boasting. She still wears her old adventuring boots behind the bar — brown leather, resoled past counting, older than several of her regulars — on the grounds, never quite spoken, that a tavern is just a dungeon with better lighting. And above the bar, polished weekly and retired with honours, hangs a set of bikini armour from her campaigning days, which she will discuss with anyone, at length, except seriously.

The rest of the village's irregular residents can be met in a single morning. Madame Myrrh keeps the atelier by the dock — a full-figured succubus who introduced herself with a wink I needed a measurable moment to recover from — and is, by some distance, the most polite person I have ever met. Ferox, the blacksmith at the top of the street, is a minotaur of advanced years and undimmed enthusiasms who makes the village's hooks, hinges, and harpoons, and greets every well-built female adventurer who enters his forge with the frank, delighted appraisal of a connoisseur being shown a fine bridge. It is entirely a matter of load-bearing structure, he insists. Nobody believes him. Nobody minds, either; he is, in the village's settled judgement, all bark and no bull. And the dockside café is Gellia's — a slime-girl the colour of blue sea glass, who flirts the way the tide comes in: reliably, twice a day, with everyone, regardless of standing, species, or visible panic. It means nothing, and everyone knows it means nothing, which is exactly what makes it the best part of several people's mornings. The harbourmaster, eighty if he is a day, has proposed to her nine times. She has accepted all nine. Neither party has ever followed up.

On my third morning I am introduced to a vending machine with a face. He blinks awake, evaluates my net worth in a single glance I feel in my spine, dispenses a complimentary can of something blue, and informs me that I have the look of a young man in need of romance, financial planning, and a clean shirt — and that he, Vending-chan, can help with two out of three.

I ask which two.

"Wrong question," says Vending-chan, already printing something.

Lodging was the village's one administrative difficulty. Puteolae has no inn — visitors being, historically, something that happens to other villages — and the only spare room on the island belongs to the villa at the end of the beach road: white sandstone, a vine-shaded terrace, a view that makes the rent dishonest at any price, and the island's two registered adventurers already living in it. The guild clerk walked me up there herself. Possibly to watch.

There was a vote on whether I could have the room. I was not present for it, but I have reconstructed the result: one in favour, on the condition that I did the laundry; one abstention, recorded as an apology; and the rent set at a figure precise enough to make the whole arrangement look like business, which I understand was important to everyone.

I moved in on a Tuesday. Masha did not look up. Lyra apologised — for the stairs, I think. The room is small and white, and the window holds the whole bay in it like a postcard, and on the first night, lying there listening to the sea and to somebody two rooms over sharpening an axe with great feeling, I slept better than I had since university.

And that — the room, the rent, the axe being sharpened two doors down like a lullaby — is, more or less, how the problem started.

## III. By Floor Eighteen, Nobody in This Party Qualifies as Clean

By the fifteenth floor, the tower has stopped teaching you about the tower. What it teaches instead, floor by floor, is the party.

You learn to walk with two women who smell of effort.

You learn that Masha, when she has been fighting, smells of hot salt and sun-warmed leather, and beneath that of something slower and darker that is not the leather — the low animal warmth of a body built for exactly this, doing it all day, on your behalf. It is not a polite smell. It does not ask to be liked. It is the smell of strength being spent, and I have begun looking forward to it at the end of a long day the way you look forward to a fire being lit. Lower down, where the boots have been sealed since dawn, the register changes again: when they come off at camp, something toasted and round and improbably moreish rises off her. Corn chips, almost. I don't feel comfortable enough with her to mention it. I think it very loudly.

You learn that Lyra is a slower study, because Lyra is barely there to study. For most of a day she smells of soap and clean wool and whatever the last room smelled of — she takes on places, the way quiet people do. It takes a punishing stair-flight, or a fright, or one of her own spells going wide to raise anything that is hers at all, and what rises is quiet too: warm skin, a faint clean salt, the gentle sour-warm note you find at the nape of a neck, occasionally a warm-amine edge. There and gone, like something half-said. Her boots are the exception, and the joke: at day's end they come off with a quick vinegar bite, sharp and brief and entirely at odds with the rest of her, and she dies a little every time, and Masha has never once let it pass unremarked, and I have begun — quietly, treacherously — to find it endearing, to the point I often laugh out loud at the gentle bullying, which is its own problem. And beneath everything, only on the evenings she has enjoyed herself, there is something else: the kind of metal that has been in someone's mouth. Faint. Conditional. Gone if you turn toward it. You have decided, without quite deciding, not to ask about that one yet.

You learn that the soda you carry is, in a literal sense, what they run on — that canteen arithmetic is real arithmetic, and that there is a moment on every long run where Masha shakes the last can, listens to it, and looks at you, and you understand that you have become, among your other duties, the minister for wells and cisterns in a country of two rivers.

You learn that the eighteenth floor smells faintly of incense and old stone, and that the things living there are afraid of clean people — which is funny, because by floor eighteen, nobody in this party qualifies.

## IV. The Book in the Pack Is Stupid, and That's the Point

It is late, and the fire in the inn's third-best room is doing the slow honest work that fires do.

Camp has its liturgy by now. Lyra is asleep sitting up, mid-annotation, quill still in her hand, and will deny having slept at all. I have inventoried the sodas twice; the second count is for pleasure. And Masha is scrubbing the gauntlet she bullied off a tower-knight two floors down — she does this with the same attention she gives everything physical, which is to say slightly too much of it, more than the object deserves, the way other people pray.

I have learned not to interrupt. I learned it the way I learn most things about Masha: by doing the wrong thing once, at speed.

After a while she says, without looking up: *"You ever read Wandering Tales of the Sky-Princess?"*

I have not.

"Mm," she says. The fire pops.

We do not speak for some time. I assume the conversation is over, in the way conversations with Masha are frequently over before any information has been exchanged. Then she reaches into her pack — the pack I have seen her threaten three men with; the pack I privately believe has a floor I have not been cleared for — and produces a small, very battered paperback. Soft at the corners. Spine gone white with rereading. The cover shows a girl in a flowing dress holding a sword as if it were a bouquet.

"It's stupid," she says.

I tell her it is not stupid.

"It is, though," she says, and she looks at me — fully, briefly, frighteningly — and then back at the gauntlet, "and that's the point."

And I understand, as clearly as I have understood anything since the fig tree, that I have just been handed something else with a floor I haven't been cleared for.

I read the first chapter aloud. She lets me. It is, for the record, magnificent garbage — the Sky-Princess rescues a knight she has visibly out-rescued three chapters running, and apologises to him for it — and I do the voices, and Masha does not tell me to stop doing the voices.

The fire pops. Somewhere above us, on the third floor of the inn, a slime is humming.

## V. In Which Vending-chan Has Charts

Vending-chan is a vending machine with opinions — an unusual thing for a vending machine to be — and he keeps his opinions, along with everything else, in charts.

He shares them unrequested, because Vending-chan regards consent as a formality between friends, over breakfast at Marcella's, while dispensing a melon soda I did not order and declining, with enormous magnanimity, to charge me for it.

"It's the witch," he says. "Mathematically. Statistically. You laugh more when she's in the party — forty per cent more, adjusted for quality. I have *charts*."

"I didn't ask for charts," I say.

"Nobody asks for charts," he says, deeply hurt. "And yet the charts exist. Data doesn't need your permission, Izumi."

I make the mistake of asking to see one chart. He produces — from where, I decline to investigate — a scroll. Figure 1 is titled *Laughter Incidence by Party Composition, Annotated*. There are error bars. There is a footnote disqualifying one of my laughs as *"polite; excluded from sample"* — which is, I realise with a chill, where the adjustment for quality comes in.

Masha, who has just walked in with the morning's firewood under one arm like a baguette, looks at the scroll, and then at me.

"What charts," she says.

"No charts," I say.

"There are," says Vending-chan, "a great many charts."

"Am I in them."

And Vending-chan, who will not lie even to be kind, draws himself up to his full and humming height. "Madam," he says, with enormous dignity, "you are a chart all of your own."

I am spared having to explain that, because the grease-trap chooses this moment to catch fire, and everybody just stops to watch Marcella put it out — personally, unhurriedly, one hand wielding a wet cloth and the other never once leaving her hip. In this village, that is the real entertainment. Nobody talks through it. Not even Vending-chan.

I make a mental note to visit him in private, to establish what else he has compiled and visualised. He has an archive room. I have seen it. It is a lot.

## VI. The Terrifying Noble Lady Summoned Me, and I Have a Confession

A great house publishes its Mistress before you reach her. The gravel is raked to a discipline; the hedges hold their ranks; the fountain confines itself to precisely the volume permitted in such a place. By the time I reach the study I have been instructed, by architecture alone, in the exact order of precedence between myself and the universe — which is the intention, and why the door, opening for me with no hand upon it, lands the way it does.

She does not ask me to sit, and I understand, after a moment, that this is a test — the chair being a sort of trap with a cushion on it. So I stand; and she says nothing about my standing, which from Victoria is a kind of applause.

Her study has the order of a well-run province. The ledgers are ruled in a hand you could set a watch by; the inkstand stands square to creation; and the single insubordinate object in the room — a teacup, abandoned at a reckless angle — survives only because it has not yet been noticed, and will, when noticed, be dealt with as a domestic matter of state.

"You're the truck one," she says. She says *truck* the way a physician names a mild but embarrassing disease. "Vending-chan keeps a file on you. It is not flattering. I have read it twice — the second time aloud, to the household."

There is no good answer to that, and she does not want one. She wants exactly what is currently happening to my face, and she takes her time over it, the way you would take your time over a good vintage. The heat reaches my ears. "Good," she says, turning a page. "You are capable of shame. The file left the question open."

She asks her questions, and they are good questions — the kind with a second question folded inside, so that answering the surface one honestly is itself a small confession — and she conducts the whole interview while continuing to sign things in that watch-setting hand, to establish that I am not the day's main item. Somewhere in the second hour I sway on my feet, and without looking up she says, "Sit down. Not because you've earned the chair. Because I dislike watching you wobble." And then, while I am sitting down and entirely too pleased about it: "That was not kindness. Do not file it as kindness."

She has been at the accounts since dawn, and her boots have been on as long as the ledgers. When she finally eases one off — not for my benefit; she has simply concluded the audience is informal now, a decision on which I was not consulted — the room acquires an opinion.

Honesty is the only tribute she accepts, and so it must be paid. It is a deep, frank, astonishing smell: warm worn leather first, and then beneath it the day itself — close, dense, ripened, the smell of a stockinged foot shut in good leather since dawn through fourteen hours of being right about everything — and at its far edge a sharpness for which the only honest word is *cheese*. Not a suggestion of it. The genuine article, rich and over the line and entirely hers. It crosses the room the way she does: without apology, expecting to be accommodated — and somehow it is the room that ends up embarrassed, and not her.

And the confession is: I didn't mind.

She watches me not mind. She is very good at watching.

"Is something wrong?" she asks, observing my composure with a connoisseur's interest. It is not a question. It is a dare with the manners of a question.

"It becomes you," I say.

There follows a silence in which I age perceptibly.

The spark reaches the corner of her mouth before she can stamp it out, and is, briefly, a smile. "Good boy," she says — two words, set down like a seal on something binding — and for the space of a breath she wears the smile openly: a cruel little curve, entirely pleased with itself, thrilled with what it has just done to me and already considering what it might do next. Then the great house resumes its tenancy of her face, and the next ledger is opened.

I walk home embroiled past extrication, composing amendments to Vending-chan's file, and posting none of them.

## VII. The Bronze Hound Explains Things Nobody Asked It To

The bronze hound was the third thing the goddess issued me, and for my first weeks in Puteolae I assumed "for your particular education" was a filing formality. Then Masha came back from a double tower day, dropped onto the bench across from me, started unlacing her boots — and the hound, cold metal, nose lifted, sitting on the shelf where I had left it, *leaned*.

Vending-chan calls it the Assessor. Everyone else calls it nosy.

Press a hand to it and it tells you things. It shows you a smell — hanging in the air, visible, the way steam stands over a bath. It lets you *taste* a smell, which I did not ask for, which nobody asks for, and which I have now done eleven times. And then it explains, which is the part I keep coming back for, and the part I would least like to be observed coming back for.

On the evening in question I consult it mostly to make a point to Masha that I am not thinking about how she smells.

The hound thinks about how she smells. At length.

> **SUBJECT: MASHA.** Dominant flora: *Proteus*, *Pseudomonas*. Substrate: refined sugars (see: four melon sodas, today; do not pretend otherwise). Conditions: enclosed, warm, fourteen hours in leather boots. Resulting profile: *toasted, yeasty, warm corn over worn leather.* Projected taste: salt first, then warm grain. Intensity: considerable, and cresting. Recommendation: she will not thank you for suggesting the onsen. She would thank you for the company.

"What's it saying," says Masha, who cannot read it.

"Statistics," I say.

"Is it being rude about me."

"It's being *thorough* about you."

She considers this. "Same thing," she decides — and then, because Masha's sense of justice is the most reliable mechanism in the village, she points at the hound and then at me. "Your turn. Hand on the dog."

I object, on several grounds. She waits out all of them with her chin on her fist.

> **SUBJECT: IZUMI.** Sweat mode: stress-dominant (subject is currently being watched, and knows it). Substrate: two melon sodas; one egg sandwich (commemorative). Profile: *nervous salt, warm cotton, the faint sour tang of the recently terrified.* Intensity: modest, rising under observation. Recommendation: the onsen. Also honesty. In either order.

She reads my face the way I could not read the plaque. "It got you," she says, delighted. "What did it say?"

"Statistics," I say.

"Mm," she says, and finishes the rest of my soda, by way of peer review.

## VIII. I Went Back for My Coin Purse and Interrupted Something

The coin purse is on the third floor, because that is where Masha took the stairs at her usual pace — the pace of a woman repossessing the building — and I bounced off a wall and, apparently, shed valuables. We discover this at the fifth-floor camp. I volunteer to go back down alone, three floors being within even my abilities, and Lyra offers to come, and I say no, and this is the only reason any of what follows happens: I am quiet on stairs, and she was not expecting anyone.

I hear it before I see it. A fire spell — but small, the size of a coin, where Lyra's usual work takes the wall temperature up a season. Then another. Then a pause, and another. Patient as needlework.

I come around the pillar with her name half out of my mouth, and stop.

The third floor keeps its monsters small: a slime, a meadow bunny, a young pot-monster with its lid on crooked. All three are in the fight, in the sense that the fight is happening to them. None of them has been finished. The spells arriving — steady, spaced, exact — are sized never to finish anything; and Lyra is standing over the whole arrangement with her sleeves rolled and an expression I know, because I watched it once across a bandage: great fondness, and complete attention.

The bunny attempts the door of the room, on the whole reasonably. A coin of fire lands in front of it, and it changes its mind.

"Lyra."

I will say this for her reflexes: the three lights that follow are instant, clean, and merciful, and it takes her perhaps a second and a half to end what she had been an hour not-ending. Pop, pop, pop. The pot lid settles. Then she turns around with her hands folded and her face already arranged, and the arrangement is *wholesome*, and it is very nearly fast enough.

"Izumi! I was — practising. Control work. Small castings." A breath. "There are herbs on this floor."

I look at the scorch marks, which are numerous, patient, and arranged in a wide, careful circle, like a garden bed.

"For the herbs," I say.

"Precision is important," says Lyra, with the piety of a woman quoting scripture she has just written. She is already moving past me toward the stairs, and she is apologising as she goes — to the ash, retroactively, at length, in the structurally complete style: context, remorse, restitution offered. It is a beautiful apology.

She is not, I notice, sorry.

"Did I frighten you?" she asks, at the landing — and there is a grin under the asking, a wicked little one, half disguised, gone before it finishes arriving. Then she hears herself, and the blush comes up like a tide, and she apologises for the question, to me, and then — I want this on the record — *to the stairwell*.

We climb. She hums on the way, two notes, down and up, one of the little island hymns. At the second landing her boots give their quick vinegar bite as she passes, and she winces and apologises for that too — and the apology comes out easier than the last one, worn smooth, a groove long practised. She keeps a whole shelf of shames, I am beginning to learn, and the boots are the one she lets people see.

At the fifth-floor camp she reports, accurately, that I found my purse, and that the third floor was quiet.

That evening, at the fire, under the salve tin and the woodsmoke, faint as a coin held in a warm fist: metal. There and gone. I add it to the count I am pretending not to keep.

The tower restocks itself; everything in it comes back in a week, slimes and bunnies and crooked little pots, none the wiser. I have checked this twice in the guild ledger, which is not something a man does about nothing. I have decided, without quite deciding, to go on not asking. But I have moved the question, quietly, nearer the front of the queue.

## IX. In Which the Laundry Is Biographical

The arrangement — voted on, as I say, in my absence — is that I do the washing.

Puddle takes the linens. He is a laundry slime; it is his vocation and his art; the villa's sheets come back smelling of rainwater and quiet pride, and in festival weeks he does the napkins *ambitiously*. But boots, wraps, and the war-worn things are, by standing agreement, mine. Puddle calls them "too biographical."

He is not wrong. Masha's boots sit at the top of the basket with the whole week still in them: leather gone warm and animal from her, and rising up through it that low toasted note, round and rich and specific and hers — a biography in one volume, unauthorised. You could date each day of the week in it like rings in a tree, if you were the sort of person who lingered over someone's boots, which is not a sentence I am prepared to finish.

She is leaning in the doorway. She has been leaning there slightly too long for someone with no opinion about laundry, wearing the studied indifference of a woman who has read enough romance novels to know exactly what scene this is, and would die before admitting she has cast it.

She crosses the room, hooks a boot up off the basket by two fingers, and holds it out between us like a court exhibit.

"Day five," she says. "Guess."

"I'd rather—"

"*With feeling.*"

I decline the exhibit. The exhibit stays where it is, at a range the law would call intimate, while she watches me decline it — grinning now, entirely unhurried, a bully at her leisure — and what she is watching for, I understand, is not whether I mind. It is whether I don't.

"They reek," she teases, dropping the boot back on the pile. "You can say it. I'm not delicate."

> ▸ **Wash them properly.**  ▸ **Air them by the fire.**  ▸ **Leave them as they are.**

*— and if you leave them —*

She doesn't say anything that evening. She wears them out the next morning, also without saying anything, and there is a great deal of nothing being said; the kitchen is loud with it.

At breakfast she puts her bread down and addresses the table generally, in the tone of someone raising a procedural matter. "You didn't clean my boots."

"I know."

A pause with weather in it.

"No," she says, to the bread, in the voice she uses when a card she wanted turns up loose in a market bin. "I suppose you do."

## X. The Island Breathes Steam at Dusk, and So Do We

The village onsen sits at the warm end of the bay, fed by the same volcanic plumbing that makes the whole archipelago feel faintly alive — the island breathes steam at dusk, and the bath is where it exhales. A wooden partition runs down the middle, of a height the original builders must have debated at length and resolved, in the end, in favour of comedy.

We go after the long runs. It is liturgy by now. Marcella holds dinner. Vending-chan stations himself in the changing room and dispenses cold cans to outstretched hands with the solemnity of a priest administering something. Puddle takes the towels and returns them warm — *how* remains his secret — and visibly proud.

From the far side of the partition, the day's debrief drifts over with the steam. Masha narrates the fights in full, with corrections, to an audience that was present. Lyra's contributions are softer, fewer, and land later — after each one there is a pause, exactly the length of Masha working out whether she has just been complimented or filleted. Twice, a wooden bucket is deployed as a gavel.

I have the men's side to myself, barring Ferox, who comes for the minerals and conducts himself, against every expectation his reputation provides, like a national park. The water is volcanic and opinionated. Lantern light stands in the steam in slabs, and now and then a bare arm rises above the partition line to illustrate a point — Masha's, braceleted in old scars, demonstrating an axe angle to an audience protesting, audibly, that it does not require demonstration.

Afterwards, in the courtyard, the party reassembles scrubbed and steaming in yukata, hair turbaned in towels, radiating heat like small furnaces. There is a specific glow a body has, coming off a hard week and a hot soak into the evening air — clean skin still flushed with the bath, sleeves loose, collarbones frank — and it would be dishonest of the narration to pretend not to notice, so the narration notices, once, properly, and then minds its manners.

Masha comes out last, visibly betrayed. She keeps lifting her wrist to her nose and finding a stranger there.

"Soap," she says, with the disgust of a woman robbed in her own house. "I smell of soap. Nothing on me is *mine*."

The honest reply — that the bath has carried off something I'd got attached to, and that I'll be glad, quietly and a little shamefully, when the week hands it back — is not one I've earned the right to say out loud. So I give her the cold can Vending-chan has been holding ready, and I don't.

She drinks. The steam climbs off all of us into the dark.

"It comes back," she tells the can — half a threat, half a comfort. "Give it a week."

"I know," I say.

Something in how I say it makes her glance at me a half-second too long, and decide — wrongly, and to my lasting relief — that I'm only agreeing about the laundry.

## XI. Ferox Does Not Deal in Ceremony, Only in Deliveries

The new cuirass is ready on a Thursday, because Ferox does not deal in ceremony, only in deliveries.

It is lacquered bronze scale over boiled leather, storm-blue, resized twice — each adjustment loudly itemised on the invoice — for Masha. Which is to say: sized for a woman the old mosaics would recognise. Built broad and deep and certain, hips like a verdict, the kind of figure the fertility shrines carve when they want the harvest to take the hint. Armour like this is not modest and is not trying to be; it is class identity worn aloud — *I am the landslide; form a queue* — and on her it reads less like display than like jurisdiction.

She tries it on in the middle of the forge, over her underlayer, because changing rooms are for people with patience. The scale settles across her shoulders with a sound like polite applause. The skirt of tassets, Ferox announces, is tactical.

"Good shoulders," he adds, circling her once at a professional radius, entirely about the armour. "Wasted on a sword. Those are axe shoulders." And then he goes back to the invoice, because admiration is free and adjustments are not.

There is a polished shield that serves as the shop mirror. Masha plants herself in front of it, jaw set, the way she squares up to mid-bosses. She turns once left, once right. She is being extremely casual about it, and the casualness has the structural integrity of wet paper: I have read three Sky-Princess books by now, and I know exactly which cover she is standing like.

"It's fine," she rules, meaning several other words.

Lyra, meanwhile, has drifted to the rack of oddments Ferox takes in part-exchange and found a dusty-teal robe with sleeves to her fingertips — cut for somebody else entirely and suiting her completely, the way her things always do. Where Masha wears armour like a declaration, Lyra wears cloth like a rumour. She checks the seams, discovers an inner pocket, approves of the inner pocket with a small private nod, and only then, as an afterthought, glances at the shield — quickly, sideways, the way you'd glance at a stranger you suspect of knowing something about you.

"The teal suits you," I say, because it does.

She apologises to the rack.

Ferox totals the purchases and recites the cuirass's care instruction in the tone of a man reading ahead in a manual he wrote: the lacquer is to be wiped down after harbour weather, and the lining aired weekly — "or the leather will remember you." He says it the way veterans say things. Fondly, and from experience.

Masha looks at me. I look at the ceiling, which has never been more interesting.

"It can remember me," she decides, counting out the gold. "Everything else does."

## XII. The Clouds Give Up the Island All at Once

The clouds had been hoarding the island for a month, and gave it up the way the gods' administration gives up anything: all at once, without apology, implying it had been available the whole time and we had simply failed to file the right form.

From the rail it is terraces first — green stitched up the volcano's shoulder in rows some great-grandmother laid out and nobody since has dared improve — then the harbour: black sand and old stone, the work below the waterline older than the village and island carpentry above it, which is the local way of saying that empires come and go but the pier must hold. The bathhouses steam against the morning. The forum smells of grilled fish, cut cedar, and the particular sweetness vending machines give off in the heat.

A procession is coming down the shrine steps as we tie up: grain-priests with paper lanterns, a censer swinging, the Keeper's litany sung two notes wide — the same two notes, I notice, that Lyra hums when she thinks nobody is listening. By the customs house there is an old mosaic of a fish that nobody can name the god of. Offerings pile up in front of it anyway, on the principle that it is cheaper to be polite. The customs officer stamps our landing chit without reading it and blesses us in two faiths, one of which he could probably name.

Masha spits over the rail, for luck. Lyra apologises to the sea, for the spit.

I write *arrived* in the ledger Vending-chan makes me keep, and then, after some thought, *glad of it.*
